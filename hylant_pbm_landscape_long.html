The PBM Landscape: Where We Are and What's Coming Over the Next 12 Months

Pharmacy is the line item that surprises employers most, and for many plans it is now growing faster than medical. The reasons sit largely inside contracts that most plan sponsors have never read closely, which is exactly where a sharp advisor creates distinction. Here is the landscape we are seeing across the PBM contracts we review at Prescription Benefit Solutions, and where it is heading over the next twelve months, shared for the work your teams do every day.

Where the market is today

Three structural realities shape almost every PBM relationship right now.

Consolidation has blurred the line between negotiator and vendor. The largest PBMs are now part of organizations that also own the mail-order pharmacy, the specialty pharmacy, and the rebate aggregator that sits between the manufacturer and the plan. When the same parent company sets the price, fills the script, and negotiates the rebate, much of what is presented as a market negotiation is internal money movement. That is not a scandal, it is the business model. But it changes what "competitive" means and what an advisor should be asking for on a client's behalf.

Rebates still drive formulary design. The drug in the preferred position is frequently the one carrying the larger manufacturer rebate, not the one with the lowest net cost to the plan. That gap between gross rebate savings and true net cost is where real dollars are won or lost, and where a sharp advisor earns their seat.

Transparency pressure is mounting from several directions at once: federal scrutiny, a growing patchwork of state PBM laws, and ERISA fiduciary litigation that has put plan sponsors on notice that "we trusted our PBM" is not a defense. Asking for transparency is no longer adversarial. Clients increasingly expect their advisors to.

What's on the horizon over the next 12 months

Four developments deserve a place in your client conversations now.

GLP-1 medications will keep reshaping budgets. Demand is not slowing, the clinical pipeline is expanding beyond diabetes and weight management, and coverage strategy has become a leadership decision about plan sustainability rather than a simple yes or no on the formulary.

Biosimilars are finally available at scale across major drug categories, but availability and adoption are not the same thing. Rebate arrangements frequently keep plans anchored to the higher-priced originator biologic. The savings are real, and capturing them depends on contract language and formulary governance, not on the PBM volunteering them.

High-cost cell and gene therapies are moving from occasional to expected. Several carry seven-figure price tags, and many plan documents, stop-loss arrangements, and PBM contracts were not written with them in mind. The next year is the time to align those three before a single claim forces the issue.

Transparent, pass-through and cost-plus PBM models continue to gain credibility and market share. For the first time in years, plan sponsors have a genuine alternative to the traditional spread-and-rebate model, which strengthens a plan sponsor's negotiating position even for the clients who ultimately choose to stay where they are.

What this means for the advisors who see it coming

None of this calls for alarm. It calls for preparation, and preparation is where advisors differentiate. The employers who navigate the next year well will be the ones whose advisor helped them do a few specific things before renewal: read the actual contract rather than the summary, benchmark current pricing against the market, confirm that audit and transparency rights are usable rather than theoretical, and document a prudent process for selecting and monitoring the PBM.

That is the kind of work that turns a renewal into a retention conversation. We review hundreds of PBM contracts a year for self-funded employers, and the pattern is consistent: the plans that prepare early negotiate measurably different outcomes than the plans that react at renewal. When a client feels their advisor saw the pharmacy issue coming, that is competitive distinction in its most durable form, and it is work we are glad to do alongside your team when a situation calls for a pharmacy specialist.

The pharmacy benefit is not getting simpler. But it is becoming far more navigable for the advisors and plan sponsors who treat the contract as the leverage point it is.

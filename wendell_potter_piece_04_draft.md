## Why this reframe matters

Ginny advocates for manufacturer copay cards as a legitimate patient affordability tool. PBS clients also use accumulator and maximizer programs as a legitimate cost-containment lever in some scenarios. A simple "ban accumulator programs" reform piece sits in tension with both positions.

The reframe locks the piece around the **structural conflict of interest** rather than program prohibition. It preserves:

- **Pro-copay-card.** Manufacturer copay cards remain a legitimate patient affordability tool.
- **Pro-plan-sponsor-judgment.** Plan sponsors retain the right to choose accumulator or maximizer programs as a cost lever.
- **Anti-PBM-self-dealing.** The villain is the structural conflict where the same PBM controls formulary placement, specialty pharmacy routing, AND accumulator crediting math.

It also differentiates Ginny from the typical patient-advocate framing (ban it) and the typical plan-sponsor framing (preserve it). She names the structural problem in the middle that both positions miss.

## Draft

### The Conflict of Interest at the Heart of Copay Accumulator Programs

Imagine a charitable foundation that gives scholarships to college students to help them afford tuition. Now imagine the foundation hires an administrator to process those scholarships. Now imagine the administrator also owns the college, decides which courses qualify for the scholarship, and decides whether the scholarship counts toward the student's tuition bill or simply toward the college's revenue. Same dollars. Different beneficiary depending on how the administrator counts. One entity controlling every decision in the transaction.

That is the structural problem at the heart of copay accumulator and maximizer programs in employer health plans. The reform conversation has focused on whether the programs should exist. The harder question is who is allowed to run them.

A copay accumulator program redirects manufacturer-provided copay assistance away from the patient's deductible and toward the plan's expense column. The patient pays the coupon at the pharmacy; the coupon dollars cover the claim; the dollars never count toward the deductible the patient still owes for the year. A copay maximizer is the same mechanic with different math: the program calibrates the coupon amount to maximize the value extracted across a year of fills.

For patients on specialty drugs, these programs can create affordability cliffs. Members who relied on coupons to manage their out-of-pocket exposure can find themselves owing four-figure balances they did not expect.

For plan sponsors, the programs can be a legitimate cost-containment lever when designed and disclosed correctly. Manufacturer dollars that would otherwise flow to a specialty patient instead reduce the plan's specialty spend.

For the PBM administering the program, the economics are a third matter entirely.

In the contracts I review, the accumulator program is almost always administered by the same PBM that owns the specialty pharmacy dispensing the drug and writes the formulary that defines the drug as accumulator-eligible. Three roles. One vendor. SaveOnSP is an Express Scripts subsidiary. PrudentRx is owned by CVS Caremark. Variable Copay is an OptumRx variant.

That is the structural problem. Not the program itself. The program design.

When the same entity controls which drugs are eligible for accumulator or maximizer treatment, which pharmacy is allowed to fill those drugs, and whether the manufacturer dollars credit the member's deductible or the plan's expense or, increasingly, neither, the conflict of interest is structural. The PBM can write formulary placement to favor drugs with the largest manufacturer assistance available. The PBM can route those fills to its own specialty pharmacy. The PBM can administer the crediting math in ways that maximize the program's revenue to the PBM rather than the savings to the plan.

Twenty-six states plus DC and Puerto Rico have enacted accumulator-related protections, most focused on requiring that copay assistance count toward member cost-sharing in fully insured plans. The state laws get the patient-protection question half right. They miss the structural problem entirely.

ERISA preempts most state law for self-insured plans, which is the majority of employer coverage. So state protections reach a minority of members, and the structural conflict of interest at the PBM administrator level operates unaffected in the larger market.

Most reform proposals on accumulator programs reach for prohibition: ban the program, force all manufacturer assistance to credit member deductibles, end of conversation.

In my view, that reform misses the legitimate use case. Plan sponsors should retain the right to choose accumulator or maximizer programs as a cost-containment lever, provided two structural conditions are met. First, the program must be disclosed to members at enrollment in plain language with the affordability consequences named. Second, the PBM administering the program must not also own the specialty pharmacy dispensing the drug or write the formulary defining program eligibility.

That is the reform that matches the actual problem. The problem is not that plans use these programs. The problem is that the entity profiting from the program also controls every other variable in the transaction.

Three named actions, in order of political achievability.

First, a federal disclosure rule: plans using accumulator or maximizer programs must disclose to members at enrollment, in plain language, that manufacturer assistance for a defined list of drugs will not count toward the member's annual deductible. HHS sub-regulatory guidance if available without statute; ERISA amendment if not.

Second, a structural firewall rule: PBMs administering accumulator or maximizer programs must not also own the specialty pharmacy dispensing the drugs eligible for the program. This is the conflict-of-interest reform. Federal statute required.

Third, a formulary-design firewall: a PBM cannot define a drug as accumulator-eligible solely on the basis of available manufacturer assistance. Eligibility must follow clinical criteria documented and auditable by the plan sponsor.

What I would argue is that this is the most under-named reform in the PBM economy. The reform conversation has been split between patient advocates who want accumulator programs banned and plan sponsors who want them preserved as a cost lever. Both positions miss the structural problem in the middle. Manufacturer copay cards are a legitimate patient affordability tool. Plan-sponsor accumulator programs can be a legitimate cost-containment lever. The same vendor should not be allowed to control both ends of the transaction without disclosure and without structural separation. The reform is the firewall. The reform is the disclosure. The reform is not the prohibition.

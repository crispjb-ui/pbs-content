# PART 1: SUBSTACK ARTICLE

## The GLP-1 Coverage Decision Just Got Harder

**Publication:** Benefit Blind Spots
**Publish:** Monday, February 9, 2026 at 7:30 AM EST
**Length:** ~1,600 words (~6 min read)
**Tone:** Analytical authority

---

### Article Body

The FDA approved oral semaglutide for weight management on December 22, 2025. By January, it was in US pharmacies. A second oral GLP-1 (orforglipron from Eli Lilly) could receive approval this year.

For self-funded employers who spent 2024 and 2025 debating whether to cover injectable GLP-1s, the decision matrix just expanded.

I want to walk through what changed, what the data actually shows, and a framework for approaching coverage decisions in a market that refuses to stabilize.

---

**Three Developments That Changed the Calculation**

*1. Oral GLP-1s Are No Longer Theoretical*

The FDA's approval of oral semaglutide (Wegovy pill, 25 mg daily) for chronic weight management followed the OASIS 4 trial: 307 adults over 64 weeks with mean weight loss of 13.6% using treatment policy estimand and 16.6% using trial product estimand.

Eli Lilly's orforglipron submitted its NDA in late 2025 with a Commissioner's National Priority Voucher. The ATTAIN-1 trial showed 12.4% weight loss at 72 weeks on the 36 mg dose.

The plan design question is no longer "injectable or nothing." Oral formulations introduce new considerations around adherence patterns, member preference, and formulary positioning.

*2. Medicare Coverage Creates a New Benchmark*

The TrumpRx agreements announced November 6, 2025 established negotiated pricing for Medicare and Medicaid: $245 per month for injectables with a $50 beneficiary copay. This represents the first time Medicare will cover GLP-1s for weight management, with implementation beginning mid-2026.

For employer-sponsored plans, this creates both a reference point and a potential problem. Members may compare their out-of-pocket costs to what Medicare beneficiaries pay. The negotiated prices apply to Medicare, Medicaid, and direct-to-consumer channels, not directly to employer plans.

TrumpRx direct-to-consumer pricing starts around $350 per month with projected decreases to $245 over two years. Oral Wegovy launches at $149 per month for starting doses.

*3. The Adherence Data Demands Attention*

Prime Therapeutics data shows 1 in 12 members remain on GLP-1 therapy after three years. Approximately two-thirds discontinue before the 12-week mark, the minimum duration typically needed for meaningful weight loss.

At annual gross costs of $12,000 to $17,000 per person (per Sequoia's 2025 employer survey), high discontinuation rates transform a clinical intervention into a pharmacy spend problem without corresponding health outcomes.

GLP-1s now represent 21% of pharmacy benefit spend according to industry surveys. Coverage rates among large employers range from 44% to 57% per industry surveys.

---

**The Oral Adherence Question**

Industry observers suggest oral formulations may improve adherence by removing injection barriers. I am not yet convinced the data supports that assumption.

Rybelsus (the existing oral semaglutide for diabetes) holds approximately 14% GLP-1 market share with roughly $2.7 billion in sales compared to Ozempic's approximately $14 billion. This suggests oral preference has limits, though the weight management indication may differ.

Oral GLP-1s require specific administration: taking the medication on an empty stomach, waiting 30 minutes before eating or drinking, and maintaining daily consistency. Whether members sustain these requirements over months and years remains unknown.

The adherence challenge with GLP-1s may have less to do with injection aversion and more to do with the realities of indefinite medication commitment, side effect management, and lifestyle integration.

---

**A Framework for Coverage Decisions**

In our experience reviewing these plans, managed access with clinical structure produces better outcomes than either blanket exclusion or unrestricted coverage.

*Phase 1: Define Clinical Criteria*

Coverage tied to documented medical necessity. Consider BMI thresholds (typically 30+ or 27+ with comorbidities), prior authorization requirements, prescriber limitations (endocrinology, obesity medicine, primary care with documented counseling).

Avoid criteria so restrictive that members who would benefit cannot access coverage. Avoid criteria so open that pharmacy spend escalates without clinical oversight.

*Phase 2: Build Support Infrastructure*

Coverage without clinical support produces the worst outcome: high pharmacy spend with high discontinuation.

The programs we see working include registered dietitian access, behavioral health resources for eating patterns, exercise physiology or physical therapy referrals, and regular check-ins tied to refill authorization.

The employers getting this right treat GLP-1s as one component of a weight management program. Not the entire program.

*Phase 3: Establish Measurement and Review*

Define what success looks like before launch. Track: member enrollment, 12-week retention, 52-week retention, weight outcomes where available, total pharmacy and medical spend for enrolled members, comorbidity changes.

Build in decision points. If 12-week retention falls below threshold, what changes? If costs exceed projections, what levers exist?

---

**Contract Language Considerations**

For employers covering GLP-1s, PBM contract language matters more than usual. This is something we flag in nearly every contract review we conduct right now.

Review rebate terms. GLP-1 rebates vary significantly by product and PBM arrangement. Make sure your contract captures competitive rebates on high-cost specialty medications.

Examine utilization management provisions. Prior authorization, step therapy, and quantity limits should align with your clinical criteria, not just the PBM's default protocols. Your broker or consultant can help you evaluate whether these match your plan's intent.

Confirm data access. You need claims data granular enough to track the metrics outlined above. Standard reporting may not suffice.

---

**What to Watch in the Next 90 Days**

- Orforglipron FDA decision timeline
- Employer response to Medicare coverage launch
- PBM formulary positioning of oral vs. injectable GLP-1s
- Real-world adherence data on oral semaglutide for weight management
- State legislative activity on GLP-1 coverage mandates

---

**The Position We Take**

GLP-1 coverage is not a yes-or-no question. It is a design question. That is the message I find myself repeating in nearly every employer conversation right now.

The employers managing this well approach it with clinical rigor, infrastructure investment, and contractual attention. The employers struggling treat it as a formulary decision disconnected from program design.

Oral GLP-1s do not change this fundamental dynamic. They add options. They may add complexity. They do not resolve the core challenge of building coverage that produces outcomes worth the investment.

## ▼ DRAFT BEGINS ▼

**Working title: Everyone Is Fighting About GLP-1 Prices. Almost Nobody Is Asking Where the Money Between the Prices Goes.**

Imagine a toll bridge where the posted toll is twelve dollars, almost nobody actually pays twelve dollars, and the operator will not say what anyone really pays or where the difference goes. Now imagine the whole country arguing about whether the posted toll is too high. That is the GLP-1 debate. The fight is about the sticker. The money is in the gap behind it.

The last piece was about ownership, the way one parent company can own the insurer, the Pharmacy Benefit Manager (PBM), and the pharmacy. This one is about what that machinery does when it meets the most budget-shaking drug class in modern memory. On the map this series started with, we are standing on two streams at once: rebate retention and manufacturer payments, because on GLP-1s they work as one system.

First, the plain facts of the class. GLP-1 drugs, the family that includes Ozempic, Wegovy, Mounjaro, and Zepbound, treat diabetes and obesity, and demand for them is unlike anything employers have budgeted for. For many self-funded plans, this one class is now the largest single line in the pharmacy budget. In the plans I work with, the questions arrive weekly: whether to cover the weight-management indication at all, what the prior authorization should require, what happens to the budget if uptake doubles again.

Here is what the public debate says about all this. One camp says the list prices are outrageous and points at the manufacturers. Another argues about whether Medicare should cover the drugs for weight loss, and when. The manufacturers answer that list prices are misleading because rebates give most of it back. Every one of those arguments is about the sticker or about who qualifies to pay it.

Now here is the part of the story the debate keeps skipping. On a heavily rebated brand drug, there are two prices. There is the list price, the number everyone fights about. And there is the net cost, what is actually left after the manufacturer pays rebates back through the chain. On GLP-1s the distance between those two numbers is unusually large, and everything in that distance passes through the PBM and its partners on the way to the plan. Some of it comes back to the employer as rebate checks. Some of it does not, because of the machinery this series has been walking through: rebates that are defined narrowly in the contract so that other manufacturer payments fall outside the definition, fees calculated as a percentage of a very high list price, and collecting entities in the middle of the chain that share owners with the PBMs themselves. The higher the list price and the bigger the rebate, the more that middle layer is worth. Simply put, the sticker everyone hates is also the raw material for the margin nobody can see.

If you want evidence that the sticker is not the real economy, the manufacturers have started providing it. Novo Nordisk has announced list-price reductions on its GLP-1 drugs of roughly half, taking effect in 2027. A list price that can fall by half overnight was never a hard cost. It was the opening position in a negotiation the public never sees, and the interesting question is what happens to the middle layer when the list price falls: whether plans actually pay less, or whether the machinery adjusts its definitions and keeps its share. Based on the contracts I read, that question will be answered in the definitions, not in the press releases.

So what is the reform? The prescription here is disclosure with teeth, aimed at the class where the stakes are highest. Congress should give the Secretary of Health and Human Services the authority to require, for any drug class above a set level of national spending, that PBMs disclose the actual net cost of each drug in the class, after every rebate, fee, and manufacturer payment, to the plans that pay for it. Not averages across a book of business. The plan's own numbers, for the plan's own claims. Medicaid programs could run the model first, since states are already wrestling with GLP-1 budgets, and the commercial market would follow the template.

My view is that GLP-1 is the test the whole system should be graded on. This is the class with the most money, the most attention, and the most political will attached to it in years. If transparency cannot be established here, where every incentive to demand it exists, it will not be established anywhere. And if it can, we will have built the template for the next expensive breakthrough, because there will always be a next one. The gene therapies arriving now make GLP-1 budgets look small.

There is a companion problem hiding inside GLP-1 coverage, at the pharmacy counter, where the copay card the manufacturer hands the member collides with a program the PBM runs quietly against it. The member thinks the card is helping them meet their deductible. Often it is doing something else entirely. That is the next piece.

### A State Audit Shows a Spread-Pricing Ban Is Not the Same as No Spread

*STAT | July 20, 2026*

An audit of Iowa's Medicaid records found that one large PBM appears to have made more than $100 million by adjusting what it paid pharmacies and never passing those savings back to the managed care plans. The auditors described accounting maneuvers that both obscured the flow of money and evaded a spread-pricing prohibition that is now law in Iowa and several other states.

This was Medicaid, but the mechanism is the same one we see in the commercial contracts we review, hundreds of them a year. A ban on spread pricing on paper does not mean spread pricing stopped. It means it moved and got relabeled. If your contract "prohibits spread," ask your broker to confirm the pieces that make the prohibition real: an acquisition-cost basis, pass-through of every network adjustment, and audit rights to actually test it. The word "prohibited" is only as strong as the definitions underneath it.

### House Committees Advance More PBM Transparency Bills

*PCMA | July 15-20, 2026*

Two House committees marked up new PBM legislation this week, including H.R. 9393, the Lower Costs, More Transparency Act, and a Ways and Means bill on price certainty. Both build on the PBM reforms already enacted in the Consolidated Appropriations Act. PCMA, the PBM trade group, argues the new bills largely duplicate existing law.

When the industry's own trade association says "we are already transparent, this is redundant," that is usually a signal that the disclosure being fought over is the one that bites. For self-funded employers, the practical read is not the odds of any single bill. It is that federal disclosure obligations keep expanding, and the reporting you are already entitled to is broader than most plans use. Ask your broker what your current contract already requires the PBM to hand over. Most plans leave leverage on the table they already paid for.

### 340B Purchases Hit $100 Billion in 2025

*Drug Channels | July 15, 2026*

Adam Fein's annual analysis put discounted 340B purchases at $100 billion in 2025, up 23% from the year before, with hospitals accounting for 87% of the total. The gap between list prices and 340B prices grew to $79.5 billion.

340B sits mostly with hospitals and providers, so I flag this as a market-shift story rather than an action item. But it reshapes the specialty channel your plan buys through. As more specialty spend routes through 340B-covered entities, the spread between what a drug costs and what your plan pays for it widens somewhere in the chain. It is worth knowing where your specialty claims actually land.

---

### FDA Approves Merck's First Oral PCSK9 Inhibitor

*STAT | July 16, 2026*

The FDA approved Merck's oral PCSK9 inhibitor, the first pill in a cholesterol-lowering class that until now existed only as high-cost injectables.

PCSK9 injectables have been a contained specialty spend, gated by cost and by the friction of an injection. A daily pill removes that friction. Convenience expands the eligible population, and expanded population is spend. This is a formulary and prior-authorization design conversation to have with your broker and clinical team before it lands in your claims data, not after.

### One More Worth Watching

Novo Nordisk sued Eli Lilly this week over GLP-1 advertising it calls deceptive, arguing Lilly leaned on outdated trial comparisons. The two makers fighting in public over whose obesity drug works better is a preview of the formulary and rebate positioning battle every plan covering GLP-1s is negotiating inside. Watch where the comparative claims settle.

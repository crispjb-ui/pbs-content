## Strategy Overview

At 24 subscribers, the fastest growth lever is engaging with other writers' audiences, not broadcasting to a small base. Every substantive reply on a Note from a writer with 500+ subscribers gets seen by a portion of their audience. That's discovery you can't replicate by posting original Notes to 15 people.

**Weekly target:** 4-6 engagements with other writers' Notes (in addition to 4-5 original Notes)

**Quality bar:** Every reply must add a specific, pharmacy-benefit insight that nobody else in the thread would have. No "great post!" comments. No generic agreement. Add something the writer and their audience didn't know.

## Reply Patterns: Their Topic → Your Pharmacy Angle

The core skill: connect any adjacent topic back to pharmacy benefits with a specific insight the writer and their audience didn't have.

### Healthcare Cost Transparency
**Their topic:** Hospital pricing, insurance opacity, surprise billing
**Your reply pattern:** "The pharmacy side is where the largest information asymmetry lives. PBMs operate with a level of opacity that hospitals can only dream of. Your plan pays one price, the pharmacy receives another, and the spread is PBM revenue. Most employers have no visibility into the gap."

### Employer Benefits Strategy
**Their topic:** Benefits design, total rewards, employer health plan decisions
**Your reply pattern:** "Pharmacy is typically the fastest-growing line item and the least scrutinized. Most employers spend more time negotiating office supply contracts than evaluating their PBM relationship. The contract language alone can mean the difference between significant savings and significant overspend."

### ERISA / Compliance / Fiduciary
**Their topic:** Fiduciary duty, plan governance, ERISA litigation
**Your reply pattern:** "The pharmacy-specific fiduciary exposure is accelerating. Courts are now examining whether employers followed a prudent process for PBM selection and oversight. 'We trusted our PBM' is not a fiduciary defense. The employers being targeted are not the ones who made bad decisions. They are the ones who made no decisions at all."

### Healthcare Reform / Policy
**Their topic:** Drug pricing legislation, 340B, PBM reform bills
**Your reply pattern:** "The 340B program is a good example of unintended consequences. Created for safety-net providers, it now affects employer pharmacy claims in ways most plans do not track. Providers can acquire drugs at substantial discounts and bill employer plans at standard rates. The spread is invisible in most claims data."

### CFO / Cost Management
**Their topic:** Healthcare spend, cost containment, financial strategy
**Your reply pattern:** "One category worth examining: specialty pharmacy. It often represents a substantial share of total health plan spend, and most of the routing decisions were made by the PBM, not the employer. The question 'who chose your specialty pharmacy?' is one most CFOs have never asked."

### Stop-Loss / Risk Management
**Their topic:** Captive insurance, self-funding risk, reinsurance
**Your reply pattern:** "The pharmacy side of stop-loss is underexplored. Gene therapy claims exceeding $1 million are no longer theoretical. Many stop-loss policies have exclusions or limitations for these therapies that employers have not reviewed. Two policies at similar premium levels can have meaningfully different pharmacy coverage."

### Drug Pricing / Pharma Industry
**Their topic:** Drug launches, manufacturer pricing, rebate dynamics
**Your reply pattern:** "From the employer plan perspective, this creates a specific problem. Copay cards funded by manufacturers redistribute cost from member to plan, not from manufacturer to plan. The drug is never free. The cost becomes invisible. Most employers do not realize their plan economics change when a manufacturer launches a copay program."

### Mental Health / Employee Wellbeing
**Their topic:** Workplace mental health, employee support, behavioral health access
**Your reply pattern:** "The pharmacy benefit side of mental health access is often overlooked. If your formulary requires stricter prior authorization or higher copays for psychiatric medications than for comparable non-behavioral health drugs, you may be creating barriers that violate MHPAEA parity requirements. Most employers have never run the pharmacy-specific comparison."

## Reply Quality Checklist

Before posting any reply, verify:

- [ ] Does it add a specific insight the writer and their audience didn't have?
- [ ] Does it connect to pharmacy benefits without being promotional?
- [ ] Is it substantive (3+ sentences, not just agreement)?
- [ ] Does it invite further discussion (question at the end)?
- [ ] Does it avoid self-promotion? (No links to your own publication in replies)
- [ ] Does it position you as a knowledgeable voice, not a salesperson?

---

## What NOT to Do

- **Do not link to your own Substack articles in replies.** This reads as promotional and turns people off. Let your profile do the work.
- **Do not use generic praise.** "Great post!" or "So true!" adds nothing and gets ignored.
- **Do not argue or correct.** Build on their point. Add nuance. Offer a different angle. Never make the writer feel challenged in front of their audience.
- **Do not engage with every Note.** Be selective. 3-5 high-quality engagements per week beats 15 generic ones.
- **Do not copy/paste the same reply across multiple Notes.** Each reply should be specific to the context.

### Daily Mix Guidelines

Every day should include a mix from these categories. Aim for 2-3 original Notes plus 1 engagement reply:

1. **Origin story / personal Note (3-4x per week minimum):** These are the highest-performing format. Reflective, first-person, connects Ginny's clinical background to the work. Best on weekends and Wednesday/Friday when the tone is more conversational.

2. **Provocative one-liner (2-3x per week):** Short, confrontational, restackable. These drive discovery. Use the confrontational hooks from LinkedIn data.

3. **Expertise insight (2-3x per week):** Shows depth of knowledge. Specific contract review patterns, claims data observations, industry mechanics.

4. **Engagement reply (1x per day minimum):** Reply to another writer's Note with a pharmacy-specific insight. This is the primary discovery engine at 24 subscribers.

5. **Content teasers (Mon + Thu):** Drive traffic to the weekly article and field note. Keep these short with a specific hook, not just "new post."

6. **Industry observation (1-2x per week):** Market trends, regulatory developments, PBM news. Positions Ginny as someone watching the industry with a unique perspective.

### Why Every Day Matters

- At 24 subscribers, consistency beats volume. The algorithm rewards daily activity.
- Weekend Notes get more visibility (less competition in the feed) and the engaged, deep-reading audience is browsing.
- Origin story Notes outperform every other format on engagement. Spreading them across the week (not just weekends) maximizes restacking potential.
- 1 engagement reply per day means 7 writers per week see Ginny's name and perspective in their threads. That's the fastest path to 100 subscribers.

## Voice and Tone

- **Analytical authority.** You are a clinical pharmacist who reviews ~100 PBM contracts annually. Speak from that expertise.
- **"We" framing** when referencing PBS work: "We see this pattern in claims audits..."
- **Never use "Dr. Ginny Crisp"** (Dr. and PharmD are redundant). Use "Ginny Crisp, PharmD" or just first person.
- **Company name:** Prescription Benefit Solutions (PBS). Never use RXBS.
- **No em dashes or hyphens as sentence separators.** Use commas, colons, semicolons, periods, or parentheses.
- **No fabricated statistics.** Use "significant," "substantial," "meaningful" instead of invented numbers.
- **Include disclaimer on illustrative examples:** "Illustrative example for educational purposes. Actual amounts vary by plan."

## Subscriber Value-Add Content (Beyond Weekly Deep Dives)

In addition to the weekly deep dive (Monday) and field note (Thursday), Benefit Blind Spots publishes several subscriber-exclusive content types. Reference files for each:

### 1. Monthly Q&A Thread (`substack_qa_template.md`)
- **Frequency:** Last Thursday of each month
- **Format:** Subscriber-only discussion thread
- **What it is:** "Ask me anything about your pharmacy benefit." Direct Q&A with Ginny.
- **Why it matters:** Only place subscribers can get personalized guidance. Not available on LinkedIn.

### 2. Quarterly "What We're Seeing" Report (`substack_what_were_seeing.md`)
- **Frequency:** End of each quarter (Jun, Sep, Dec, Mar)
- **Format:** Subscriber-only post, 500-700 words
- **What it is:** Aggregated patterns from PBS's ~100 annual PBM contract reviews. No client identified.
- **Why it matters:** Proprietary insight only PBS can produce. The single strongest reason to subscribe.

### 3. Contract Language Library (`substack_contract_language_library.md`)
- **Frequency:** Evergreen post, updated quarterly
- **Format:** Free (not paywalled; this is a discovery/SEO play)
- **What it is:** Side-by-side comparison of vague vs. protective PBM contract language across 10+ provisions.
- **Why it matters:** The most bookmarkable, searchable, shareable post on the publication. Attracts new readers through Google.

### 4. "What I'd Ask" Series (`substack_what_id_ask_series.md`)
- **Frequency:** Every other Tuesday
- **Format:** Subscriber-only post, 300-400 words
- **What it is:** Scenario-based question lists. "If I were reviewing [X], here are the 10 questions I'd ask."
- **Why it matters:** Immediately actionable. Readers can take these questions to their next meeting.

### 5. Weekly Exclusive Sections (in each newsletter file)
- **Frequency:** Every Monday deep dive
- **Format:** Section at the end of each Substack article
- **What it is:** Practical tool (template, checklist, walkthrough, scorecard) not included in the LinkedIn newsletter
- **Why it matters:** The specific reason for a LinkedIn newsletter reader to also subscribe to Substack

# Topic Roster for HEALTH CARE un-covered

**Ginny Crisp, PharmD — Prescription Benefit Solutions**
Twice-monthly contributing author proposal
May 13, 2026

---

My lane: clinical pharmacist auditing hundreds of PBM contracts a year, focused on PBM-specific structural reform. The roster below front-loads six pieces for impact, naming the structural mechanics that employer plan sponsors and policymakers most need to see, each with a specific regulatory or legislative prescription. Pieces seven through twelve continue the framework into more specialized PBM economics once the first six have established the analytic frame.

## First Six (Impact-Front-Loaded)

1. **Spread Pricing: Why the FTC's Next Move Should Be Mandatory Disclosure.** Same drug, same dose, two prices, and the difference vanishes into PBM accounts the plan never sees. The FTC already has the data; this piece names the regulatory move that should follow.

2. **Vertical Integration in PBMs.** Your PBM owns your specialty pharmacy, your insurer owns your PBM, and your PBM writes the rule that decides which pharmacy fills your member's high-cost drug. Three jobs, one vendor, and the bill that should break it up.

3. **GLP-1 Coverage and the PBM Profit Layer.** The GLP-1 access debate everyone is having is the wrong debate. The list-price-to-net-cost gap on the highest-leverage drug class in modern memory is where the money is actually moving, and where reform should focus.

4. **The Conflict of Interest at the Heart of Copay Accumulator Programs.** Manufacturer copay cards are a legitimate patient affordability tool and plan-sponsor accumulator programs can be a legitimate cost-containment lever, but the same PBM should not be controlling formulary placement, specialty pharmacy routing, and accumulator crediting math simultaneously. The reform is the structural firewall and member disclosure, not the prohibition.

5. **White-Bagging and Channel Steering.** Same drug. Same dose. Same patient. Three different prices, all set by the same PBM, who is paid more when the pharmacy it chooses is its own.

6. **The FTC PBM Report: What Should Happen Next.** In July 2024, the FTC published an interim staff report titled "Pharmacy Benefit Managers: The Powerful Middlemen Inflating Drug Costs and Squeezing Main Street Pharmacies." Nearly two years later there has been no final report and no comprehensive enforcement action; here are five named actions that should follow.

## Long Roster (Pieces 7-12)

7. **Rebate Aggregators: The Offshore Middlemen.** Ascent, Emisar, Zinc, and Premier are four PBM-owned entities most plan sponsors have never heard of, and they intercept manufacturer rebate flow before the pass-through math is calculated. A structural deception in plain sight.

8. **DIR Fees and Their Successors.** The 2024 CMS rule banned retroactive DIR clawbacks in Medicare Part D, a real win. The PBMs are now reconstructing the same economic effect under new names; this piece names what to watch for.

9. **Specialty Drug Routing Economics.** Beyond white-bagging, specialty routing is where vertical integration generates the largest hidden margin in the modern PBM contract. The PBM-owned specialty pharmacy does not compete on price; it competes on routing.

10. **Manufacturer Patient Assistance Diversion.** A manufacturer foundation pays $5,000 to help a patient afford a specialty drug, and the PBM-owned specialty pharmacy is the gatekeeper, the formulary writer, and the entity that decides whether the assistance counts toward the deductible. Three conflicts, same vendor.

11. **The PBM RFP Methodology Problem.** The PBMs know which scoring methodology your broker is using, and the headline pricing in their proposal is engineered to win that exact methodology. The information asymmetry is structural, and the fix is fiduciary clarity.

12. **The Audit Rights Gap.** Plan sponsor audit rights in PBM contracts are technically present and operationally hollow: the windows are tight, the designees are restricted, the data extracts are PBM-defined. A federal audit-rights floor is the cleanest structural reform on the table.

## Editorial Approach

Each piece runs 800-1,000 words, opens with a short analogy that frames the reform argument, names the responsible PBM actors and their captive entities by company, anchors the structural argument in patterns drawn from hundreds of PBM contracts reviewed each year at PBS, and closes with a specific prescription: named regulator, named action, named instrument.

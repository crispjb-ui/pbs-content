## ▼ DRAFT BEGINS ▼

**Working title: Same Drug. Same Dose. Three Prices. And the Company That Picks the Price Owns One of the Pharmacies.**

Imagine a gas station where the price depends on which pump you pull into, none of the prices are posted, and the station owns the middle pump. You signed a fuel contract for your whole fleet, so your drivers go where the dispatcher sends them. The dispatcher works for the station. That is specialty drug routing in employer health plans. It is not a metaphor stretched. It is the prior authorization workflow.

The last chapter was about whose column the manufacturer's money lands in. This one is about a decision made before that: which door the prescription is allowed to walk through. On the series map we are back at the fifth revenue stream, the margin inside the pharmacies the Pharmacy Benefit Managers (PBMs) own, and specifically at the routing machinery that feeds it.

Here is how the routing works inside a plan. A specialty drug, one of the expensive medicines that now drive half or more of many plans' drug spending, can typically be dispensed through more than one channel: the PBM-owned specialty pharmacy, an independent specialty pharmacy, or the hospital or clinic where the drug is administered. The price for the same drug, same dose, same patient can differ substantially by channel. And the member does not choose the channel. Neither, usually, does the doctor. The PBM's rules choose it. The prior authorization workflow approves the drug on the condition it is filled at a designated pharmacy. The specialty drug list, which the PBM writes, defines which drugs are subject to that routing. Sometimes the arrangement goes further into what the industry calls white-bagging, where the PBM's pharmacy ships the drug to the hospital, displacing the hospital's own supply, because the shipping pharmacy captures the margin that way.

In the channel pricing audits we run at my firm, the pattern repeats: three different net costs for the same drug, and the fills concentrated in the channel the PBM's parent company owns. The names from earlier in this series are the names here: CVS Health's Caremark routes to CVS Specialty, UnitedHealth's OptumRx to Optum Specialty, Cigna's Express Scripts to Accredo. The contract language about network choice reads as if options exist. The claims data says the dispatcher has a favorite pump.

Why does the pricing gap survive? Because the contract's pricing protections were mostly built for the retail generic world. The Maximum Allowable Cost (MAC) schedule, the ceiling-price list that disciplines generic pricing, is generally silent on specialty channels. The guarantees are measured in aggregates that blend the channels together. So the one category of drug where the money is largest is the category where the contract says the least, and the entity that profits from the widest channel writes the routing rules.

The states have started moving on the most visible piece of this. Several, including Louisiana and Texas, have restricted PBM-mandated white-bagging, mostly on patient-safety and provider-autonomy grounds. That momentum is real and worth having. But it reaches the practice, not the economics, and like every state fix this series has covered, it largely stops at the border of self-funded employer plans, which sit under the federal ERISA framework.

So the prescription here is a federal floor with two planks. The first is site-of-care neutrality with disclosure: before a PBM may require that a drug be filled in a particular channel, the plan sponsor should receive the net cost of that drug in each available channel, in writing. Routing to the expensive door should have to survive being seen. The second is audit rights that reach specialty economics: an ERISA amendment giving self-funded plans the right to audit per-channel pricing and routing patterns, with their own auditor. Where a state wants to go further for the plans it can reach, the insurance commissioner should have clear authority to do it.

My view is that this is the reform where state legislatures are leading and the federal government is simply behind. The states have documented the practice. The employers are paying for it. A disclosure-and-audit floor is not a radical intervention; it is the federal regulator catching up to what the paper already shows.

Two chapters ago this series looked at who owns the pharmacies. This chapter looked at how the prescriptions are steered into them. The next one steps back, because the federal government's own competition agency has already investigated all of it, documented most of it, and then went quiet. What should happen after a diagnosis like that is the subject of the next piece.

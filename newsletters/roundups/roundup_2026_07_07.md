### The GLP-1 Cash Price Keeps Falling. Your Plan Price May Not.

*STAT News, Mercer | July 1, 2026*

New coverage this week put a finer point on something we have been telling clients for months. The Trump administration's deals with Lilly and Novo set roughly $245-per-month GLP-1 pricing for Medicare, Medicaid, and cash-paying patients buying through the direct-to-consumer channel. Those agreements say nothing about what employer plans pay. The PBMs insist their rebates already bring your net cost close to the cash price.

I would want to see that math before I believed it. If a member can buy the drug direct for $245 and your plan is paying meaningfully more after rebates, the rebate is working for the PBM, not for you. Ask your broker to pull your net GLP-1 cost per fill and put it next to the published cash price. That single comparison tells you whether your contract is capturing the savings or letting them route around you.

---

### Some Insurers Are Keeping the Copay Assistance Meant for Patients

*KFF Health News | July 7, 2026*

KFF reported this week on copay accumulator and maximizer programs, where the plan collects a drugmaker's copay coupon but refuses to count it toward the member's deductible or out-of-pocket max. One patient on Enbrel paid $10,600 out of pocket instead of roughly $3,000 because his new insurer would not count the assistance. Nearly 40% of ACA marketplace plans now use these designs, and 26 states plus D.C. have restricted them.

This is a design choice sitting inside your contract, and most self-funded employers do not know which version they have. We see accumulators and maximizers described in vague benefit language that most plan sponsors never decode. If specialty copay assistance touches your plan, ask specifically how those dollars are treated and who keeps them.

---

### CMS Proposes Cutting 340B Drug Payments by Nearly 40%

*STAT News, CMS | July 2, 2026*

CMS proposed reimbursing 340B-acquired drugs under Medicare Part B at Average Sales Price minus 33.4% starting in 2027, close to a 40% reduction, alongside broader site-neutral payment changes. Comments are due August 31.

This is a Medicare rule, so the direct hit lands on hospitals, not your plan. The reason I flag it: it reprices hospital drug economics, and when a revenue stream gets cut, the pressure moves to commercial payers. Watch your hospital-based infusion and specialty claims over the next year. The site-of-care conversation just got more urgent.

---

### A Federal Judge Blocks Colorado's Drug Price Cap

*STAT News | July 2, 2026*

A federal judge blocked Colorado's first-of-its-kind upper payment limit on Amgen's Enbrel, set by the state's prescription drug affordability board. Several states run similar boards.

If you have banked on state price caps to control specialty spend, this is a reminder that the legal ground under them is still shifting. The levers you actually control, contract language and formulary strategy, are more reliable than waiting for a state remedy that may not survive court.

---

### One More Worth Watching

The FDA is moving toward treating most non-vaccine biosimilars as interchangeable by default and dropping some comparative efficacy study requirements. If that framework finalizes, biosimilar competition accelerates and cheaper biologic options reach your formulary faster. Worth a formulary review conversation with your broker before renewal.

## ▼ DRAFT BEGINS ▼

**Working title: Your PBM Owns the Pharmacy It Sends Your Prescriptions To. Here Is Why That Should Worry You.**

Imagine if your auto insurer also owned the repair shop, the parts supplier, and the salvage yard, and got to decide where your car was towed after an accident. The repair bill comes back and it looks reasonable enough. But you have no way to test whether the parts were fairly priced or whether the salvage credit ever made it back to you, because every receipt in the file was written by a company that answers to the same owner. That is the structure of the modern pharmacy benefit. It is not a metaphor stretched. It is the org chart.

In the last piece we followed one number, the spread between what a health plan pays for a generic prescription and what the pharmacy that fills it actually receives. This piece is about the fifth revenue stream from the map this series started with: the margin inside the pharmacies the Pharmacy Benefit Managers (PBMs) own themselves.

Start with who owns what, because the names matter. CVS Health owns Caremark, the PBM, and it owns CVS Specialty, a specialty pharmacy. UnitedHealth owns OptumRx, the PBM, and Optum Specialty. Cigna owns Express Scripts, the PBM, and Accredo. In each case, one parent company owns the insurer, the PBM, and the pharmacy. Simply put, the company that decides where a prescription can be filled also owns one of the places that fills it.

Now walk through what that means inside a single plan year. A member of a self-funded employer plan is prescribed a specialty drug, one of the high-cost medicines that now make up half or more of many plans' drug spending. The PBM writes the prior authorization rule that decides whether the drug is approved. The PBM's formulary decides which version of the drug is preferred. The PBM's network rules decide which pharmacies are allowed to fill it, and specialty drugs are usually restricted to a short list. And the pharmacy at the top of that short list, very often, is the one the PBM's parent company owns. At the end of the year, the PBM writes the report telling the employer how well all of this worked.

Every step in that chain can be reasonable on its own. The problem is that no one independent ever touches the transaction. The same vendor writes the rule, routes the prescription, fills it, prices it, and grades its own performance. I review PBM contracts for a living, and here is the pattern I see again and again: the contract language about pharmacy networks reads as if the plan has choices, and the actual routing of high-cost prescriptions runs overwhelmingly to the pharmacy the PBM owns. The paper says open. The claims say captive.

The Federal Trade Commission (FTC) documented this structure in its interim report on PBMs in 2024. The report described how the largest PBMs are vertically integrated with insurers and pharmacies and how that integration can disadvantage independent pharmacies and obscure costs. What the report did not do is fix anything. Documentation is not enforcement, and in the time since, the integration has only deepened.

You might expect the states to step in, and many have tried. Here is the wall they hit. Most large employers self-fund their health plans, which places those plans under a federal law, the Employee Retirement Income Security Act (ERISA). State insurance rules generally cannot reach inside an ERISA plan. So when a state passes a law about PBM steering or pharmacy ownership, it usually covers the smaller, fully insured market and leaves the self-funded majority untouched. The regulator that could reach the whole market is federal.

So what should Congress actually do about ownership? There are two honest paths.

The first is separation: a federal requirement that PBMs above a certain market share divest their pharmacies, so the company routing prescriptions no longer owns a destination. That is the structural fix, and it is also the slow one. Divestiture fights take years, and plans are paying for this structure now.

The second path is a firewall with a flashlight. Congress could require that when a PBM and a pharmacy share an owner, the functions that create the conflict must be contractually separated: the people who write the routing and prior authorization rules cannot be paid on the owned pharmacy's results, the plan must be told, drug by drug, what was routed to an owned pharmacy and what it cost there compared to elsewhere, and the plan must have the right to audit that routing without needing the PBM's permission. None of that breaks up anything. It simply lets the customer see the steering.

My view is that the firewall is the reform worth spending this Congress on. It is achievable, it produces benefits employers can measure in the first year, and it builds the evidence record that a future divestiture case would need anyway. Separation can stay on the table as the endpoint. Sight is the immediate need, because you cannot negotiate with a structure you cannot see.

There is one more piece of this ownership story worth flagging now. The routing conflict this piece describes has a quieter cousin on the rebate side, where the entities collecting manufacturer payments also share owners with the PBMs. That is a later chapter. The next one is about the drug class putting more pressure on plan budgets than any in modern memory, and what the fight over it keeps missing.

### Warren and Hawley Reintroduce the Patients Before Monopolies Act

*Multiple outlets | May 13, 2026*

Last Tuesday, May 13, Senators Elizabeth Warren and Josh Hawley reintroduced the Patients Before Monopolies Act, federal legislation that would prohibit joint ownership of pharmacy benefit managers and pharmacies. The companion bill in the House comes from Rep. Diana Harshbarger (R-Tenn.), herself a lifelong pharmacist, and Rep. Jake Auchincloss (D-Mass.). New cosponsors crossed both chambers and both parties: Senators John Fetterman (D-Pa.) and Roger Marshall (R-Kan.), Representatives Greg Landsman (D-Ohio) and Buddy Carter (R-Ga.) of Energy and Commerce, and Jerrold Nadler (D-N.Y.) and Troy Nehls (R-Texas) of Judiciary. Bipartisan momentum is unusual on antitrust legislation. This is not.

The mechanic is direct. If a corporation owns a PBM or insurer, it cannot also own a pharmacy business. Existing vertical integrations divest within one year. The FTC, Department of Health and Human Services, DOJ, state attorneys general, and harmed individuals all get enforcement authority. The FTC distributes disgorged revenue to harmed communities, including consumers overcharged at vertically integrated pharmacies. The bill also requires reporting of all divestitures to the FTC with FTC review.

Three PBMs would be materially affected: CVS Caremark (owns CVS Pharmacy), Express Scripts (owns specialty and mail-order pharmacy networks), and OptumRx (owns specialty pharmacy and home delivery operations). Together these three handle roughly 80% of all U.S. prescription claims.

What changed since Warren and Hawley first introduced the bill in December 2024: 39 state attorneys general have formally asked Congress to pass it. Tennessee passed the same divestiture requirement at the state level in April; Sen. Warren's office indicates the Tennessee Governor is expected to sign that bill into law soon. The FTC's July 2024 report on PBM market concentration laid the empirical groundwork the legislation now builds on. Several organizations are publicly supporting the bill, including the American Pharmacy Cooperative, Pharmacists United for Truth and Transparency, the Coalition of State Rheumatology Organizations, the American Economic Liberties Project, the Alliance for Transparent and Affordable Prescriptions, and American Pharmacies.

What to watch: the bill is expected to go through the Committee on Energy and Commerce or the Judiciary Committee before reaching the House floor under regular order. That favors a slower but more durable path than budget reconciliation. The April 14 PCMA testimony on the parallel Rhode Island spread-pricing legislation, which I flagged last week, is still the cleanest articulation of the industry's defense; reading both alongside each other gives you the full picture of where the policy fight is on the record.

For self-funded plan sponsors, the audit work matters regardless of what happens to the bill. In the hundreds of PBM contracts PBS reviews each year, the most opaque pricing clauses are the ones where the PBM owns the dispensing pharmacy. Specialty drug routing patterns are the clearest tell. Pull a quarter's specialty fills, sort by dispensing pharmacy ownership, and the gap between the network clause and the actual per-claim margin surfaces in an afternoon. Whether you support or oppose the legislation, the routing audit is the line item to run this quarter.

### Supreme Court Rejects Pharma Challenge to Medicare Drug Price Negotiation

*STAT News | May 18, 2026*

The Supreme Court rejected pharma's challenge to the Inflation Reduction Act's Medicare drug price negotiation provisions. STAT's reporting frames the remaining manufacturer lawsuits as "more difficult" after the rejection, which is the polite way of saying the public-price floor is now durable.

I have been waiting to see what happens to the rebate-and-spread math when CMS publishes negotiated prices for the highest-spend drugs in Part D. We are about to find out. Every employer's PBM rebate quote, including yours, gets recalibrated against a public benchmark that did not exist before. Ask your broker how negotiated Part D prices for drugs on your formulary compare to your net-of-rebate cost. The gap is the conversation.

### Maryland Affordability Board Caps Ozempic

*STAT News | May 18, 2026*

Maryland's prescription drug affordability board placed a price ceiling on Ozempic, the first time a state board has set a binding upper limit on a GLP-1. Nine other states have similar boards in various stages of implementation.

State boards do not bind self-funded ERISA plans directly. What they do is publish a number. When Maryland says Ozempic cannot cost more than X for a state-regulated plan, your plan's rebate-adjusted net cost on Ozempic now has a visible comparison point. We see plan sponsors using state board prices as leverage in PBM conversations where the "guaranteed" net somehow lands well above the public ceiling. Worth pulling your last quarter's Ozempic per-fill cost and lining it up against the Maryland cap before your next pricing review.

### TrumpRx Adds Amazon, GoodRx, Cost Plus Drugs and 600 Generics

*STAT News | May 18, 2026*

The White House added Amazon Pharmacy, GoodRx, and Mark Cuban's Cost Plus Drugs to the TrumpRx platform, expanding the cash-pay catalog to 600 generic medications. PCMA, the PBM trade association, issued a statement applauding the move.

Six hundred generics is a small slice of plan spend. The architecture is the story. A federally branded cash-pay rail that bypasses PBM rebates entirely now has three major retail partners and tacit industry endorsement. For plans with high-deductible designs, the lowest-cost option for a routine generic may already sit outside your plan benefit, and members will find it. Plans should be asking whether the PBM's "guaranteed" generic discount actually beats Cost Plus and GoodRx, or simply beats AWP.

### New Jersey Passes A1502 PBM Reform Bill

*PCMA | May 18, 2026*

The New Jersey Assembly passed A1502, restricting PBM practices including spread pricing and steering. PCMA, the PBM trade association, claims the bill will add roughly $1 billion in prescription costs in year one for NJ employers and state health programs.

When the PBM trade group publishes a billion-dollar cost-impact figure, that is industry advocacy, not an audit. Self-funded ERISA plans may sit outside the direct reach of state PBM laws, but vendor contracts written under NJ law often pull similar provisions into their MAC and audit language anyway. Brokers in NJ are the right call this week. Which clauses in your current PBM agreement track A1502, and which do not.

### One More Worth Watching

Takeda agreed to pay $13.6 million to settle DOJ allegations it paid kickbacks to doctors to prescribe its antidepressant. When a manufacturer settles on kickbacks for a drug your plan reimburses, the question worth asking is how much of that drug's plan-spend market share was earned by clinical fit and how much by paid promotion. A formulary review is the answer.

An independent PBM review means the reviewer takes no PBM revenue, has no ownership stake in any vendor the findings could affect, earns nothing from which PBM you pick, discloses its compensation in writing, and delivers complete findings to you and no one else first. That is the whole test. This week we published it.

It is live at [www.rxbs.org/standards](https://www.rxbs.org/standards): The Standards for Independent PBM Review, version 1.0. Five independence criteria, the minimum scope a real review has to cover, and the deliverables you should receive at the end. It is written so any plan sponsor can hand it to any reviewer, including us, and ask a simple question: do you meet this?

Here is why we wrote it down. "Independent" is the most used and least defined word in pharmacy benefits consulting. We review hundreds of PBM contracts a year, and the reviews that cross our desk secondhand carry the word constantly: independent analysis, independent assessment, independent market check. The word appears in the deliverable. The revenue behind the deliverable does not. A review can be branded independent while the firm producing it earns placement income from the outcome, participates in rebate economics somewhere in the chain, or answers to an owner whose book of business the findings could disturb. Both things are true at once, and only one of them is disclosed.

The five criteria are the disclosure. In the published version, each one is a plain statement a reviewer either signs or does not: no PBM revenue in any form; no ownership entanglement in either direction; no compensation contingent on which vendor the plan selects; compensation disclosed in writing before work begins; findings owned by the plan, unfiltered by any third party.

Two things worth knowing about how we operate under it. Every review engagement we deliver now includes a signed, one-page Independence Attestation stating all five criteria for that specific engagement. Not a philosophy page on a website: a document with a client name, an engagement name, and a signature on it. And the Standards carry a revision date, not just a publish date. We revise each January, in public, on the same page.

One more thing the page is deliberately not. It is not an argument that your current advisors have failed you. The best brokers we work with are the ones who bring us in, precisely because a strong independent read makes their renewal recommendation stronger. The Standards give them, and you, a shared yardstick.

The plain next step: before your next review, whoever performs it, send them the five criteria and ask which ones they will attest to in writing. The answer tells you most of what you need to know before a single claim is analyzed.

# Topic Roster for HEALTH CARE un-covered

**Ginny Crisp, PharmD — Prescription Benefit Solutions**
Twice-monthly contributing author proposal

---

My lane: clinical pharmacist auditing hundreds of PBM contracts a year, focused on PBM-specific structural reform. The roster below front-loads six pieces for impact — naming the structural mechanics that employer plan sponsors and policymakers most need to see, each with a specific regulatory or legislative prescription. Pieces seven through twelve continue the framework into more specialized PBM economics once the first six have established the analytic frame.

## First Six (Impact-Front-Loaded)

1. **Spread Pricing — Why the FTC's Next Move Should Be Mandatory Disclosure.** PBM spread pricing is a structural deception enabled by contract opacity. The FTC has the authority and the data; per-NDC disclosure is the next regulatory move.

2. **Vertical Integration in PBMs.** Two paths exist for the structural problem the FTC interim report documented: divestiture mandate or contractual firewall with plan sponsor audit rights. The contractual firewall path is achievable in the current Congress.

3. **GLP-1 Coverage and the PBM Profit Layer.** The current GLP-1 access debate misses the structural problem. The list-price-to-net-cost gap on the highest-leverage drug class in modern memory is where reform should focus.

4. **Copay Accumulator and Maximizer Programs.** Twenty-one states have enacted protections; ERISA preemption gaps the floor for self-insured plans. Federal floor with ERISA amendment is the next step.

5. **White-Bagging and Channel Steering.** Three different prices for the same drug, set by the same PBM. Site-of-care neutrality regulation and plan sponsor audit rights for specialty pharmacy pass-through are the reform paths.

6. **The FTC PBM Report — What Should Happen Next.** The diagnostic is complete. Five named actions follow: two within existing FTC and HHS authority, three requiring Congress.

## Long Roster (Pieces 7-12)

7. **Rebate Aggregators — The Offshore Middlemen.** PBM-owned aggregators intercept rebate flow before the pass-through math. GPO-style transparency rules plus federal pass-through legislation are the reform paths.

8. **DIR Fees and Their Successors.** The 2024 CMS rule on Medicare Part D DIR was a real win. The PBM workarounds reconstructing the same economics are the next reform target.

9. **Specialty Drug Routing Economics.** Beyond white-bagging, specialty routing is where vertical integration generates the largest hidden margin. Per-fill pass-through disclosure and audit rights are the reform paths.

10. **Manufacturer Patient Assistance Diversion.** The PBM-owned specialty pharmacy controls assistance access, formulary access, and assistance crediting. The structural conflict-of-interest reform follows.

11. **The PBM RFP Methodology Problem.** PBM RFP methodologies are engineered to be won, not understood. Standardized methodology requirements and ERISA fiduciary clarity are the reform paths.

12. **The Audit Rights Gap.** Plan sponsor audit rights in PBM contracts are technically present but operationally hollow. A federal audit-rights floor via ERISA amendment is the cleanest structural reform on the table.

## Editorial Approach

Each piece runs 800-1,000 words, opens with a short analogy that frames the reform argument, names the responsible PBM actors and their captive entities by company, anchors the structural argument in patterns drawn from hundreds of PBM contracts reviewed each year at PBS, and closes with a specific prescription — named regulator, named action, named instrument.

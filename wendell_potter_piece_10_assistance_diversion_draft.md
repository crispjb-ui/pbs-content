## ▼ DRAFT BEGINS ▼

**Working title: The Money Meant to Help Patients Afford Their Drugs Is Being Collected by the Companies That Set the Prices**

Imagine a charity that exists to help patients afford expensive medicine. Its money is intercepted on the way to the patient by the same organization that decides which medicines are covered, which pharmacy dispenses them, and whether the charity's help counts toward what the patient owes. The patient sees none of the mechanics, only the result: the help ran out, and the deductible is somehow still there. That interception is legal, common, and written into plan documents almost nobody reads. This chapter is about how it works and where the money goes.

The last chapter followed specialty prescriptions into the routed pharmacies; this one follows a different tributary into the same machinery. On the series map we are back on stream four, manufacturer payments, but at its patient-facing branch: copay cards, foundation grants, and assistance funds, the money manufacturers spend so that list prices do not crush the people taking the drugs.

The accumulator chapter earlier in this series showed the crediting trick: assistance dollars applied to the claim but not to the member's deductible. Here is the fuller pathway, because the crediting trick is only the last step. A manufacturer funds an assistance program. A patient on a specialty drug applies, or more often, is enrolled by the specialty pharmacy itself, since enrollment is part of the pharmacy's service model. That pharmacy, as this series has established, typically belongs to the PBM's parent company. The PBM's program, the SaveOnSP and PrudentRx machinery from the accumulator chapter, then sets the copay amounts to draw down the manufacturer's fund efficiently across the year and applies the money in the column that benefits the program's economics. Follow the dollar end to end and the picture resolves: money that entered the system as patient assistance exits as program revenue, while the patient's own obligations were, at best, deferred.

Look at the conflicts stacked inside that pathway, one entity deep at every layer. The gatekeeper that enrolls the patient in assistance is the pharmacy that profits from the fill. The formulary that decides which drugs are eligible for the program is written by the PBM that runs the program. And the math deciding whether the assistance counts toward the member's deductible is administered by the party whose fees are calculated from the assistance collected. In the contracts I review, each of these roles is disclosed somewhere, in some document. What is almost never disclosed is that they are the same vendor, described three ways.

The state protections story repeats from the accumulator chapter, so I will state it briefly: a majority of states now require assistance to count toward member cost-sharing in the plans they regulate, and ERISA keeps those requirements out of most employer plans. The floor exists where the problem is smallest. The pattern is now familiar enough that it is fair to call it the series' refrain.

The reform, stated plainly. First, a federal crediting rule: manufacturer assistance connected to a member's claim counts toward that member's deductible and out-of-pocket maximum, in every market, with plan-design flexibility preserved through disclosure rather than through silence: a plan that wants to run a maximizer openly, with members told exactly what it means, may make that case in daylight. Second, the structural plank this whole series keeps arriving at: the entity that gatekeeps assistance enrollment should not be the entity that profits from the fill or from the program's fees. Where the roles cannot be separated by divestiture, they can be separated by contract, by audit, and by the disclosure that makes the arrangement's economics legible to the plan sponsor paying for all of it.

My view is that this is not a coupon-program controversy, though it is usually covered as one. It is the same structural story as every other chapter: a conflicted intermediary sitting on all sides of a transaction, with the added feature that here, the dollars being redirected were aimed at patients. Whatever one thinks about manufacturer assistance as pricing strategy, and there is a real debate there, the answer to marketing money is not letting the middleman keep it.

This chapter closes the mechanisms act of the series. Every stream on the map has now been walked. The remaining chapters turn to the response: first, the process by which plan sponsors choose these vendors at all, an auction that the bidders understand far better than the buyers, and then the single contractual right that decides whether anything in this series can be verified by the people paying for it.

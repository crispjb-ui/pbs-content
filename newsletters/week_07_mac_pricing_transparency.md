## The Hidden Math in Your Generic Drug Costs

**Publication:** Benefit Blind Spots
**Publish:** Monday, February 16, 2026 at 7:30 AM EST
**Length:** ~1,400 words (~5 min read)
**Tone:** Analytical authority
**Pillar:** Transparency & Industry Education

---

### Article Body

Generic drugs cost pennies to manufacture. So why does your plan sometimes pay dollars?

I reviewed a PBM contract last quarter where the employer had no idea what their MAC pricing arrangement looked like. They assumed generics were cheap and moved on. When we dug into the claims data, the spread on generic drugs was costing them more per month than they realized.

The answer lives in three letters most employers have never examined closely: MAC.

Maximum Allowable Cost pricing is one of the least understood, and most manipulated, aspects of pharmacy benefits. It determines what you pay for the majority of prescriptions in your plan. And the entity that controls your MAC list also controls your generic drug costs.

For self-funded employers, understanding MAC pricing is not optional. It is foundational to knowing whether your PBM contract delivers what it promises.

---

**What MAC Actually Means**

MAC stands for Maximum Allowable Cost. It represents the ceiling your PBM sets for reimbursement on multi-source generic drugs, medications where multiple manufacturers produce the same product.

The concept seems straightforward: establish a maximum price to prevent overpayment when generic alternatives exist. The execution is where complexity (and margin) enters.

Here is the basic formula:

Your Cost = MAC Price + Dispensing Fee

The MAC price is what the PBM allows for the drug itself. The dispensing fee compensates the pharmacy for filling the prescription. Together, they determine what your plan pays.

The problem: your PBM controls both variables. They set the MAC list. They decide which drugs appear on it. They determine when prices update. They negotiate dispensing fees with pharmacies.

The spread between what your PBM pays the pharmacy and what they charge your plan? That is where margin hides.

---

**How MAC Lists Create Hidden Margin**

Consider a generic medication with an acquisition cost of $0.03 per tablet.

Your PBM's MAC price might be $0.45 per tablet. They pay the pharmacy $0.15. They charge your plan $0.45. The $0.30 difference per tablet is spread: margin the PBM captures on every fill.

Illustrative example for educational purposes. Actual amounts vary by plan.

Now multiply this across thousands of generic prescriptions monthly.

The 2025 Drug Channels Institute analysis found that spread pricing on generics remains a significant PBM revenue source, even as transparency pressure has reduced spread on brand medications. The generic drug market (where employers assume they are getting commodity pricing) is often where the largest per-claim margins exist.

Three mechanisms drive this:

*1. MAC List Timing*

When generic acquisition costs drop (due to increased competition or market changes), there is often a delay before MAC prices adjust downward. During this lag, the PBM captures additional margin.

Some PBMs update MAC lists weekly. Others update monthly or less frequently. The update cadence directly impacts your costs.

*2. MAC List Selection*

Your PBM decides which drugs appear on their MAC list. Drugs not on the MAC list default to different pricing, often higher. The composition of the MAC list is rarely disclosed in detail.

*3. MAC List Proprietary Status*

Most PBM MAC lists are proprietary. You cannot see the actual prices. You cannot compare them to acquisition costs. You cannot verify whether the pricing is competitive without conducting an audit (which your contract may limit).

---

**What to Look for in Your Contract**

PBM contracts handle MAC pricing in various ways. When we sit down with a plan sponsor to review their arrangement, here is what we examine:

*MAC Update Frequency*

Does your contract specify how often the MAC list updates? Weekly updates are industry standard for competitive arrangements. Monthly or "periodic" updates favor the PBM.

*MAC List Transparency*

Can you access the MAC list? Can you see which drugs are included and at what prices? "Summary" access is not the same as detailed access.

*MAC Appeals Process*

When pharmacies appeal MAC prices as below acquisition cost, what happens? Does your plan benefit from successful appeals, or does the PBM absorb the adjustment without passing savings through?

*Spread Pricing vs. Pass-Through*

Does your contract use spread pricing (PBM captures difference between what they pay pharmacy and charge you) or pass-through pricing (you pay what PBM pays, plus transparent admin fee)?

Pass-through arrangements with transparent MAC lists provide the clearest view of actual costs. Spread arrangements with proprietary MAC lists provide the least.

---

**The Pass-Through Alternative**

Pass-through pricing removes the spread incentive. Under this model:

- You pay what the PBM pays the pharmacy (actual acquisition cost plus pharmacy margin)
- The PBM charges a transparent administrative fee per claim
- MAC list composition and updates become visible because there is no margin to hide

The trade-off: pass-through arrangements may carry higher per-claim administrative fees. The question is whether transparent admin fees exceed hidden spread. In our experience, the math favors transparency for most employers.

According to the 2025 PBMI survey, approximately 35% of employers now use some form of pass-through generic pricing, up from 22% in 2022. The trend toward transparency continues, but the majority of arrangements still involve spread.

---

**Questions to Ask Your PBM**

If you have not examined your MAC pricing arrangement recently, these are the questions I would start with. Your broker or consultant can help you work through these:

1. How frequently does your MAC list update?
2. Can we access the MAC list with specific drug pricing?
3. What percentage of generic claims process through MAC pricing?
4. What is the average MAC price versus average acquisition cost?
5. Do we have spread pricing or pass-through pricing on generics?
6. What was our total generic spend last year, and what would it have been under pass-through?

Some PBMs will answer these questions readily. Others will deflect. The response itself is informative.

---

**The Position We Take**

Generic drugs should be commodities. When 15 manufacturers produce the same molecule, pricing should approach manufacturing cost plus reasonable distribution margin.

The fact that generic drug pricing remains opaque for most employers tells you something about the market structure. MAC pricing is the mechanism that allows PBMs to capture margin on drugs that should be priced as commodities.

We advise employers to pursue transparency on generic pricing with the same rigor applied to brand and specialty medications. The dollars at stake are significant. Generics represent 90% of prescriptions dispensed, and small per-claim margin multiplied across high volume produces meaningful plan cost. The best brokers are already flagging this for their clients.

Understanding MAC is step one. Negotiating better terms is step two. Auditing performance is step three. All three require moving past the assumption that generics are "already cheap" and therefore not worth examining.

The pennies add up.

**Created:** July 7, 2026. This is the few-shot pack for Ginny Crisp's voice: her best actual published copy, annotated with why each passage works mechanically. It pairs with `ginny_voice_fingerprint.md` (the rules extracted from these examples). **Any model drafting anything in Ginny's voice loads this file first and matches the shape of the nearest canon entry before writing.** Models imitate examples better than they follow rules; this file is the examples.

**How entries were chosen:** by measured performance (the all-time top posts, the record deep dives) and by provenance (copy Ginny herself finalized, especially where her edits to drafts are documented). Every excerpt is verbatim from the repo file cited.

**Maintenance:** `/log-voice-delta` appends to the delta log at the bottom whenever Ginny edits drafted copy before publishing. When the canon grows stale (a new breakout post, a new format), add the new entry with the same annotation structure and cite the performance data. Do not remove entries unless the format itself is retired.

### Entry 1 — The named-actor dollar comparison (340B, 42,968 impressions, Cuban repost)
**Source:** `newsletters/week_15_340b_program_impact.md` (Strong POV Text)

> A provider acquires a drug at a steep 340B discount. Your employee fills the prescription at that provider's pharmacy. Your plan gets billed at standard rates.
>
> The spread between what they paid and what you paid becomes their revenue. Not yours.
>
> This is legal. It is how the 340B program works. And most employers have no idea it is happening in their claims data.
>
> You cannot manage what you cannot see. And right now, most plans are not even looking.
>
> The first step is knowing to ask.

**Why it works:** three plain declaratives walk the money's route before any judgment appears (actor → mechanism → who pays, fingerprint Layer 3.1). "This is legal." is the calm escalation: the most damning line is the flattest. Sentence lengths run 14-9-13 then 3 ("Not yours."), the arrhythmic landing pattern. Zero adjectives doing the outrage; the structure does it. Ends on a plain action, not a CTA. A lesser writer opens with "Here's something shocking about 340B" and kills it.

### Entry 2 — The single-number hook (GLP-1 "1 in 12," 64,323 impressions, all-time #1)
**Source:** `pbs_q1_2026_post_copy.md` (GLP-1 Reality Check, Text POV)

> Here's the GLP-1 number nobody's talking about.
>
> 1 in 12.
>
> That's how many members are still on GLP-1 therapy after three years.
>
> Nearly two-thirds stop before the 12-week mark needed for meaningful weight loss.
>
> So when an employer adds GLP-1 coverage at $12,000-$17,000 per person annually, the real question isn't "do these drugs work?"
>
> They do.
>
> The question is: does your plan design support the conditions that make them work long-term?
>
> Coverage without clinical support produces the worst outcome: high pharmacy spend with high discontinuation.
>
> The employers getting this right treat GLP-1s as one piece of a program.
>
> Not the whole program.

**Why it works:** one exact number carries the whole post (Layer 4.1); everything after it is reasoning, not more stats. The both-things-are-true move ("do these drugs work? They do.") concedes in two words before the pivot (Layer 3.4). The colon delivers both reveals (Layer 1.3). Note the caveat: this is the one canon entry using the pre-Humanize-Check "nobody's talking about" opener shape; the number earned it. Do not copy the opener; copy the number-then-reasoning architecture.

### Entry 3 — The shocking-structural reveal (offshore rebate aggregators, ~17.8K fully organic)
**Source:** `newsletters/week_21_quarterly_reporting.md` (Post 75)

> Your PBM passes through 100% of manufacturer rebates. The rebate aggregator does not have to.
>
> Most PBM contracts I review do not name the rebate aggregator.
>
> Here is the structure. Manufacturer pays a rebate. The rebate is invoiced through a Group Purchasing Organization, not the PBM directly. The GPO is a separately incorporated entity, often offshore, with a small employee count. The GPO retains a portion of the rebate as a service fee. What the GPO forwards to the PBM is what the PBM "passes through" to your plan.
>
> Three PBMs. Three GPOs. Each PBM's aggregator shares its corporate parent.
>
> CVS Caremark uses Zinc Health Services. Both under CVS Health.
>
> Express Scripts uses Ascent Health Services. Both under Cigna. Ascent is registered in Switzerland.
>
> OptumRx uses Emisar Pharma Services. Both under UnitedHealth Group. Emisar is registered in Ireland.
>
> I review hundreds of PBM contracts a year at PBS. The rebate pass-through clause almost always defines pass-through as a percentage of what the PBM receives. Not as a percentage of what the manufacturer paid. The difference between those two definitions is where the aggregator's fee lives.
>
> Same rebate dollar from the manufacturer. The aggregator keeps a cut. The PBM passes through what is left.
>
> Ask for the GPO's role in your contract. Ask where the aggregator's compensation is disclosed. Save this for your next renewal.

**Why it works:** the hook is a two-sentence contradiction with no adjectives; the reader's "wait, what?" does the work. "Here is the structure." then a teacherly walk in short declaratives (glossing GPO in stride, Layer 2.4). Named companies, named registries, named countries: unfakeable specificity (Layer 4.2). The proprietary anchor sits two-thirds down, exactly where a cold reader needs credibility (Beat 3). The payload is a definitional distinction ("what the PBM receives" vs "what the manufacturer paid"): the words-versus-money test in its purest form (Layer 3.2). Closes with two askable questions and a save prompt. (Note: this pre-dates the spell-out rule; today "at PBS" becomes "at Prescription Benefit Solutions.")

### Entry 4 — Thursday Excellence audit voice (W18 Channel Pricing)
**Source:** `newsletters/week_18_drug_pipeline.md` (Post 63)

> Same drug. Same dose. Same manufacturer. Three channels. Three different prices — set by the same PBM.
>
> We see this on every channel-pricing audit we run. The retail price, the mail-order price, and the specialty-channel price for the same drug rarely match. The gap is not a bug; it is the design.
>
> What plan sponsors miss most often: the per-channel prices use different methodologies. Retail uses one AWP discount and dispensing fee. Mail uses a different discount, a different dispensing fee. Specialty uses a different methodology entirely, with different guarantees, often excluded from the contract's overall pricing performance language.
>
> Three channels. Three pricing structures. One PBM setting every number. And the spread is rarely on the standard quarterly report.
>
> The single data request that makes the channel design visible: net cost per script, by channel, for your top 10 drugs by spend. If the PBM cannot produce it, that is a finding before any negotiation begins.

**Why it works:** (Verbatim note: the hook's em dash is a pre-rule artifact from April 2026; today's rule renders it "Three different prices, set by the same PBM." Do not imitate the dash.) One formula (Same X/Same Y, Humanize Check rule b), never stacked; the body under it is narrative audit observation. "The gap is not a bug; it is the design." is the signature calm escalation in semicolon form. The close hands the reader one specific, doable data request and reframes refusal as evidence ("that is a finding"): plan-sponsor-actionable without homework scolding (Layer 3.5). Anchor is behavioral, not numeric: "every channel-pricing audit we run."

### Entry 5 — The definitional-leak reveal (W26 manufacturer payments, 5,502 organic)
**Source:** `newsletters/week_26_formulary_management.md` (Post 95)

> Your PBM passes through 100% of rebates. Then it collects five other payments from drug manufacturers it never calls rebates, so the pass-through guarantee never touches them.
>
> This is the part most plan sponsors have never seen. The leak is not in the guarantee. It is in the definition of the word "rebate." A loose definition counts only the payment a manufacturer literally labels a rebate, which leaves four other categories of manufacturer money free to flow to the PBM under different names.
>
> We review hundreds of PBM contracts a year at Prescription Benefit Solutions. These line items hide outside the rebate definition on most of them. Same manufacturer money, tied to your plan's drugs, sitting one defined word away from your report.

...and the close:

> Your contract still says 100%. 100% of whatever is left after the PBM paid itself first.
>
> Read your rebate definition, not your rebate guarantee. The gap between them is the money you never see.
>
> Save this before your next rebate-guarantee conversation.

**Why it works:** the same reveal architecture as Entry 3 pointed at a different mechanism, proving the shape is reusable when the fact is genuinely unknown. "One defined word away from your report" is the kind of compressed image Ginny lands after the plain explanation, never instead of it. The echo close ("100%... 100% of whatever is left") is her version of a callback: repetition with a twist, not a parallel-punch run.

### Entry 7 — The deep-dive opening (W20 Manufacturer Programs, the 145-view record)
**Source:** `newsletters/week_20_manufacturer_programs.md` (Article Body)

> Free drug. Full price claim. Your plan pays.
>
> That is how copay cards work. The manufacturer funds the card. The member pays nothing at the counter. The pharmacy submits a full-price claim. Your plan covers its share based on list price. The drug was never free. The cost just moved: from member to plan, from visible to invisible.
>
> In the early days of Prescription Benefit Solutions I walked a client through this flow. They had flagged a specialty drug generating significant plan spend, but the members on it were not complaining because the drug appeared "affordable" to them. Zero out of pocket, every fill. We pulled the claims and traced what was actually happening.

**Why it works:** the Substack register shift (Layer 5): the three-beat compression opens, then paragraphs of connected reasoning, then the first-person scene ("I walked a client through this flow") that LinkedIn copy never has room for. "The drug was never free. The cost just moved: from member to plan, from visible to invisible." is thesis-early, colon-delivered, and the whole article in one line. Note "significant plan spend" where a lesser draft would invent a dollar figure (Layer 4.1).

### Entry 8 — The Field Note voice (W18 "What We See When We Audit Channel Pricing")
**Source:** `newsletters/week_18_drug_pipeline.md` (PART 1B)

> Tuesday's LinkedIn carousel walked through GLP-1 coverage. Today's field note is about something quieter and, for most plans, more expensive: the same drug, on the same plan, billed at three different per-unit prices because it goes through three different channels.
>
> We run channel pricing audits as a standard part of our work at Prescription Benefit Solutions. The pattern is the same on almost every audit. Pull the top 10 drugs by spend. Check what the plan paid per unit through retail, mail-order, and specialty in the same month. The retail price, the mail-order price, and the specialty price rarely match. The gap is not a bug. It is how the contract was designed.

**Why it works:** the Field Note register: "we have been doing this work this week" practicality, imperative verbs walking the reader through the actual audit steps (Pull. Check.), no hook theatrics. "Quieter and, for most plans, more expensive" is understatement as escalation. Same closing construction as Entry 4 across a different channel: consistency of construction, not copy-paste of words.

### Entry 9 — Ginny's own hand: the Potter finals (the ground truth)
**Source:** `wendell_potter_editorial_style_analysis.md` (her published copy vs the drafts; the full diff is the file)

Her kept-verbatim lines:

> "Every PBM contract I audit has five revenue streams running through it"
>
> "That is the modern Pharmacy Benefit Manager (PBM) business model. It is not a metaphor stretched. It is the contract."

The decisive before/after (the fragmented-reform run):

> **Draft (engineered parallelism):** "Ban spread, and the margin moves into rebate retention. Guarantee rebate pass-through, and it moves into manufacturer-direct payments. Cap admin fees, and it moves into owned-pharmacy routing."
>
> **Ginny's final:** "For example, if spread pricing is banned, the margin will just move into rebate retention. Or if a requirement is made to pass-through all rebates, the revenue is rerouted to manufacturer-direct payments that are not classified as rebates. Each well intended reform risks moving the revenue margin around rather than reducing or eliminating it altogether."

And the admin-fees pair:

> **Draft:** "Per-claim, per-member-per-month… Five definitions the PBM wrote."
>
> **Ginny's final:** "They show up as per-claim, per employee per month, or per member per month charges, and fees for formulary management, clinical programs, and network access. The definitions behind them are all written by the PBM."

**Why this entry matters most:** it is the only place we can watch Ginny edit. Every edit moves the same direction: away from engineered rhythm and fragment-drama, toward complete sentences that teach ("For example, if..."), with the punch landing as a plain summary sentence at the end. When a drafting model is unsure whether a passage is too composed, this entry is the tiebreaker: if it reads like the drafts here, rewrite it toward the finals.

### Entry 10 — The X compression (DIR fees)
**Source:** `newsletters/week_21_quarterly_reporting.md` PART 4B (variant with "the pause" close in `week_23_mid_year_claims.md`)

> DIR fees are pharmacy's best-kept secret. A PBM claws money back from the pharmacy weeks after the prescription is filled. Retroactively. The plan never sees it. The pharmacy absorbs it. The PBM keeps it. Ask your PBM what it collected in DIR fees last year, and whether any of it flowed back to your plan.

(W23 variant closes: *"The pause before the answer is the answer."*)

**Why it works:** the X delta (Layer 5): half the words, more teeth, same anchor logic. One-word sentence ("Retroactively.") used once, for the mechanism's most damning property, not as a style tic. Three-beat actor accounting (plan/pharmacy/PBM) in six words each. Ends on the askable question. The W23 close shows her aphorism form: earned by the setup, never free-floating.

### Entry 11 — The consensus-entry POV (PA "everyone agrees is broken," 22,289, Cuban comment)
**Source:** `newsletters/week_15_340b_program_impact.md` (Strong POV Text)

> There is a process in pharmacy benefits that everyone agrees is broken.
>
> Prior authorization.
>
> Providers hate it. Patients hate it. The way most plans run it, I understand why.
>
> PA was designed to ensure appropriate care. It has become a tool for delay, a tool for routing, and in too many cases, a tool for denying access to medications patients need.
>
> But here is the part nobody says out loud: your PBM may not be incentivized to fix it. The friction serves a purpose. It shifts utilization. It reduces dispensing. It protects margins.
>
> Gold card programs exist. Smarter criteria exist. Better outcomes exist. Most plans have not asked for them.
>
> More Monday.

**Why it works:** the soft-consensus entry (everyone agrees) that pivots to the structural claim nobody makes (the incentive). "I understand why" is the clinician's empathy beat, one line, then back to mechanism. "May not be incentivized" is precise hedging: she asserts the structure, hedges the intent (Layer 4.3). The two-word close ("More Monday.") teases without a link, the Friday-tease register.

### Entry 12 — The amplifier reply (Cuban launch-day repost, ~25 seconds to repost)
**Source:** `newsletters/week_21_quarterly_reporting.md` PART 4B reaction log

> Exactly. The three biggest PBMs are owned by the three biggest insurers. We review hundreds of their contracts a year and the self-dealing is right there in the language: your plan pays the PBM, the PBM sends the script to its own specialty pharmacy, and the markup never leaves the building. That's not a market. It's a closed loop with a contract stapled to it.

**Why it works:** note the reply-rule nuance: it opens with one-word agreement ("Exactly.") and then is 100% addition. It passes the delete test (cover "Exactly." and everything remaining is net new) — this is the legal version of agreement, versus the banned validate-then-echo paragraph. The anchor, the money route in one colon-driven sentence, and a closing image that compresses the whole argument. This is reply move 1 (go deeper) executed at amplifier speed.

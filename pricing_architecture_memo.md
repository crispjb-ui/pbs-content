# Pricing Architecture Memo — options for the product ladder (decision-ready)

**Status:** for Ginny (Decision #13; a call, not a checkbox). This memo does not know PBS's current fees; it defines the ARCHITECTURE (how pricing is structured and presented). Ginny sets the numbers into whichever structure she picks. Includes the publish-ready "How we charge" page copy with bracketed slots.

## The strategic frame

PBS sells against two anchors: conflicted competitors whose "free or cheap review" is subsidized by placement income, and the client's fear of open-ended consulting fees. The architecture should therefore do three jobs: (1) make independence legible in the price itself (we charge you because no one else pays us — the fee IS the independence), (2) kill fee anxiety with fixed scopes, (3) build the recurring rung (oversight) so revenue compounds.

## Option A — Value-anchored fixed fee (single price per engagement type)

One price per product (Renewal Second Opinion / Contract Review / PBR), stated openly, anchored to the findings record.
**For:** simplest to sell and publish; the transparency of a posted price is on-brand to the point of being marketing. **Against:** one price misfits both a 600-life credit union and a 9,000-life system; leaves money on large plans or overprices small ones.

## Option B — Tiered fixed fees by plan size (RECOMMENDED)

Each product carries three posted tiers by covered lives (e.g., <1,000 / 1,000-4,999 / 5,000+). Scope identical; the tiers track data volume and stakes.
**For:** keeps fixed-fee certainty AND fits the market's real spread; tiers map cleanly to the funnel's existing size bands (the scoring already segments this way); publishing tier logic (even as "starting at") reads as radical transparency in a category that hides fees. **Against:** three numbers to defend instead of one; occasional edge-case negotiation at boundaries (solve: the band is set by the claims file, not negotiation).

## Option C — Retainer-plus-project hybrid

Projects priced per B, plus the **PBM Oversight retainer** (the productized twice-yearly PBR from growth plan §5.1): an annual fee covering the two reviews, quarterly reporting reads, renewal-window watch, and standing committee access.
**For:** this is where firm value compounds (recurring, sticky, fiduciary-cadenced). **Against:** premature as the LEAD offer before the ladder's lower rungs are proven at volume.

**Recommendation: B now, C layered in Q1 2027** as the post-engagement upgrade path ("most clients move into ongoing oversight after the first review"), priced so the retainer is obviously better than buying two reviews à la carte. A is a worthy fallback if three tiers feel heavy.

## How the guarantee interacts (if approved)

The guarantee (see `findings_guarantee_design.md`) attaches to Contract Review / PBR tiers only, never the Renewal Second Opinion. In presentation, price and guarantee always travel together: the fee states confidence, the guarantee proves it.

## Presentation rules (any option)

- Price is stated in ranges publicly, exactly on the call, never apologized for. The anchor sentence precedes the number: *"across 132 reviews our average identified savings ran several hundred thousand dollars per client."*
- Never hourly. Hours price effort; PBS sells findings and the File.
- The independence line always rides with the fee: *we take no money from PBMs, so the fee is the entirety of our compensation, and you can see all of it.*

# How we charge, and why we publish it

Most firms in pharmacy benefits do not publish how they are paid. There is usually a reason: many are compensated, at least in part, by the vendors involved in the outcome. Prescription Benefit Solutions works differently, and our pricing is built to make that visible.

**We take no money from PBMs.** No consulting fees, no data fees, no participation in rebate economics. Our fee from you is the entirety of our compensation for your engagement, and you will see all of it in writing before we begin.

**We charge fixed fees, set by the size of your plan.** A pharmacy benefit review is a defined piece of work: the contract, the claims, the findings, the Fiduciary File. You should know the cost before you say yes, and it should not change because the work got interesting. Engagements start at [$X] for plans under [1,000] lives, [$Y] through [4,999] lives, and [$Z] above that; the exact figure is confirmed in your proposal.

**Some context for the number.** Across 132 pharmacy benefit reviews, our average identified savings ran several hundred thousand dollars per client. We publish that so the fee conversation can be an honest one: this work reliably finds multiples of what it costs. **What happens after a review.** Most plans move into ongoing oversight: reviews on a set cadence, quarterly reporting reads, and a watch on every renewal window, so the committee's Fiduciary File stays current without anyone reconstructing diligence from memory. Ask us about oversight pricing when you talk with our team.

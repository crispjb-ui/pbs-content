### What Worked: Clear Patterns

**1. Confrontational hooks outperform informational by 10-100x.**
This is the single most important thing I have learned. "GLP-1 Reality Check" (confrontational text): 41,594 impressions. "Data without questions is just numbers" (informational text): 499 impressions. Same format, same day slot, 83x difference. Every top performer names an adversary, creates a knowledge gap, or provokes discomfort. The audience does not want to be educated. They want to be told what is being done to them.

**2. Messy infographic is the breakout visual format.**
"PBM contracts have their own language" hit 22,047 impressions in 2 days, making it the #3 all-time post. I did not expect the informal whiteboard style to outperform polished carousels, but the data is overwhelming. Imperfect signals authentic. We should use this format every other week minimum.

**3. PBM Contract Insights is the dominant pillar.**
8 posts, 82,517 total impressions, 10,315 average. Nearly 2x the next closest pillar. This audience has an appetite for content that reveals hidden contract economics. When I share what I see in the approximately 100 contracts we review annually, the engagement follows.

**4. Short, repetitive hook structures drive saves.**
"Same drug. Same dose. Same member." got 12 saves in 1 day. The cadence works for bookmarking. Saves correlate with educational depth: checklists, frameworks, comparisons.

**5. LinkedIn Newsletter is the primary conversion engine.**
605 subscribers, 49% open rate (climbing from 44%), 468 email sends on our best edition. Newsletter subscriber-to-follower ratio: 44%. This channel is doing the heaviest audience-building work. I need to protect it.

**6. Mark Cuban amplification proved delayed viral potential.**
The white bagging post went from 373 impressions (2 days) to 19,507 (1 week) after Cuban reposted it. Posts built on substance can go viral on a delayed timeline when the right amplifier picks them up. We cannot plan for this, but we can keep writing posts worth amplifying.

**7. Tuesday visual MUST differ from Monday newsletter topic.**
Same-topic Tuesday visual: 623 impressions. Different-topic Tuesday visual: 22,047 impressions. Algorithm cannibalization is real and devastating. I learned this the hard way in Week 10.

### What Didn't Work: Honest Assessment

**1. Informational/educational hooks consistently underperform.**
Posts framed as "here is useful information" average 183-499 impressions. Posts framed as "here is what is being done to you" average 1,993-41,594. I keep learning this lesson. Helpful does not drive engagement. Confrontational does.

**2. Polls are dead.**
3 polls averaging 300 impressions, 0 meaningful engagement. We discontinued these in Q2. Not coming back.

**3. Thursday carousels have been inconsistent.**
When placed as the weekly theme visual (3 days after the newsletter), they perform moderately. But carousel format alone does not guarantee reach. The hook still matters more than the format. A boring carousel is still boring.

**4. Substack growth is slow relative to the LinkedIn Newsletter.**
24 subscribers vs. 653. The 60% open rate is strong, but the base is tiny. Substack needs its own discovery engine through Notes, cross-recommendations, and SEO to scale independently of LinkedIn. I cannot keep relying on LinkedIn to do the work for both platforms.

**5. Some Substack articles read like longer LinkedIn newsletters, not genuinely different products.**
When I audited the Q2 articles, the exclusive tools are present but the differentiation varies. Weeks 16 (PA ROI scoring rubric) and 19 (fiduciary documentation framework) are strong: a reader could not replicate them from the LinkedIn version. Weeks 14 and 20 cover 70-80% of the same insight as the LinkedIn version, with the Substack adding a template overlay. That is not enough. Q3 must fix this. If someone could get the same value from both publications, there is no reason to subscribe to both.

**6. Friday tease hooks are uneven.**
Best performer: "Specialty pharmacy question most employers never ask" at 2,549 impressions. But most Friday teases land at 495-917. The hook quality varies too much. Every Friday tease needs the same confrontational edge as the Wednesday posts.

## Substack Differentiation Strategy: Q3 Elevation

### The Problem I Am Fixing

In Q2, some of our Substack articles were essentially longer versions of the LinkedIn newsletter with a template tacked on at the end. The LinkedIn newsletter covered 70-80% of the insight. That is not a compelling reason for anyone to subscribe to a second publication. I would not subscribe to that. Neither will anyone else.

### The Q3 Standard

**LinkedIn Newsletter = the problem framed.** It names the issue, explains why it matters, and creates urgency. It ends with: "The [specific tool] for solving this is exclusive to the Substack."

**Substack = the solution methodology.** It does not just go deeper on the same narrative. It provides a different analytical framework: proprietary scoring models, comparison libraries, calculation tools, or decision trees that a reader cannot replicate from the LinkedIn version alone. If someone can read the LinkedIn newsletter and approximate the Substack tool, we have failed.

### Three Tiers of Substack-Exclusive Content (Q3)

**Tier 1: Scoring Rubrics with Thresholds**
Numbered evaluation frameworks where each factor has specific benchmarks (green/yellow/red). The reader inputs their own data and gets a measurable score. Examples: PBM evaluation scorecard, renewal readiness assessment, clinical program ROI calculator.

**Tier 2: Side-by-Side Comparison Libraries**
Multi-column tables comparing contract language, pricing models, program structures, or vendor approaches. The reader uses these as reference documents during negotiations. Examples: contract language library (weak vs. strong provisions), rebate model comparison, network design options.

**Tier 3: Decision Trees with Scenario Analysis**
"If X, then Y" frameworks where the reader follows their specific situation to a recommended action. Examples: carve-out decision tree, PBM switch vs. renegotiate framework, formulary strategy by population profile.

## Carousel Redesign: Retiring the Corporate Template

### Why the Current Carousels Underperform

When we strip out the Mark Cuban-amplified posts (Rebate Guide, 5 PBM Reporting Questions), organic carousel performance averages ~2,366 impressions. The messy infographic hit 22,047 with zero external help. The polished corporate carousel template is not working.

The problem is not the carousel format itself. Carousels still drive the highest save rates and follower conversions. The problem is that our carousels look like every other corporate LinkedIn carousel: clean blue backgrounds, Krona One headers, bullet points, PBS logo. They do not stop the scroll. They do not signal "this is different." They look like a pitch deck, and nobody stops scrolling for a pitch deck.

The messy infographic works because it looks like someone grabbed a marker and drew on a whiteboard. It signals: a real person made this, not a design team. That same principle should drive our carousel redesign.

### Three New Carousel Templates for Q3

**Template A: "The Whiteboard" (Messy Carousel)**

The carousel version of our best-performing visual format. Each slide looks hand-drawn: marker text, circled numbers, arrows, underlines, crossed-out words. Not actually hand-drawn (produced in Canva) but designed to look raw and imperfect.

| Element | Spec |
|---------|------|
| Background | Off-white (#F5F5F0) with faint grid lines, like graph paper |
| Text Style | Handwritten font (Caveat or Patrick Hand on Canva) in Primary Blue (#015880) |
| Accent Marks | Hand-drawn circles in Accent Blue (#A7E0FA) around key numbers. Use Primary Blue (#015880) for underlines. |
| Arrows | Rough arrows in Primary Blue (#015880) pointing between concepts, not clean vector arrows |
| Annotations | Margin-note style callouts in Gray (#4D4D4D): "← this is the problem" or "most employers miss this" |
| Highlight | Light Accent Blue (#A7E0FA at 30% opacity) as background highlight behind key phrases |
| Dimensions | 1080 x 1350 px (vertical) |
| Slide Count | 5-7 slides |
| Footer | Small "Benefit Blind Spots" in Gray (#4D4D4D) handwritten-style text, bottom-left. |
| PBS Logo | Minimal. Small logo bottom-right, 50-60px. Does not dominate. |

**Best for:** Exposé content, "what your PBM does not want you to see," contract reveals, industry mechanics explained.

**Slide 1 formula:** Large handwritten question or provocative statement. One circled number or crossed-out word. Must create curiosity gap.

**Example (Week 29 - Rebate Economics):**
- Slide 1: "Your PBM reports $2M in rebates." Below, in red: "Your net cost went up." Circled: "Both are true."
- Slide 2: Arrow from "Manufacturer" → "PBM" → "Your Plan" with dollar amounts shrinking at each step. Red circle around the shrinkage.
- Slide 3: "Pass-through" vs. "Traditional" with crossed-out words showing what each term actually means
- Slide 4: "The number that matters" with one large stat: rebate per claim, not total rebates
- Slide 5: "What to ask your PBM" with three handwritten questions, checkboxes empty
- Slide 6: "What the answer reveals" with two paths: good answer (green check) vs. red flag answer (red X)
- Slide 7: "Run this comparison before renewal." Small CTA. team@rxbs.org

### GAPS: Changes I Recommend for Q3

---

**GAP 1: POSTING TIME — 8:30 AM vs. 10:00-11:00 AM**

Research says 10:00-11:00 AM in the audience's time zone is the consistent winner. Our feed posts go out at 8:30 AM EST.

**Why this matters:** The first 60-90 minutes determine ~70% of a post's total reach (the "golden hour"). At 8:30 AM, many of our audience (CFOs, HR directors at large employers) may not be on LinkedIn yet. A 10:00 AM post catches the mid-morning scroll.

**Recommendation:** Test shifting Tuesday and Wednesday posts to 10:00 AM EST for Weeks 26-28 (July). Compare impressions per post against the 8:30 AM baseline from Q2. If 10:00 AM outperforms, shift all feed posts. Keep Monday newsletters at 7:45 AM (email, not feed; different engagement pattern).

**Risk:** Our audience skews healthcare, which may start earlier. But the data across 4.8M posts is strong enough to test.

---

**GAP 2: TEXT POST LENGTH — Too Short**

Our Wednesday/Friday text posts are 65-95 words (~400-550 characters). Research says the sweet spot is 1,800-2,000 characters (~300-350 words).

**Why this matters:** LinkedIn's primary algorithm signal is now **Dwell Time** (how long someone spends on your post before scrolling). Longer text = more reading time = higher dwell time score. A 65-word post can be read in 15 seconds. A 300-word post takes 60-90 seconds. That is a 4-6x difference in the signal the algorithm cares most about.

**The counterargument:** Our confrontational hooks have performed well at short length (GLP-1 at 41K, white bagging at 19.5K). But both of those had external amplification or viral hooks that transcended length. The organic average for short text is ~2,271 impressions. Longer text with the same hook quality could push that higher.

**Recommendation:** Expand Wednesday Strong POV posts to 200-300 words (1,200-1,800 characters). Keep the same confrontational hook in the first 210 characters (the "See More" cutoff). Add 2-3 supporting paragraphs after the fold that expand the argument with specific examples, data points, or "here is what I see in contracts" anecdotes. This gives the algorithm more dwell time while keeping the scroll-stopping hook.

Friday tease hooks can stay shorter (80-120 words) since their job is curiosity, not dwell time.

**Format for expanded Wednesday posts:**

```
[Hook: 1-2 lines, confrontational, under 210 characters]

[Line break]

[Expansion paragraph 1: specific example or "what I see in practice"]

[Line break]

[Expansion paragraph 2: the implication or "why this costs you money"]

[Line break]

[Close: 1 line, declarative, no CTA — let the hook do the work]
```

---

**GAP 3: PRE-POSTING ENGAGEMENT — Not in Our Workflow**

Research strongly recommends commenting on 10-20 posts from your target audience daily BEFORE you publish. The algorithm prioritizes showing your content to people whose posts you have recently engaged with.

**Why this matters:** This is essentially "warming up" the algorithm. When Ginny comments on a CFO's post at 9:30 AM, and then publishes her own post at 10:00 AM, LinkedIn is more likely to show her post to that CFO and their network. This is not an engagement pod (those are detected and penalized). This is authentic, one-on-one engagement that the algorithm rewards.

**Recommendation:** Add a daily pre-posting routine to the workflow:
- 9:00-9:30 AM (or 30 min before posting): Ginny engages with 5-10 posts from target audience members (CFOs, HR directors, brokers, benefits consultants, other pharmacy leaders)
- Comments must be substantive (not "great post!"). Add a specific pharmacy benefit insight the writer and their audience would not have.
- This is essentially the same skill as the Substack engagement strategy, applied to LinkedIn.

**Add to Publishing Checklist in every weekly bundle:**
- [ ] Pre-posting engagement: 5-10 substantive comments on target audience posts (30 min before scheduled post)

---

**GAP 4: REPLY STRATEGY — Too Slow, Missing the Follow-Up**

Our checklist says "Respond to comments within 2 hours during business hours." Research says the first 30 minutes after posting are critical, and the most powerful tactic is asking a follow-up question to generate a second comment from each person.

**Why this matters:** Comments are weighted 2x over likes. Multi-reply threads with back-and-forth discussion get 5.2x more amplification than posts without conversation depth. Every comment that generates a reply creates a thread, and threads signal "this post is producing real conversation" to the algorithm.

**Recommendation:** Change the workflow:
- First 30 minutes after posting: Ginny responds to EVERY comment
- Every reply should include a follow-up question: "That is a great point. What have you seen work for plans your size?" or "Exactly. Has your broker raised this at renewal yet?"
- Goal: turn every comment into a 2-3 message thread

**Update in checklist:**
- [ ] Post-publish: Stay on LinkedIn for 30 minutes. Reply to every comment with a follow-up question.

---

**GAP 5: VIDEO STRATEGY — Temper Expectations**

Our Q3 plan includes 3 video tests in July. The research shows video views declining 36% year-over-year on LinkedIn, though engagement per view is up 7%.

**What this means:** Video is not the algorithm favorite it once was. Document/carousel posts now outperform video by 278% on engagement. However, video still builds personal brand recognition in a way text and images cannot. A face attached to the expertise matters for trust.

**Recommendation:** Keep the 3 video tests but adjust expectations:
- Do not expect video to outperform text posts on impressions
- Measure video on brand building metrics: profile views, follower conversion rate, newsletter signups
- If video drives more profile views and newsletter subscribes per impression (even at lower total impressions), it is working
- Optimal length confirmed: 30-90 seconds, vertical (9:16 or 4:5), captions mandatory
- Always post the full text version as the caption so the algorithm can index the text content

**Do NOT replace the Wednesday text post with video.** Instead, test video as an additional post (perhaps Tuesday evening or an experimental time slot) or alternate weeks.

---

**GAP 6: SUBSTACK RECOMMENDATION NETWORK — Not Built Yet**

Research shows 40% of all new Substack subscriptions come from within the platform's recommendation network. Substack-sourced subscribers convert to paid at 4x the rate of external traffic. We have no formal recommendation partnerships.

**Why this matters:** At 24 subscribers, organic Notes growth is important but slow. A mutual recommendation from a publication with 500+ subscribers in an adjacent niche can deliver 5-20 new subscribers in a single week. A network of 10-15 such relationships compounds.

**Recommendation:** Build a target list of 10-15 Substack publications for mutual recommendation partnerships. Ginny should:
1. Identify publications covering: self-funded employer strategy, employee benefits, healthcare costs/transparency, HR leadership, health policy, insurance/risk management, CFO content
2. Subscribe to each. Read their last 3 issues.
3. Engage with their Notes substantively for 2-3 weeks (build the relationship before asking)
4. Propose mutual recommendations: "I recommend your publication to my subscribers, you recommend mine"

This should be a Q3 project goal alongside the content calendar.

**Add to Substack engagement guide:** Build and maintain a recommendation network of 10-15 adjacent publications.

---

**GAP 7: CAROUSEL DESIGN — Validated by Research**

Research confirms: 10-20 words per slide maximum. Text-oriented carousels outperform image-oriented. Dwell time on a 10-slide carousel (30-60 seconds) is a strong algorithm signal.

Our carousel redesign (Whiteboard, Contract Markup, One Number templates) is well-aligned with this data. The "One Number" template naturally stays under 10 words per slide. The "Whiteboard" template uses handwritten-style text that reads fast.

**One additional finding:** Carousels should ideally be 6-10 slides. Our standard is 7 slides, which fits the sweet spot. Some research suggests below-35% click-through rate (swipe rate) triggers a visibility penalty. This means every slide must earn the next swipe. Weak slides in the middle kill distribution.

**Recommendation:** Apply a "swipe test" to every carousel: Would someone swipe from this slide to the next? If any slide does not earn the swipe, cut it or strengthen it. Five strong slides beat seven with two weak ones in the middle.

---

**GAP 8: SUBSTACK SEO — Underutilized**

Substack has strong domain authority on Google. Our articles could rank for search terms like "PBM contract audit checklist," "biosimilar readiness assessment," or "pharmacy benefit renewal preparation." We are not optimizing for this.

**Recommendation:**
- Edit URL slugs on every article (Substack's SEO Options) to short, keyword-rich slugs
- Write subtitles as search-optimized meta descriptions (40-60 character titles get 33% higher CTR)
- Structure articles with clear H2 headers that match search queries
- The exclusive tools (scorecards, checklists, comparison libraries) are exactly the type of content AI search engines (ChatGPT, Perplexity, Claude) surface. Authoritative, niche, structured content benefits most from AI search.

**Add to Article Meta section in every weekly bundle:** SEO-optimized URL slug.

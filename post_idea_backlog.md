## PI-01: Five contract-side levers on GLP-1 spend that come before carving it out of the plan

**Source observation:** Mike Athas (broker-channel-class LinkedIn voice) ran a sales pitch for supplemental health benefits as a workaround to keep GLP-1 utilization out of the major medical plan and therefore out of next year's renewal premium math. The pitch is a real product that solves a real problem (renewal-pressure reduction) but does not reduce total plan exposure — it reclassifies it. The contract-side levers on the GLP-1 spend itself go unaddressed.

**Why PBS should write this:** Carve-out workarounds are being pitched to PBS's target audience (CFOs, HR Directors, benefits committees) as the answer to GLP-1 cost pressure. Plan sponsors who adopt them without first pulling the contract-side levers may end up with reclassified-but-undiminished exposure plus ERISA/stop-loss/wraparound complications. PBS has unique authority to publish the "before you carve it out" framework because PBS reviews ~100 PBM contracts annually and sees the levers most carve-out vendors do not touch.

**Hook candidate (Wednesday text post):**

> Plan sponsors are getting pitched supplemental GLP-1 carve-outs as the answer to renewal pressure. Carve-outs reclassify the cost. They do not reduce it. The contract-side levers on the GLP-1 spend itself usually come first.

**Five levers (body content, in approximate order):**

1. **Rebate eligibility tied to dosing volumes.** Standard manufacturer rebate agreements assume FDA-approved dosing volumes. Patients on titration schedules, off-label microdosing, or extended-interval maintenance can fall outside the rebate flow even while paying a smaller per-fill price. Net cost can move in either direction depending on contract math.
2. **Channel routing on the same drug.** Mail-order vs retail vs specialty pricing on the same NDC can differ by 30%+. Many contracts default-route GLP-1s through whichever channel maximizes PBM spread, not which one minimizes plan cost.
3. **PA criteria specificity.** Loose PA criteria ("BMI ≥ 30") let almost everyone through; tight PA criteria with documentation requirements both reduce inappropriate utilization and create renewal-eligibility gates that some PBM contracts also tighten on the rebate side.
4. **Formulary placement vs lower-cost alternative.** When generic semaglutide arrives (post-patent or via alternative pathway), formulary placement of brand vs generic determines whether the plan captures the savings or the PBM does via formulary tier economics.
5. **Rebate flow definition for the GLP-1 drug class specifically.** Many contracts use broad rebate definitions ("rebates and manufacturer payments") that get qualified at the drug-class level. GLP-1-specific rebate flow language in the contract determines whether manufacturer payments on GLP-1s aggregate with other rebates (plan loses visibility) or flow class-specifically (plan can audit).

**Close candidate:**

> A carve-out solves the renewal-impact problem. The contract levers solve the cost problem. Both conversations are usually needed, in that order.

**Format recommendation:** Wednesday confrontational text post is the primary candidate (confrontational hook test passes: names the carve-out-vendor framing as the adversary frame, creates a knowledge gap with the five levers most plan sponsors haven't been told about, plan-sponsor-actionable). Thursday messy infographic Slot A is the secondary candidate if the slot needs a Cost Containment / PBM Contract Insights pillar play. Either format can pair with a Field Note titled something like *"Pull These Five Levers Before You Carve GLP-1 Out of Your Plan"* for first-comment cross-promo per Thursday Excellence Rule.

**Proprietary anchor to thread through the post:** "In the contracts we review each year, four of these five levers are addressable in renewal negotiations. Plan sponsors who pull them before adopting a carve-out usually find the carve-out isn't necessary."

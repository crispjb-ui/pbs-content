## The Specialty Pharmacy Decision Your PBM Made For You

**Publication:** Benefit Blind Spots
**Publish:** Monday, March 30, 2026 at 7:30 AM EST
**Length:** ~1,500 words (~6 min read)
**Tone:** Analytical authority
**Pillar:** Cost Containment

---

### Article Body

Your specialty pharmacy was probably chosen for you.

I reviewed a contract last quarter where the employer had no idea their specialty pharmacy was a PBM-owned operation. They assumed it was an independent selection based on clinical quality. It was not. When a self-funded employer signs a PBM contract, specialty pharmacy routing is typically predetermined. The PBM owns or has exclusive arrangements with specific specialty pharmacies. Your members get directed there automatically.

This is not necessarily a problem. But it becomes one when employers assume the routing decision was made in their interest rather than the PBM's.

This issue breaks down how specialty pharmacy selection works, what employers should evaluate, and where the hidden cost variables actually live.

---

**How Specialty Routing Works**

Most PBM contracts include mandatory specialty pharmacy provisions. When a member fills a specialty medication, the claim routes to a designated specialty pharmacy, typically one owned by or affiliated with the PBM.

The three major PBMs all own specialty pharmacy operations:
- CVS Caremark owns CVS Specialty
- Express Scripts owns Accredo
- OptumRx owns OptumRx Specialty (formerly BriovaRx)

This vertical integration creates efficiencies. It also creates conflicts. The PBM profits from both the administrative services and the pharmacy dispensing margin. The question for plan sponsors is whether those profits align with your cost optimization.

---

**What Employers Often Miss**

In our contract reviews, we see several variables that employers frequently overlook:

*Dispensing Fees vs. Drug Cost*

Specialty pharmacy contracts typically include dispensing fees separate from drug acquisition cost. These fees vary significantly. Some contracts bury dispensing fees in ways that make comparison difficult.

When evaluating specialty pharmacy performance, separate the drug cost from the dispensing cost. A pharmacy offering lower drug prices may charge higher dispensing fees, or vice versa.

*Limited Distribution Drug Access*

Certain specialty medications are available only through specific pharmacies. Manufacturers restrict distribution to maintain control over pricing, patient support, and data collection.

Your specialty pharmacy's access to limited distribution drugs matters. If your PBM's specialty pharmacy lacks access to a critical medication, members face delays or alternative routing that may increase cost.

*Clinical Services Bundling*

Specialty pharmacies bundle clinical services: patient education, adherence monitoring, outcomes tracking. The value of these services varies considerably.

Some clinical programs demonstrably improve outcomes and reduce total cost of care. Others are checkbox exercises that add cost without adding value. As a clinical pharmacist, I can tell you: evaluate clinical service quality, not just existence.

*White Bagging and Brown Bagging*

These terms describe alternative dispensing models for specialty drugs administered in clinical settings:

White bagging: Specialty pharmacy ships medication directly to the provider site for administration.

Brown bagging: Specialty pharmacy ships medication to the patient, who brings it to the provider site.

Both models can reduce cost compared to buy-and-bill (where the provider purchases and bills for the drug). But both create logistical complexity and potential waste if medications are not used.

The cost savings depend on your specific provider network, drug mix, and operational capacity.

---

**Evaluation Framework**

When we help employers assess specialty pharmacy arrangements, we walk through these areas:

*Cost Transparency*

Can you see actual acquisition cost, dispensing fees, and rebate passthrough separately? Or does your reporting bundle everything into a single line item?

Transparent specialty pharmacy arrangements allow employers to verify pricing against guarantees and benchmark against market rates.

*Network Flexibility*

Are you locked into a single specialty pharmacy, or do you have network options? Exclusive arrangements may offer better pricing but limit flexibility. Network options provide choice but may dilute volume-based discounts.

*Clinical Program Outcomes*

Does your specialty pharmacy report clinical outcomes, not just dispensing metrics? Adherence rates, therapy completion, and clinical interventions should be measurable and reported.

*Limited Distribution Coverage*

Review the limited distribution drug list relevant to your population. Confirm your specialty pharmacy has access to medications your members currently use or may need.

*Provider Integration*

How does your specialty pharmacy coordinate with prescribers and sites of care? Poor integration creates administrative burden for providers and delays for members.

---

**The White Bagging Question**

White bagging deserves its own discussion because it represents a significant cost opportunity for many employers.

When specialty drugs are administered in hospital outpatient or physician office settings, the traditional model is buy-and-bill: the provider purchases the drug, administers it, and bills the plan at a markup.

White bagging removes the provider markup by having the specialty pharmacy ship directly to the administration site. The plan pays pharmacy benefit rates rather than medical benefit rates.

The savings can be substantial, often 20-40% on high-cost specialty drugs.

But implementation is not simple:
- Providers may resist losing drug margin revenue
- Logistics require coordination between pharmacy and administration schedules
- Waste occurs when shipped medications are not used
- Some drugs have stability requirements that complicate shipping

If you are considering white bagging, evaluate the drug candidates carefully. High-volume, stable medications with predictable administration schedules are better candidates than low-volume drugs with variable dosing.

---

**Contract Language Considerations**

When we review PBM contracts for specialty pharmacy provisions, we focus on these areas:

*Mandatory vs. Preferred Routing*

Understand whether specialty routing is mandatory (members must use designated pharmacy) or preferred (incentives but not requirements). Mandatory arrangements concentrate volume but limit flexibility.

*Performance Guarantees*

What guarantees exist for specialty pharmacy performance? Discount guarantees, dispensing fee caps, and service level commitments should be explicit and auditable.

*Carve-Out Rights*

Can you carve out specialty pharmacy from your PBM arrangement? Some employers we work with achieve better results with independent specialty pharmacy contracts. Your PBM contract should address whether this is permitted and what impact it has on other contract terms.

*Audit Access*

Do your audit rights extend to specialty pharmacy operations? Specialty claims should be auditable with the same rigor as retail claims.

---

**The Position We Take**

Specialty pharmacy selection should be a deliberate employer decision, not a PBM default.

This is a Visibility Gap problem. The PBM bundles acquisition cost, dispensing fees, and rebates into a single line item. You see total spend. The PBM sees margin. The gap between those two views is where the money lives.

Employers who accept default routing without evaluation are trusting that PBM incentives align with plan interests. In our experience reviewing approximately 100 contracts a year, that trust is not always warranted.

We advise every employer we work with to treat specialty pharmacy as a distinct decision point:
- Evaluate cost transparency and reporting
- Assess clinical program quality
- Understand limited distribution access
- Consider white bagging where appropriate
- Ensure contract language protects your interests

The pharmacy that dispenses your most expensive medications should be chosen, not assigned.

## Biosimilar Substitution at Specialty Pharmacies: What's Changing

**Publication:** Benefit Blind Spots
**Publish:** Thursday, April 2, 2026 at 7:30 AM EST
**Length:** ~550 words (~3 min read)
**Tone:** Practical, tactical
**Pillar:** Clinical Pharmacy
**Type:** Field Note

---

### Field Note Body

We have been reviewing specialty pharmacy contracts more closely this quarter, and one pattern keeps coming up: biosimilar substitution policies that vary significantly depending on which specialty pharmacy is dispensing.

When a PBM mandates biosimilar substitution at its owned specialty pharmacy, the stated goal is cost savings. The biosimilar is less expensive than the reference biologic. The plan saves money. Straightforward.

But the details matter more than the mandate.

**What we are seeing in practice**

PBM-owned specialty pharmacies may implement biosimilar substitution protocols that prioritize products where the PBM has favorable rebate arrangements, not necessarily the lowest net cost option. The biosimilar your plan switches to may not be the one that produces the best financial outcome for you.

This distinction matters because biosimilar pricing varies. The list price discount relative to the reference product is one number. The net cost after rebates is a different number. And the net cost your plan actually realizes depends on your specific contract terms for biosimilar reimbursement.

**Three things to verify**

First, ask which biosimilars are on the substitution list and why. If your PBM's specialty pharmacy is substituting to a specific biosimilar across its book of business, understand whether that selection reflects plan-level economics or PBM-level economics. The answer is usually the latter.

Second, compare net cost before and after substitution. A biosimilar switch that reduces list price but changes your rebate position may not produce the savings you expect. Request claims-level detail showing actual reimbursement for both the reference product and the biosimilar.

*Illustrative example for educational purposes. Actual amounts vary by plan.* A reference biologic reimbursed at $5,000 per fill with a $1,500 rebate nets $3,500. A biosimilar reimbursed at $4,200 per fill with a $200 rebate nets $4,000. The "savings" went in the wrong direction.

Third, review the clinical transition protocol. As a pharmacist, this one matters to me. Biosimilar substitution for patients stable on a reference biologic requires clinical oversight. Confirm that your specialty pharmacy has a defined process for provider notification, patient communication, and clinical monitoring during transitions. Poorly managed switches create adherence disruptions that cost more than they save.

**The practical takeaway**

Biosimilar substitution is generally a positive development for plan cost management. I am a strong supporter of appropriate biosimilar use. But the execution details determine whether savings reach your plan or stay with the PBM. Do not assume that a biosimilar mandate automatically produces employer savings. Verify the net cost math, understand the product selection rationale, and confirm clinical transition protocols are in place.

The specialty pharmacy dispensing your most expensive medications should be transparent about how substitution decisions are made and who benefits from them.

### Rhode Island AG Pushes to Ban Spread Pricing Before His Term Ends

*Rhode Island Current | May 11, 2026*

Rhode Island Attorney General Peter Neronha is putting his last six months in office behind a quartet of healthcare bills, two of which target pharmacy benefit managers directly. The first, H8360 / S3260, would amend Rhode Island's deceptive trade practices law to define a list of PBM behaviors as unfair, including spread pricing, which is the practice of charging a plan one price for a drug while paying the pharmacy a different, lower price and keeping the difference. The second, H8382 / S3258, builds on a 2025 law Neronha used earlier this year against unannounced PBM audits of independent pharmacies; the new version requires written justification for any audit that bypasses the PBM's annual quota, bans common-owner audits, requires advance notice to the AG, and authorizes civil penalties of up to $10,000 per violation.

The detail worth flagging for plan sponsors is on the record at the House Committee on Corporations hearing on April 14. The Pharmaceutical Care Management Association (the trade association representing the major PBMs) submitted testimony defending spread pricing on the grounds that "many small and mid-sized employers select spread pricing because it gives them predictable, stable drug costs." The argument is that the PBM is "transferring risk" by holding the spread. When PBS reviews PBM contracts and the spread pricing language is in the definitions section, the actual mechanic is that the PBM charges the plan $80 to $100 for a drug it pays the pharmacy $15 to $25 for, and keeps the difference. That is not risk transfer in any direction a CFO would recognize. Watch the Rhode Island legislative session through June for movement on H8360 specifically; even if it does not pass this cycle, the on-record PCMA defense is the cleanest articulation of the industry's position you will find this year. Read it; bring it up at renewal.

### Pay-for-Delay Settlement Holds a $225K Heart Drug Off Generics Until 2031

*PCMA Blog | May 7, 2026*

PCMA flagged a fresh pay-for-delay settlement in which a brand manufacturer paid three generic makers to keep generic Vyndamax (tafamidis) off the market until mid-2031. The brand list price runs north of $225,000 per year. For self-funded plans covering members with transthyretin cardiomyopathy on tafamidis, the math is direct: five more years of brand-only pricing on a drug already concentrated in the high-cost specialty tier.

Pay-for-delay is also the cleanest reason to pressure-test the generic substitution language in your PBM contract. The clause is usually a one-liner buried in the definitions section. Ask your broker to confirm whether the contract obligates the PBM to substitute on day one of generic availability, or simply permits it.

### KFF: TrumpRx Discount Coupons Carry Hidden Cost for Insured Members

*KFF Health News | May 7, 2026*

KFF Health News looked at how the new TrumpRx program routes consumers to manufacturer discount coupons at the pharmacy counter. For insured members, those coupons can trigger copay accumulator or maximizer programs that pull the coupon value back out of the deductible math, surprising the member and exposing the plan when coupon funds run out mid-year.

If your PBM contract has accumulator or maximizer language, the counter conversation is one your members are about to have without your input. Get ahead of it with member communication, not complaint calls in June.

### A New Medicare Pathway Will Discount GLP-1s for Weight Loss

*KFF Health News | May 6, 2026*

KFF reported that a new Medicare pathway will expand access to discounted GLP-1s for weight loss for millions of older Americans, with bridge pricing that lands below current commercial rates. Medicare pricing does not bind commercial plans. It does shift the reference point your broker uses at the negotiation table. When Medicare gets a price your commercial plan does not, that is a number worth raising at your next renewal conversation.

### One More Worth Watching

Sanofi pulled its type 1 diabetes drug teplizumab from the FDA's new fast-review voucher program after a political appointee disagreed with agency staff over the approval, per STAT. Separate reporting this week suggests Commissioner Marty Makary may be on the way out. Regulatory volatility around specialty pipeline drugs is not a 2027 story; it lands inside the renewal cycle you are negotiating now.

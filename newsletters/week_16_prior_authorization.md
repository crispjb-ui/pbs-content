# PART 1: SUBSTACK ARTICLE

## The Prior Authorization Problem: Balancing Control and Care
**Subtitle:** How to evaluate whether your PA program delivers value or just creates barriers

**Publication:** Benefit Blind Spots
**Publish:** Monday, April 20, 2026 at 7:30 AM EST
**Length:** ~1,500 words (~6 min read)
**Tone:** Analytical authority
**Pillar:** Clinical Pharmacy

---

### Article Body

PA is the topic that gets the most emotional reaction in every meeting I attend. Not rebates. Not spread pricing. Prior authorization.

I sat across from an employer last month who told me about a member with a chronic autoimmune condition. She had been stable on the same medication for two years. The authorization expired. A renewal triggered a full clinical review. Eleven days later, she got approved.

She was always going to get approved. Nothing had changed. But she missed doses, her symptoms flared, and by the time the medication restarted, she had already called HR twice and her physician's office three times.

That is the PA problem in one story. The tool works when it is applied in the right places. In others, it creates friction that helps nobody. Most employers I talk to have never measured whether their PA program is delivering value that justifies the burden. They know they have PA. They do not know what it costs them. And this is not a knock on their advisory teams. The brokers and consultants we work with are often the first ones to flag these gaps once they see the data. The problem is that PBMs do not hand over PA outcome data in a format that makes analysis easy.

**Where PA actually works**

I am not anti-PA. High-cost specialty medications with wide variation in use benefit from a clinical checkpoint before dispensing. Drugs with black box warnings or abuse potential warrant that extra step. Step therapy that routes first-line treatment toward clinically equivalent, lower-cost alternatives can save real money without compromising care.

Those are the cases where PA earns its cost. The friction changes outcomes.

The problem is that most PA programs go well beyond those applications.

**Where PA does damage**

This is where I get frustrated. PA becomes a net negative when criteria have not kept up with current clinical practice. When renewals for stable chronic therapy add paperwork without changing a single outcome. When the process delays time-sensitive medications that should have been approved on day one.

I understand why it happens. PA criteria get set, and nobody revisits them. A drug that needed close scrutiny five years ago is now standard of care. But the PA requirement is still there, generating denials and appeals for a medication that gets approved almost every time.

Physicians and their staff spend hours every week on PA: documentation, submission, follow-up, appeals. That pulls resources from patient care. Patients experience delays. Some abandon therapy entirely. I have seen it happen. And employers bear the administrative cost through PBM fees, appeals processing, and utilization management charges that may exceed whatever savings the PA generated.

Your PBM may not be incentivized to fix this. The friction shifts utilization. It reduces dispensing volume. It can protect PBM margins. Who benefits from keeping PA burden high for appropriate prescribers? That is worth sitting with.

**Gold card programs**

Gold card programs (sometimes called PA exemption programs) are a practical middle ground. Prescribers with strong track records get exempted from PA requirements for certain drugs or conditions. If a prescriber's PAs are approved at a high rate consistently, the authorization step is just friction. It is not changing anything.

Multiple states have enacted gold card legislation. The details vary, but these programs reduce burden for good prescribers and speed access for patients while keeping oversight where it matters. If your plan has not explored this, bring it up with your PBM. Your broker or consultant can help you evaluate whether a gold card program fits your population.

**Auditing your PA program for ROI**

Most employers know they have PA. Few can tell you whether the value justifies the friction. I walk clients through this regularly, and the exercise is always eye-opening.

The basic equation: cost avoided through appropriate denials and redirections, minus administrative costs, care delay costs, and therapy abandonment costs. If the second number is bigger, your PA program is a net negative. Simple math, but it rarely gets done. Not because the advisory teams are missing it, but because PBMs do not hand over this data in a usable format.

Pull your PA data from the PBM for the most recent twelve months. You want total requests submitted, approval rate (first-pass and after appeal), average turnaround time, top twenty drugs by PA volume, denial reasons by category, and appeal volume with overturn rate.

Then look for low-value PA requirements. Flag any drug where:

- The approval rate exceeds 90% on first pass. That is a rubber stamp.
- The drug is a maintenance medication for stable patients. Renewals are creating burden without clinical value.
- Average turnaround exceeds 48 hours. Delays are affecting care.
- The appeal overturn rate exceeds 50%. Initial criteria are too restrictive.

Those are your candidates for PA removal or gold card exemption.

Now calculate the cost side. Start with PBM fees for PA processing. In our contract reviews we see three common structures:

> **Administrative PA review** (no clinical review required): $25 to $40 per PA
>
> **Clinical pharmacist review**: $60 to $80 per PA
>
> **"Included" in the admin fee** (no separate line item): Not visible without a fee transparency clause

The third structure is the one that causes the most trouble. If your PA cost is bundled into your admin fee, you cannot audit it. You cannot benchmark it. You cannot tell how much of your administrative spend is actually review work versus everything else your admin fee is supposed to cover. A fee transparency clause that requires itemized reporting is the fix. If you do not have one, that belongs on the next contract redline.

Add internal staff time managing requests and member complaints. Add provider relations impact. Then estimate care delays: average time between prescription and approval, percentage of prescriptions abandoned during the PA process, downstream costs of delayed treatment. Even directional estimates help.

Score your program against these benchmarks:

> **Overall approval rate**
> Green: 70-85%. Yellow: 85-95%. Red: Above 95% (rubber stamp) or below 70% (too restrictive).

> **Average turnaround time**
> Green: Under 24 hours. Yellow: 24 to 72 hours. Red: Over 72 hours.

> **Appeal overturn rate**
> Green: Under 25%. Yellow: 25 to 50%. Red: Over 50% (initial criteria too tight).

> **Therapy abandonment**
> Green: Under 5%. Yellow: 5 to 15%. Red: Over 15%.

> **Top 20 drugs with approval rate above 90%**
> Green: 0 to 3 drugs. Yellow: 4 to 8 drugs. Red: More than 8 drugs (remove PA from these).

*Illustrative example for educational purposes. Actual metrics vary by plan.*

Based on what you find, build the optimization plan. Remove PA from drugs with greater than 90% approval rates. Implement gold card exemptions for high-performing prescribers. Negotiate turnaround time guarantees with your PBM. Request quarterly PA performance reports as a contract deliverable. Set a twelve-month review cycle. Your broker or consultant can help structure the request to the PBM.

One PBM data request. About two hours of analysis. That is the investment.

**What your contract should guarantee**

I see too many PBM contracts that treat PA as an afterthought. And I mean too many. Criteria should be accessible and clearly defined, not buried in clinical policy documents that never surface during normal reviews. The contract should specify how often PA criteria are reviewed and updated. Processing time guarantees protect access: we advise 48 hours maximum for standard requests, 24 hours for urgent, same business day for electronic submissions. Appeals pathways and timelines should be explicit. Approval, denial, and appeal rates should be a standing deliverable, not something you have to chase.

If your contract says PA requests will be processed in a "timely manner" without defining what that means, it protects no one. I see that language all the time.

**What we advise**

Prior authorization is a legitimate tool that has been overextended. The gap between having PA and understanding PA is where value leaks out of your plan.

We tell every client the same thing: review whether criteria reflect current clinical practice. Monitor burden on providers and members. Assess whether denial rates indicate criteria problems rather than bad prescribing. Explore gold card programs. And demand PA reporting in your PBM contract.

The goal is appropriate utilization. Not maximum barriers.

Have you audited your PA program for ROI? If you found something unexpected, leave a comment below. If this analysis is useful, share it with your broker or benefits team.

## PA Turnaround Time: What Your Contract Should Guarantee

**Publication:** Benefit Blind Spots
**Publish:** Thursday, April 23, 2026 at 7:30 AM EST
**Length:** ~550 words (~3 min read)
**Tone:** Practical, tactical
**Pillar:** PBM Contracts
**Type:** Field Note

---

### Field Note Body

We review hundreds of PBM contracts each year. The majority contain some reference to prior authorization turnaround time. Very few contain language that would actually protect a plan sponsor when it matters.

Here is what we typically see, and what we think you should push for instead.

**The Standard Language Problem**

Most PBM contracts reference turnaround time in general terms. "PBM shall process prior authorization requests in a timely manner" is common. Some specify 48-72 hours for standard requests and 24 hours for urgent requests.

The problem is not the existence of turnaround standards. It is the lack of specificity around what counts as "urgent," what happens when standards are missed, and how turnaround is measured.

**What "Urgent" Should Actually Mean**

State-level PA reform is pushing toward clearer definitions. Several states now require 24-hour turnaround for urgent requests and 48-72 hours for standard requests. But the definition of "urgent" varies.

We advise employers to define urgency based on clinical criteria in their contracts:

- Acute conditions requiring immediate treatment initiation
- Medications where delay creates measurable clinical risk
- Post-discharge prescriptions supporting care transitions
- Conditions where therapy interruption causes harm

Without these definitions, the PBM decides what qualifies as urgent. That decision may not align with your members' clinical needs.

**Benchmarks Worth Requiring**

Based on what we see across contracts and what leading employers negotiate:

- Standard PA turnaround: 48 hours maximum from complete submission
- Urgent PA turnaround: 24 hours maximum
- Electronic PA response: 2 hours for formulary alternatives, same business day for clinical review
- Appeal turnaround: 48 hours for urgent, 5 business days for standard

The key phrase is "from complete submission." PBMs sometimes measure from when they consider the request complete, not from when the provider submits it. Define the starting clock clearly.

**Escalation Protocols**

Your contract should specify what happens when turnaround standards are missed:

- Automatic temporary supply authorization (e.g., 72-hour emergency fill) when PA exceeds the standard turnaround window
- Defined escalation contacts for PA delays affecting urgent clinical situations
- Reporting on turnaround compliance as part of regular performance reviews
- Financial accountability if turnaround failures are systematic

**The State Reform Trend**

Multiple states have enacted or proposed PA reform legislation requiring specific turnaround standards, gold card programs, and transparency in approval and denial rates. These requirements set a floor, not a ceiling. You can and should negotiate standards that exceed minimum state requirements. The best consultants we work with are already building these into contract redlines.

**Three Actions for Your Next Contract Review**

1. Audit your current PA turnaround language. If it says "timely" without specific hours, it needs revision.
2. Define urgency criteria in the contract rather than leaving that determination to the PBM.
3. Require turnaround compliance reporting as a standing deliverable, not something you have to request.

PA turnaround is not an administrative detail. It is a care access issue with contractual solutions.

**Slide 2 (02/07) — Generic Drug Definition, Section 2.06:**
- Provision tag: "DEFINITION OF GENERIC"
- Strike-through: *"those products identified as generic in the PBM's proprietary classification and pricing file, as updated from time to time in the PBM's sole discretion"*
- Insertion: *"drug products assigned an 'A' therapeutic equivalence rating in the current FDA Orange Book, excluding brand-name, authorized-generic, and single-source products."*
- Why it matters: "If the PBM gets to define 'generic,' they can reclassify a brand whenever it helps their margin on a guarantee. Anchor the definition to the Orange Book and the leverage disappears."

**Slide 3 (03/07) — Pass-Through of Manufacturer Payments, Section 5.03:**
- Provision tag: "REBATE PASS-THROUGH"
- Strike-through: *"a portion of manufacturer rebates attributable to the Plan Sponsor's utilization"*
- Insertion: *"100% of all Manufacturer Payments, including rebates, administrative fees, market-share payments, data fees, and inflation protection, received by the PBM or any Affiliate, within ninety (90) days of receipt."*
- Why it matters: "'A portion of' 'rebates' is a trap door. The money lives in the fees that aren't called rebates: aggregator, admin, data, inflation. Name them all. Put a clock on them."

**Slide 4 (04/07) — Audit Scope and Auditor Selection, Section 6.02:**
- Provision tag: "AUDIT RIGHTS"
- Strike-through on scope: *"Claims data"* → Insertion: *"100% of Claims, including pharmacy-level acquisition cost, rebate invoices, and MAC lists"*
- Strike-through on auditor: *"mutually agreed upon by the parties"* → Insertion: *"of Plan Sponsor's sole choosing"*
- Added sentence: *"No confidentiality provision shall limit audit scope."*
- Why it matters: "'Mutually agreed' is where audits go to die. Take auditor selection and scope off the table as negotiable items. They are the audit."

**Slide 5 (05/07) — Claim Pricing / Spread Pricing, Section 7.03:**
- Provision tag: "SPREAD PRICING"
- Strike-through: *"be calculated in accordance with the applicable AWP discount guarantee, measured on an aggregate basis"*
- Insertion: *"equal the amount paid to the dispensing pharmacy, plus a disclosed per-claim administrative fee. No spread pricing of any kind is permitted."*
- Why it matters: "Aggregate guarantees hide per-claim spread. Tie the price you pay to the price the pharmacy receives, claim by claim, and the spread disappears."

**Slide 6 (06/07) — Termination for Convenience, Sections 8.02 & 8.06:**
- Provision tag: "TERMINATION FOR CONVENIENCE"
- Strike-through: *"Either party may terminate this Agreement for material breach following a 90-day cure period and written notice."*
- Insertion: *"Plan Sponsor may terminate this Agreement for convenience upon ninety (90) days' written notice, without penalty, repayment of implementation credits, or recoupment of rebates already earned."*
- Why it matters: "Without 'for convenience,' you're locked in for the duration. No PBM will ever concede 'material breach' in writing. Convenience is the only exit that actually opens."

## ▼ DRAFT BEGINS ▼

**Working title: The Auction Where One Bidder Knows the Scoring Sheet**

Imagine a sealed-bid auction where one bidder has seen the scoring sheet. That bidder does not waste effort building the best offer. It builds the offer that wins the score. The PBM Request for Proposals, the RFP, the competitive process through which employers choose these vendors, is that auction more often than anyone involved would like. This chapter is about why, and what would fix it, because after nine chapters of mechanisms, the natural question is: fine, then switch vendors. This is what actually happens when a plan tries.

The mechanisms act of this series ended with the last piece. We now know what the contracts do. The question for the closing act is what the people paying can do about it, and the first answer everyone reaches for is the RFP: run a competitive process, make the PBMs bid, pick the best deal. It is the right instinct. Here is what it collides with.

Most PBM RFPs are scored against headline pricing: the discount guarantees, the rebate minimums, the administrative fees. Those are exactly the numbers this series has spent nine chapters showing to be functions of definitions: what counts as a rebate, which claims sit inside the guarantee, what the exclusion list removes from the math. The PBM's response team knows precisely which scoring approach is in use, because they see hundreds of these processes a year from the inside, and the headline numbers are calibrated to top the sheet. The deeper economics, the specialty routing margins, the aggregator retention, the audit restrictions, the amendment rights, are usually outside the scoring rubric entirely. So the winning bid is real, in the sense that the vendor will honor its engineered numbers, and the plan's actual net cost is decided by everything the scoring sheet never asked about. In the reviews I do, the gap between what an RFP scored and what the resulting contract does is one of the most repeatable findings there is.

Now the part that must be said carefully, because it involves the advisors, and the good ones are the solution here, not the problem. The information asymmetry in this auction is structural: PBM response teams see the market's whole RFP landscape; any individual advisor sees their slice of it. The best brokers and consultants I work with know this asymmetry intimately. They are the ones who insist on normalized net-cost scoring, who drag the definitions into the evaluation, who bring independent pharmacy expertise to the table precisely because they know what the headline numbers conceal. The structural problem is that nothing in the system requires that standard of practice, and the vendors' engineering rewards every process that falls short of it.

So the fixes aim at the structure. First, a scoring floor: the Department of Labor, which oversees employer benefit plans, should publish guidance on what a prudent PBM selection process examines at minimum: net cost normalized across the bidders' own definitions, audit rights, termination rights, and specialty channel economics, not headline discounts alone. Fiduciaries follow DOL guidance the way drivers follow posted limits; publishing the floor changes behavior without a single enforcement action. Second, fiduciary clarity for the selection process itself, so that everyone advising a plan on this decision is unambiguously accountable to the plan. The professionals already practicing that way lose nothing; the arrangement quietly engineered around the scoring sheet loses its camouflage. Third, methodology-agnostic baseline disclosure: the bidders should file the definitional information that lets any evaluator normalize the bids, so no process depends on knowing which questions unlock the real numbers.

My view is that the RFP is still worth running, and worth running well; some of the strongest contracts I see came out of processes where the evaluators knew exactly where the engineering lives. The reform is not to replace the auction. It is to take the scoring sheet out of one bidder's hands and put the minimum questions into everyone's.

Everything in this chapter, and honestly, everything in this series, depends on one thing the RFP can win but only the contract can keep: the right to look. The final chapter is about the audit clause, the single provision that decides whether every promise described across these twelve pieces can ever be checked by the people paying for it.

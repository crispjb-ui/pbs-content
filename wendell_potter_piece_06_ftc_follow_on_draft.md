## ▼ DRAFT BEGINS ▼

**Working title: The Government Already Diagnosed the PBM Problem. The Treatment Plan Is Overdue.**

When a doctor runs every test, reads every scan, and writes a diagnosis in plain language, you expect the next appointment to be about treatment. In July 2024 the Federal Trade Commission (FTC) published an interim staff report about Pharmacy Benefit Managers with a title that reads like a diagnosis: powerful middlemen inflating drug costs and squeezing pharmacies. A second interim report followed, documenting enormous markups on specialty generic drugs dispensed through PBM-owned pharmacies. The tests have been run. The scans have been read. This piece is about the treatment plan.

Every chapter of this series so far has walked one mechanism: the spread between prices, the ownership of the pharmacies, the profit layer on the most expensive drug class in memory, the interception of copay assistance, the steering of prescriptions between channels. Here is the thing worth pausing on: none of that was news to the federal government. Nearly everything this series has described, the FTC's own staff documented. Reading that report was, for me, a strange experience. I review PBM contracts for a living, and page after page described patterns I had been finding in contract language and claims data for years, now printed under a federal seal.

So what has happened since the diagnosis? Less than the diagnosis deserved. There has been real movement, and it should be counted honestly: enforcement activity around insulin pricing reached settlements with the largest PBMs, several states passed serious PBM laws, and Congress moved reform provisions further than in any prior session. But the central structural findings, the vertical integration, the routing economics, the opacity of the intermediary layers, remain standing. A diagnosis this thorough, followed by treatment this partial, is how a disease gets managed instead of cured.

Here are the five actions the diagnosis actually supports. Two of them need no new law at all.

First, the FTC should finish what it started: a final report, and enforcement under its existing authority against the specific practices its staff documented, starting with the specialty markup findings from its own second report. An interim report is not an outcome. Second, the Secretary of Health and Human Services can act through the programs the government already runs: Medicare's prescription drug program can require, in its contracts, the disclosure the FTC found missing, and what Medicare requires has a way of becoming the market's default.

The other three need Congress. Third, an audit rights floor for self-funded employer plans, written into the federal benefits law (ERISA) those plans live under, so the verification rights this series keeps returning to stop depending on negotiating leverage. Fourth, a fix for the gap every chapter has hit: when a state regulates a PBM practice and ERISA preemption walls off the self-funded majority, federal law should backstop the state's floor instead of erasing it. Fifth, real scrutiny of further consolidation. The report documented what vertical integration has already produced; approving more of it while the findings sit unaddressed is a choice, and it should be an examined one.

You will notice none of these five is exotic. That is the point. The hard intellectual work, the documentation, the pattern-finding, the naming of mechanisms, has been done, much of it by the government itself. What stands between the diagnosis and the treatment is not complexity. It is the lobbying weight of an industry whose largest members are also the country's largest health care corporations, and the ordinary difficulty of moving Congress on anything with that much money defending the status quo.

My view is that plan sponsors and the people who advise them should stop waiting for the treatment plan and start acting as if the diagnosis is the operative document, because it is. Everything the FTC documented is visible, in miniature, in an individual plan's own contract and claims, and unlike Congress, a plan sponsor does not need sixty votes to demand its own numbers.

There is one entity this whole diagnostic record touches only lightly, and it happens to sit exactly where the rebate dollars bend out of sight. It has a bland name, an ownership chart that leads back to the PBMs, and in some cases a mailing address outside the country. The next chapter is a field guide to it.

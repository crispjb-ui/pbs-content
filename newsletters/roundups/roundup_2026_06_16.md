## Article Body

Five stories crossed my desk this week. Four of them touch your pharmacy spend directly, and one of them is the clearest proof I have seen all year of what rebates actually do to a formulary.

---

### Employers Start Dropping GLP-1 Weight-Loss Coverage for 2027

*Business Group on Health / Mercer (via STAT) | June 11, 2026*

About 10% of employers who currently cover GLP-1s for weight loss told the Business Group on Health they plan to drop that coverage in 2027. Mercer put the number at roughly 5% of employers with more than 500 workers. Only 72% of employers covering these drugs expect to still be covering them next year, even as Wegovy and Zepbound net prices have come down.

This is the cost decision I am fielding most right now. Lower per-unit pricing has not helped, because enrollment keeps climbing, especially with oral options pulling in people who never tried an injectable. Before you cut coverage outright, ask your broker to model the middle paths: tighter clinical criteria, step therapy, or a separate cost-share tier. A blunt yes-or-no on GLP-1s usually leaves money and goodwill on the table.

---

### Express Scripts and PCMA Sue Tennessee to Block the PBM-Pharmacy Breakup Law

*Healthcare Dive / Healthcare Finance News | June 12, 2026*

Express Scripts and PCMA filed in federal court to strike down Tennessee's FAIR Rx Act, joining CVS Caremark, which sued last month, and Cigna, which followed on June 15. The law bars any pharmacy affiliated with both a PBM and an insurer from dispensing in the state once it takes effect in 2028.

I read these suits as a tell. The PBMs are fighting this hard because vertical integration, owning the PBM, the pharmacy, and the plan, is where a lot of the margin lives. In the contracts we review, that affiliated-pharmacy routing is rarely spelled out in language a plan sponsor would catch on a first read. You do not need to wait for Tennessee to resolve. Ask your broker which of your own claims route through a PBM-owned pharmacy, and what those scripts cost compared with an unaffiliated pharmacy filling the same drug. That gap is the thing these laws are aimed at.

---

### Medicare's $50 GLP-1 Bridge Program Starts July 1

*STAT / CMS | June 15, 2026*

Starting July 1, eligible Medicare beneficiaries can get Wegovy, Zepbound, and Foundayo for a $50 monthly copay through a temporary CMS demonstration running into 2027. Manufacturers agreed to a net price of roughly $245 a month to make it work.

That $245 number is the one to write down. It is a public, federally negotiated net price on the same drugs your plan covers, and it gives you a reference point you did not have a month ago. If your net cost per fill sits well above $245 after rebates, that is a fair question to put to your PBM at renewal. "Temporary" Medicare programs have a way of becoming permanent, so I expect this benchmark to stick around.

---

### FDA Clears Colorado to Import Drugs From Canada

*STAT / Colorado Public Radio | June 15, 2026*

The FDA approved Colorado's plan to import 20 prescription drugs from Canada, including Ozempic and Eliquis, the second state to win that approval. The state projects savings of 20% to 60% on the covered drugs.

For most self-funded plans this is a signal more than an action item, since the program runs through the state and excludes insulin. What it confirms is that the price you pay domestically is a policy choice, not a fixed cost. Watch whether other states follow, because a working importation channel changes the leverage in every drug-pricing conversation.

---

### One More Worth Watching

New research in JAMA Health Forum, flagged by Drug Channels this week, found Medicare Part D plans have reversed years of favoring high-list, high-rebate brand-name MS drugs and are now covering lower-cost generics after the Inflation Reduction Act changed the incentives. It is the cleanest evidence I have seen that formularies follow rebate dollars, not patient cost. Worth remembering the next time your PBM defends a brand-preferred placement.

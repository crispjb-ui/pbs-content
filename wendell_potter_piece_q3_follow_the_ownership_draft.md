## ▼ DRAFT BEGINS ▼

**Working title: Every Road in Pharmacy Benefits Leads to a Company the PBM's Parent Owns. That Is Not a Coincidence. It Is the Design.**

There is an old rule for understanding any complicated system where money seems to disappear: follow the money. In pharmacy benefits, that rule has stopped being enough. You can follow a rebate dollar honestly, ledger to ledger, and lose it anyway, because somewhere along its route it passes through a company you have never heard of. So here is the updated rule, and the subject of this piece: follow the ownership.

Last year I wrote a series walking through the mechanisms of the Pharmacy Benefit Manager (PBM) economy one at a time: the pricing spreads, the rebate routing, the specialty pharmacies, the fee layers. Each mechanism looked different up close. Step back far enough and they resolve into a single picture. In nearly every mechanism, the counterparty on the other side of the transaction, the entity collecting the margin, shares a parent company with the PBM that arranged the transaction.

Walk the four biggest examples with me, briefly, because seeing them side by side is the point.

The rebate dollar first. When a manufacturer pays for formulary position, the payment is negotiated by a group purchasing organization, and each of the big three PBMs works through one its own corporate family created: CVS Health's Caremark through Ascent, UnitedHealth's OptumRx through Emisar, Cigna's Express Scripts through Zinc. The entity taking a cut of the rebate before the pass-through math begins is not a stranger. It is a sibling.

The prescription itself, next. When a specialty drug is routed to a designated pharmacy, the pharmacy at the top of the routing list, CVS Specialty, Optum Specialty, Accredo, belongs to the same parent as the PBM writing the routing rule. The margin earned at the counter stays in the family.

Now the newer one: the drug's own label. In the last few years, each of the big-three families has created private-label subsidiaries that put the family's name on biosimilar versions of expensive drugs, arrangements publicly associated with names like Cordavis at CVS Health and Quallent at Cigna's Express Scripts. When the PBM's formulary then prefers the family's own label, the manufacturer's margin, the position that used to belong to an outside drugmaker, also stays in the family.

And finally the quietest one, which almost nobody outside of accounting has heard of. Health insurers operate under a rule requiring that most of every premium dollar, generally 80 to 85 cents, be spent on actual care rather than kept as profit. Here is what vertical integration does to that rule: when the insurer's sibling pharmacy or PBM charges the insurer a healthy price, that payment counts as care spending, and the profit surfaces in the sibling, on the other side of the corporate wall, where the rule does not look. The regulation still works exactly as written. The ownership works around it.

Four mechanisms. Four different bodies of law and contract language. One identical answer to the question "who ended up with the margin?" Simply put, the modern PBM conglomerate has learned that any rule aimed at a transaction can be answered with an acquisition, because a rule that polices the deal between two companies goes quiet when both companies are the same company.

This is why I have come to believe that most PBM reform proposals, including some good ones, are aimed one level too low. Disclosure rules, pass-through mandates, and pricing definitions all regulate transactions. The industry's decade-long project has been making transactions internal. You cannot referee a trade that never leaves the building.

So the reform lens that matters now is the ownership lens, and it has three practical expressions. Regulators reviewing any conduct rule should ask the ownership question first: does this rule still bind when both parties share a parent, and if not, what closes that gap? Lawmakers writing pass-through and disclosure statutes should write them the way I argued in the aggregator piece, following the dollar to whatever entity holds it, rather than naming entities that can be reorganized. And the enforcement agencies weighing the next merger in this industry should treat the four examples above as the record of what the previous approvals produced.

Plan sponsors do not have to wait for any of that. The ownership lens works at contract scale: for every counterparty your pharmacy benefit touches, the pharmacy, the rebate entity, the label on the biosimilar, ask what its parent company is, and ask what your contract says about transactions where your vendor sits on both sides. In the contracts I review, that one question organizes more findings than any other single question we ask.

My view, stated plainly: the industry consolidated faster than the rulebook, and the gap between those two speeds is where most of the money in this series went. The rulebook can catch up. It starts by regulating what the industry actually built, which is not a set of transactions. It is a family tree.

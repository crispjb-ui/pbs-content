## CA-01: Self-Funded Health System as Plan Sponsor

**Surfaced:** May 16, 2026 in Bryce Platt LinkedIn thread on hospital-owned specialty pharmacies. Ginny referenced UNC Health experience in the comment chain.

**Core insight:** The hospital-owned specialty pharmacy debate is usually framed as a system-level question (good or bad for healthcare). The plan sponsor angle almost nobody articulates: **who owns the plan determines whether the 340B savings flow back to the plan or stay with the hospital.**

- **Self-funded health system as plan sponsor** (UNC Health, Cleveland Clinic, Mayo, similar hospital-system-owned plans): the dispensing hospital and the plan share the same balance sheet. 340B savings on specialty fills flow back to the plan as reduced net cost. Integration genuinely benefits the plan.
- **Independent employer plan buying specialty from an unrelated health system:** the 340B acquisition discount stays on the hospital's balance sheet. Plan pays AWP-based or WAC-based reimbursement on a drug the hospital acquired at 340B. Hospital captures the spread.

**Best-fit formats:**
- Substack deep dive: "When the Plan Sponsor Owns the Pharmacy: 340B Savings, Channel Routing, and Who Captures What"
- Thursday Field Note: "Who Owns the Specialty Pharmacy Determines Who Captures the Savings"
- LinkedIn carousel (decoder): "Hospital-Owned Specialty Pharmacy: When It Helps Your Plan and When It Doesn't"

**Open questions to develop before drafting:**
- Hospital-system-owned plans that have published cost containment results from in-house specialty (UNC, Cleveland Clinic, Mayo, others). Concrete examples make the piece.
- How 340B Drug Pricing Program eligibility intersects with the hospital's own self-funded plan vs. third-party employer plans (covered entity status, GPO prohibition, child-site rules).
- The PBM contract clause that determines whether channel routing is plan-controlled or PBM-controlled (specialty exclusivity, network design, step therapy bias).

## CA-02: GLP-1 Coverage Alternatives + the Three PBM Contract Clauses Underneath

**Surfaced:** May 16, 2026 in Bryce Platt LinkedIn thread on GLP-1 coverage alternatives. Bryce posted a "4 Alternatives to Traditional GLP-1 Coverage" decision matrix (HRA, EBHRA, Health FSA, Carve-Out) and listed PBM contract considerations as a one-line afterthought. PBS's lane is the layer underneath the matrix — which contract clauses determine whether the carve-out math actually works.

**Core insight:** Most coverage-model matrices in circulation (Bryce's matrix, Mercer / Aon advisory PDFs, broker decks) stop at the financial structure layer: who pays, how it integrates with ACA, what the administrative burden looks like. The clauses in the existing PBM contract are what determine whether a GLP-1 carve-out is cost-saving or cost-amplifying. PBS sees three structural clauses that decide the outcome:

1. **The exclusivity / network-flow clause.** Varies meaningfully across PBM contracts. Some require all prescriptions through the PBM network. Some specify all formulary drugs. Some already carve out specific therapeutic categories. Reading the actual clause determines whether a GLP-1 carve-out needs (a) a contract amendment, (b) a sole-source waiver, or (c) sits cleanly within existing language.

2. **The rebate-guarantee voiding clause.** Most PBM contracts include language that voids rebate guarantees across the entire formulary if the plan changes coverage outside the agreed structure. A GLP-1 carve-out that triggers this clause can cost the plan more in lost rebates on the rest of the formulary than it saves on the GLP-1 spend. This is the trap that turns a "high cost savings" carve-out on paper into a net negative in practice.

3. **The service-category exclusivity clause (the competing-program trap).** Distinct from drug-level exclusivity. Most PBM contracts now include right-of-first-refusal or service-category exclusivity language on programs the PBM also operates (CVS Weight Management Program, ESI EncircleRx, Optum specialty weight management). A carve-out that touches a service category the PBM operates can trigger this language even when the GLP-1 drug itself sits outside drug-level network requirements. The conflict isn't always at the drug level.

**Best-fit formats:**
- Substack deep dive: "Four Ways to Cover GLP-1s. Three Contract Clauses That Decide If Any of Them Work."
- Plan Sponsor Toolkit handout: "GLP-1 Carve-Out Contract Review Checklist" (paste-ready language audit for the three clauses above, decision tree for amendment vs. waiver vs. existing-language path)
- LinkedIn carousel (decoder): "Decoding the GLP-1 Carve-Out Contract" — five slides on the exclusivity clause, rebate-voiding language, service-category trap, amendment timing, and audit-rights interaction
- Thursday Field Note: "The Competing-Program Trap: Why Your GLP-1 Carve-Out May Already Be Blocked"

**Open questions to develop before drafting:**

Without those, carve-out is administrative complexity without unit-cost savings. Math example worth building.

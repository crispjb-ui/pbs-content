## ▼ DRAFT BEGINS ▼

**Working title: The Copay Card Your Employee Uses Is Being Intercepted. By Your Own Plan's Middleman.**

Imagine a scholarship fund that helps students afford tuition. Now imagine the fund hires an administrator to process the scholarships, and the administrator also owns the college, decides which courses qualify, and decides whether the scholarship counts toward the student's bill or toward the college's revenue. Same dollars, different beneficiary, depending entirely on how the administrator counts. That is the structure underneath copay accumulator programs. It is not a metaphor stretched. It is the crediting math.

The last piece ended at the pharmacy counter, where the manufacturer's copay card collides with a program the Pharmacy Benefit Manager (PBM) runs quietly against it. This chapter is that collision. On the map this series is walking, we are watching two streams meet: manufacturer money and the machinery that decides where it lands.

Start with what these programs actually are, because the vocabulary hides the mechanics. A drug manufacturer offers a copay assistance card so a patient can afford an expensive specialty drug. Under an **accumulator** program, the plan accepts the manufacturer's money toward the claim but does not count it toward the member's deductible. The member's costs are covered this month, and then, when the card runs out, the member discovers their deductible is still nearly untouched. A **maximizer** is the same mechanic run more deliberately: the program sets the copay amounts to draw the maximum value from the card across the whole year.

Now the honest part. There are three ways to look at this, and each has something real in it. From the patient's side, these programs create affordability cliffs on the drugs people most depend on. From the plan sponsor's side, there is a legitimate argument: manufacturer assistance is marketing money designed to keep patients on the highest-priced brand, and capturing it lowers plan costs, which pays for everyone else's benefits. If a plan adopts that position openly, designs it carefully, and tells its members plainly, that is a defensible plan-design choice.

Here is the problem, and it sits with neither the patient nor the plan. In the contracts I review, the accumulator program is almost always administered by the same PBM that owns the specialty pharmacy dispensing the drug, and that also writes the formulary deciding which drugs are eligible for the program. The named programs make the point: SaveOnSP operates in the Express Scripts orbit, and Cigna owns Express Scripts. PrudentRx is paired with CVS Caremark, owned by CVS Health. One vendor decides which drugs are in the program, fills the prescription at a pharmacy it owns, and runs the math on whose column the manufacturer's money lands in. Simply put, the entity counting the dollars has interests on every side of the count, except the member's.

That structure creates possibilities a plan sponsor cannot see from the summary reporting. Formulary placement can favor the drugs with the richest assistance available. Fills can route to the affiliated pharmacy. Program fees can be calculated as a share of the manufacturer money collected, which means the administrator is paid more when more assistance is captured, regardless of what that does to the member or to the plan's net position.

The states have noticed, partially. A majority of states have now passed some form of accumulator protection, most of them requiring that assistance count toward the member's deductible. But those laws mostly reach fully insured plans. Most large employers self-fund, and self-funded plans sit under the federal ERISA framework, out of reach of the state fixes. So the protection exists mainly where the problem is smallest.

What should actually happen is structural, not prohibitive. First, disclosure: a federal rule requiring that any accumulator or maximizer program be described to members in plain language before the plan year begins, including what happens to their deductible. Second, a firewall: the entity administering the program should not also own the pharmacy dispensing the eligible drugs. Third, the formulary test: program eligibility should rest on clinical criteria the plan sponsor can audit, not on which manufacturer's assistance budget is largest.

My view is that the ban-or-keep fight has been arguing past the real defect for years. A transparent, plan-designed assistance strategy is a legitimate tool. A program run in the dark by a vertically integrated administrator with fees tied to the intercepted dollars is something else. The reform worth having separates those two things.

The programs in this chapter decide whose column the money lands in. The next chapter is about a quieter decision made upstream: which pharmacy the prescription is allowed to go to at all, and what the same drug costs at each of the three doors.

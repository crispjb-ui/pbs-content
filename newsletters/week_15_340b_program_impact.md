# PART 1: SUBSTACK ARTICLE

## 340B and Your Pharmacy Benefit: What Employers Should Know
**Subtitle:** How the 340B drug pricing program affects self-funded employer pharmacy claims

**Publication:** Benefit Blind Spots
**Publish:** Monday, April 13, 2026 at 7:30 AM EST
**Length:** ~1,500 words (~6 min read)
**Tone:** Analytical authority
**Pillar:** Transparency

---

### Article Body

A provider acquires a drug at a steep 340B discount. Your employee fills the prescription. Your plan gets billed at standard rates. The spread is revenue. Not yours.

I bring this up in client meetings a lot, and the reaction is almost always the same: "What's 340B?" That is the problem in one sentence. The 340B Drug Pricing Program was created to help safety-net providers stretch federal resources. Good program, good intent. But it has grown into something most employers have never examined, and it is probably affecting your claims right now without you knowing.

Here is the short version: providers in the 340B program buy outpatient drugs at 25-50% below wholesale. They can dispense those drugs to anyone, including your commercially insured employees. When that happens, your plan reimburses at standard contract rates for a drug the provider bought at a fraction of that cost. The difference is theirs to keep. Legal. By design. And completely invisible in your claims data.

**Why you have never seen this in your reports**

The mechanics are simple enough. Drug manufacturers participating in Medicaid are required to sell outpatient drugs at discounted prices to qualifying hospitals, health centers, and other safety-net organizations (called "covered entities"). The original idea made sense: help providers serving vulnerable populations afford medications.

The part that affects employers is what happens next. Nothing in your standard claims reporting tells you a drug was acquired at a 340B price. Your PBM processes the claim at whatever reimbursement rate your contract specifies. The provider got the drug cheaper. Your plan paid full price. The gap between those two numbers is invisible.

We review hundreds of PBM contracts each year. Almost none of them mention 340B. When the contract is silent, you pay standard rates no matter what the dispensing entity paid for the medication.

**Contract pharmacies made it bigger**

Originally, 340B was contained. A qualifying hospital bought drugs at the discount and dispensed them through its own pharmacy. Simple enough.

Then contract pharmacy arrangements changed the math. Covered entities can now partner with outside pharmacies (retail chains, independents, specialty pharmacies) to dispense 340B-priced drugs on their behalf. Your local CVS or Walgreens may have contract pharmacy relationships with nearby hospitals. So 340B claims show up in your data at pharmacies you would never connect to the program.

If your employee population is concentrated near urban medical centers or safety-net providers, the volume of 340B-touched claims could be meaningful. The financial arrangement between the covered entity and the contract pharmacy? That is between them. You are not part of that conversation, but you are paying the claim.

**The reform debate (and why you should not wait for it)**

340B has attracted a lot of regulatory attention. Critics point to program expansion beyond the original safety-net mission, with some covered entities now serving large commercially insured populations and generating substantial 340B revenue. Manufacturers have tried to limit 340B discounts for contract pharmacy arrangements, and the legal battles continue. Various reform proposals would tighten eligibility or require covered entities to direct savings toward patient care.

I am not taking a position on whether 340B is good or bad. It does real good for safety-net providers. But waiting for Congress to fix the transparency problem is not a strategy. You can get visibility into your own data right now.

**How to find 340B claims in your data**

Most employers have never looked. Here is where to start.

Ask your PBM directly: "Can you identify claims dispensed by 340B-covered entities in our pharmacy data?" Some PBMs can flag these. Many will not unless you specifically ask. If your PBM tells you they cannot identify 340B claims, that answer is itself worth documenting.

Next, look up who is in your network. HRSA (the Health Resources and Services Administration) publishes a database of every 340B-registered covered entity. You can cross-reference that list against the pharmacies in your PBM's network. How many 340B entities are in there? What percentage of your specialty claims route through them?

Then look at the pricing. For claims you can identify as dispensed by 340B entities, compare what your plan paid against what the entity likely paid (340B ceiling prices are publicly available for most drugs). The gap between those numbers is the revenue you are funding.

Pull out your PBM contract and search for "340B." Look for pricing adjustment provisions, passthrough language, and audit rights that cover 340B identification. If you find nothing, that is your answer: you are paying standard rates regardless of acquisition cost.

And bring these questions to your next PBM review: How many claims in the past 12 months went through 340B entities? What did we spend at those entities compared to non-340B pharmacies for the same drugs? Can you implement 340B-aware pricing? What would it save us? Your broker or benefits consultant can help you frame these requests and push for answers.

**Three questions that tell you where you stand**

If you can answer these, you have visibility: What percentage of your claims flow through 340B entities? What is the estimated pricing gap? Does your PBM contract address it at all?

If you cannot answer them, the gap is open. And it is probably wider than you think.

I am not arguing against 340B. The program does real work for safety-net providers. But employers deserve to know when they are paying standard rates for drugs acquired at steep discounts. That is not opposition. That is just knowing what you are paying for.

Have you looked at 340B in your claims data? If you found something you did not expect, leave a comment. If this is useful, send it to your broker or benefits team.

## Contract Pharmacy Arrangements: The 340B Variable Employers Miss

**Publication:** Benefit Blind Spots
**Publish:** Thursday, April 16, 2026 at 7:30 AM EST

**Tone:** Practical, tactical
**Pillar:** Transparency
**Type:** Field Note

### Field Note Body

Monday's article covered how 340B affects employer pharmacy benefits broadly. Today we are narrowing the focus to one specific element that creates the most confusion: contract pharmacy arrangements.

The original 340B model was relatively contained. A covered entity (a qualifying hospital or health center) purchased drugs at the 340B discount and dispensed them through its own pharmacy. The pricing benefit stayed within the entity's four walls.

Contract pharmacy arrangements changed that equation. Covered entities can now designate outside pharmacies (retail chains, independent pharmacies, specialty pharmacies) to dispense 340B-priced drugs on their behalf. The covered entity purchases the drug at the 340B discount. The contract pharmacy dispenses it. The financial arrangement between the two is governed by their contract, not visible to the employer.

**Why this matters for your claims data**

When your member fills a prescription at a retail pharmacy that has a 340B contract pharmacy arrangement with a nearby hospital, that claim may involve a 340B-acquired drug. But nothing in your standard claims reporting flags it as such.

Your PBM processes the claim at standard reimbursement rates. The pharmacy received the drug at 340B pricing through the covered entity relationship. The spread between acquisition cost and what your plan paid is significant, but invisible in your data.

This is not a small-scale issue. Contract pharmacy arrangements have expanded considerably over the past decade. National retail chains participate in thousands of contract pharmacy relationships. If your population is concentrated near urban medical centers or safety-net providers, the odds are good that your members are filling prescriptions at pharmacies with 340B arrangements.

**What to look for**

Start by asking your PBM whether they can identify claims dispensed through 340B contract pharmacy arrangements. Not all PBMs have this capability, but some have developed 340B identification tools. If yours cannot flag these claims, that is a data gap worth understanding.

Then identify which pharmacies in your network have contract pharmacy relationships with 340B-covered entities. Your PBM or pharmacy network administrator should be able to provide this mapping, though it may require a specific request.

Finally, evaluate the pricing impact. If a meaningful volume of your claims flows through contract pharmacies dispensing 340B-acquired medications, the gap between acquisition cost and plan reimbursement represents a cost variable you are not currently managing.

*Illustrative example for educational purposes. Actual amounts vary by plan.* A brand medication with an AWP of $500 acquired at a 340B price of $275, dispensed through a contract pharmacy and billed to your plan at $450 after PBM discount. The contract pharmacy's effective acquisition cost is substantially below what your plan reimbursed.

**The practical takeaway**

I am not saying contract pharmacy arrangements are inherently problematic. They extend 340B access and can serve legitimate program purposes. But for employers, the lack of visibility into which claims involve 340B-acquired medications creates a pricing transparency gap you should know about.

Start by asking the question. If your PBM cannot identify 340B contract pharmacy claims, that gap should be part of your next contract discussion. Your broker or consultant can help you frame the request.

## "What I'd Ask" #1: Reviewing a PBM Renewal Proposal

**Publish:** Tuesday, April 15, 2026 at 7:30 AM EST
**Format:** Standalone Substack post
**Access:** Free (subscriber-only when paid tier launches)
**Length:** ~350 words

> This is a standalone post separate from the Monday article. Publish as its own article on Benefit Blind Spots.

### Ready-to-Publish Content

If I sat down with your PBM renewal proposal today, here are the questions I would ask before recommending whether to accept, negotiate, or RFP.

**About pricing:**
1. What is the proposed Generic Effective Rate, and how does it compare to the current guaranteed rate? Is it improving, flat, or eroding?
2. Are brand discount guarantees based on AWP or WAC? Did the benchmark change from the current contract?
3. What is the specialty drug pricing methodology? Is it a discount guarantee, a cost-plus model, or something else?

**About rebates:**
4. Is the rebate guarantee gross or net of PBM administrative fees?
5. What manufacturer revenue categories are excluded from the passthrough definition?
6. How does the proposed rebate per brand claim compare to the current guarantee and to actual performance?

**About terms:**
7. What changed from the current contract? Ask for a redline showing every modification.
8. Did the termination provisions change? Check for auto-renewal language that was not in the prior contract.
9. Are audit rights the same, expanded, or narrowed?

**About performance:**
10. How did the PBM perform against current contract guarantees? Request a guarantee reconciliation report before evaluating the renewal.

The renewal proposal is the PBM's opening position, not the final offer. Every number in it was calculated to protect their margin. Your job is to determine where the margin is and how much of it should be yours.

## ▼ DRAFT BEGINS ▼

**Working title: Regulators Killed the PBM's Retroactive Fee. Watch What Grew Back in Its Place.**

Imagine a bank fee charged six months after the transaction cleared, in an amount the bank calculates unilaterally, based on performance metrics the bank also defines. For years that was a fact of life for pharmacies in Medicare's drug program. The fees were called DIR fees, for Direct and Indirect Remuneration, and they worked like a tide: a pharmacy filled a prescription, got paid, and then months later watched part of the payment flow back out under a formula it could neither predict nor audit. Regulators eventually agreed this was indefensible. Starting with 2024, the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services (CMS) required those price concessions to be applied at the point of sale, ending the retroactive clawback. It was a genuine win. This chapter is about what the win teaches, because the economics did not die. They moved.

The last chapter was about concealment through corporate architecture. This one is about concealment through renaming, which is cheaper. On the map, we are at the third revenue stream, the fee layer, in its least visible wing: the fees that arrive after the fact.

Here is the pattern I see in contracts reviewed since the rule took effect. The retroactive DIR fee, as a named instrument in Medicare pharmacy contracts, is gone; the rule worked on its own terms. But instruments that behave like it keep appearing under other names. Pay-for-performance programs, where a portion of pharmacy reimbursement rides on quality metrics the PBM defines and scores. Network participation fees, charged for the privilege of being in the network at all. Reconciliation timing on effective-rate guarantees, where the true-up math arrives long after the claims and points, somehow, in a familiar direction. None of these is identical to the old fee, and each has a defensible description. Together, they reconstruct its economic effect: money that leaves the pharmacy after the transaction, on the payer's math.

Why should a self-funded employer care about a pharmacy-side fee fight in Medicare? Two reasons, and the second is the one that matters. First, the pharmacies squeezed hardest are the independents, and network adequacy is a plan problem when they close. Second, and this is the structural point of the whole chapter: the CMS rule applies to Medicare. The commercial market, where employer plans live, has no equivalent rule at all. The same PBMs run both. A mechanic proven profitable in the regulated market and merely banned there does not retire; it works the unregulated one. When I review commercial contracts, the after-the-fact adjustment layer, in its various costumes, is not an occasional finding. It is the norm.

What should happen next follows from what already worked. CMS should say, in guidance, what everyone involved already understands: the 2024 rule's purpose was to end retroactive pharmacy clawbacks, and instruments that reconstruct the same effect under new names sit inside that purpose. The Federal Trade Commission, whose own reports this series discussed two chapters ago, has existing authority to examine whether the commercial-market versions of these mechanics constitute the kind of unfair practice it exists to police. And Congress, when it is ready, should give the commercial market the disclosure parity the Medicare market now enjoys: if a fee, program, or reconciliation adjusts a pharmacy's payment after the fact, it should be disclosed as such, in both markets, in language a plan sponsor can audit.

My view is that this chapter's lesson is the most important process lesson in the series: banning a named practice invites a renamed one. The 2024 rule was real, and the workarounds are real, and both things are true at once. Regulation that chases names will lose to an industry that can generate new names faster than rulemaking can chase them. Regulation that defines effects, the way the aggregator fix in the last chapter follows the dollar rather than the entity, is the kind that holds.

The fee layer this chapter walked is measured in percentages and adjustments. The next chapter returns to the biggest single pool of hidden margin left on the map, the economics of routing specialty prescriptions, where the dollars are largest and the reporting is thinnest.

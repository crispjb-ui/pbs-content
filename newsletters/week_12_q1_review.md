# PART 1: SUBSTACK ARTICLE

## Q1 Review: What to Measure Before Renewal

**Publication:** Benefit Blind Spots
**Publish:** Monday, March 23, 2026 at 7:30 AM EST
**Length:** ~1,500 words (~6 min read)
**Tone:** Analytical authority
**Pillar:** Cost Containment / Self-Funded Guidance

---

### Article Body

Q1 is in the books.

Before you move on, your pharmacy data has a story to tell.

We just finished reviewing Q1 data for several clients, and the pattern is always the same. The metrics you review now determine your leverage at renewal and your strategy for the rest of the year. Yet most employers file their Q1 reports without looking closely, missing the window when data is fresh enough to act on and far enough ahead of renewal to create real positioning.

This issue covers what to measure, what the numbers should trigger, and how to turn Q1 data into renewal leverage.

---

**Why Q1 Review Matters**

Timing drives value.

Most employer pharmacy benefit renewals occur in Q4 for January effective dates. This means renewal discussions begin in earnest during Q2 and Q3. By the time you reach October negotiations, the leverage window has narrowed.

Q1 data arrives in March and April, the optimal moment to establish your position. You have:
- A full quarter of current-year performance to evaluate
- 6-8 months before substantive renewal discussions
- Time to investigate anomalies and build negotiation cases
- Opportunity to make mid-year adjustments if warranted

In my experience, employers who show up to renewal with data-backed questions negotiate differently than employers who accept PBM-generated summaries at face value. Q1 review is where that preparation begins.

---

**The Metrics That Actually Matter**

Not all metrics carry equal weight. When we sit down with a plan sponsor to review Q1 data, we focus on these:

*Total Pharmacy Spend vs. Budget/Projection*

Start with the headline number: how does Q1 spend compare to what you projected?

If you are significantly over projection, understand why:
- Is it price (higher cost per claim)?
- Is it utilization (more claims)?
- Is it mix (shift toward higher-cost categories)?

Each driver requires a different response. Price problems suggest contract issues. Utilization problems suggest clinical program opportunities. Mix problems suggest formulary or prior authorization review.

If you are under projection, understand that too. Favorable variance might indicate conservative projections, useful information for future budgeting.

*Per Member Per Month (PMPM) Trend*

PMPM normalizes for membership changes and provides the clearest view of underlying trend.

Compare Q1 2026 PMPM to Q1 2025. National pharmacy trend benchmarks typically run 5-8% annually. If your trend significantly exceeds benchmark, investigate. If your trend significantly trails benchmark, understand what is working.

*Generic Dispensing Rate (GDR) and Generic Effective Rate (GER)*

GDR measures what percentage of prescriptions filled are generics. Industry average exceeds 90%.

GER measures the actual savings captured from generic utilization. This is often lower than GDR would suggest, because generic pricing varies significantly.

Compare both metrics to your PBM's guarantees. Underperformance on either warrants a conversation. GER underperformance specifically may indicate MAC pricing issues worth investigating, and I will cover this in more detail in Thursday's Field Note.

*Specialty Spend as Percentage of Total*

Specialty now represents 50%+ of total pharmacy spend for many employers despite comprising only 2-3% of prescriptions.

Track this percentage over time. Rapid growth may indicate:
- New high-cost claimants entering the population
- Pipeline medications launching (new gene therapies, cell therapies)
- Utilization management opportunities being missed

*Top 10 Drugs by Spend - Changes from Prior Period*

Pull your top 10 drugs by total spend. Compare to the prior year's top 10.

What moved up? What dropped off? What is new?

Changes in your top 10 often tell you more about trend than aggregate numbers. A single new specialty medication can shift your entire cost profile.

*Rebate Performance vs. Guarantees*

If your contract includes rebate guarantees, verify Q1 performance.

Rebate timing complicates this; actual rebate payments often lag claims by 90+ days. But your PBM should provide estimated rebate performance that can be compared to guarantees.

Underperformance requires explanation. Outperformance may indicate conservative guarantees worth revisiting at renewal.

*High-Cost Claimant Analysis*

Identify members exceeding threshold spend levels (common thresholds: $50,000 or $100,000 annually).

How many high-cost claimants exist? How does this compare to prior periods? What conditions and medications drive their costs?

High-cost claimants often represent the highest-impact opportunity for coordinated care intervention. They also represent the highest risk for stop-loss and reinsurance planning.

---

**What the Numbers Should Trigger**

Data without action is academic. Here is what we tell clients each finding should prompt:

*If spend exceeds projection by more than 15%:*
- Immediate root cause analysis
- PBM meeting to review drivers
- Consider mid-year utilization management adjustments
- Evaluate stop-loss exposure

*If GDR or GER underperforms guarantees:*
- Request detailed guarantee calculation from PBM
- Identify drugs driving underperformance
- Evaluate MAC pricing if GER specifically lags

*If specialty spend percentage is increasing rapidly:*
- Identify new specialty medications in the population
- Review specialty pharmacy arrangements
- Evaluate site of care (retail vs. specialty pharmacy vs. medical benefit)
- Consider specialty carve-out if not already in place

*If high-cost claimant count is increasing:*
- Ensure clinical programs are engaged with these members
- Coordinate between pharmacy and medical care management
- Review predictive modeling for emerging high-cost members

*If a single drug represents more than 10% of total spend:*
- Understand the clinical situation
- Evaluate therapeutic alternatives
- Review rebate arrangements for that specific drug
- Consider whether contract renegotiation is warranted

---

**Building Your Renewal Timeline**

Q1 review feeds directly into renewal preparation. Based on what we see work best, here is where you should be:

*April (Now):*
- Q1 claims analysis complete
- Initial findings documented
- Questions for PBM identified
- High-level renewal strategy forming

*May-June:*
- Q1 + partial Q2 data reviewed
- Detailed negotiation priorities established
- Decision on RFP vs. renewal negotiation
- Your broker or consultant engaged for external support

*July-August:*
- If RFP: RFP issued, responses collected
- If negotiating: initial discussions with incumbent PBM
- Formulary and utilization management changes for next year identified

*September-October:*
- Final negotiations
- Contract terms finalized
- Implementation planning begins

*November-December:*
- Contracts signed
- Member communications prepared
- Transition activities (if changing PBMs)

If you are reading this in late March and have not completed Q1 review, you are not behind, but the window is now. Delay into May compresses everything downstream.

---

**What Changed in Q1 2026: Market Context**

A quick recap of what shifted this quarter that is relevant to your analysis and renewal planning:

*GLP-1 Landscape:* Oral semaglutide launched in January. Medicare coverage begins mid-year. Member awareness and demand continue to grow. If GLP-1s are not yet a significant line item in your spend, they likely will be.

*Biosimilar Adoption:* Humira biosimilar uptake continues to accelerate. PBM formulary positioning varies significantly. If your plan is still paying reference biologic prices, this represents an opportunity.

*Specialty Pipeline:* Several high-cost specialty medications launched or are pending. Gene and cell therapy pricing models remain unsettled. Site of care (pharmacy vs. medical benefit) continues to impact total cost.

*PBM Market:* Transparency pressure continues. FTC activity has increased scrutiny. Some employers are seeing improved contract terms; others report no change.

---

**Questions to Bring to Your PBM**

Armed with Q1 data, these are the questions I would bring to my PBM:

1. "Our GER is 2 points below guarantee. Walk me through the calculation and what is driving the gap."

2. "These five drugs represent 35% of our spend. What are the rebate terms for each, and are there therapeutic alternatives we should consider?"

3. "Our high-cost claimant count increased 30% year over year. What clinical programs are engaged with these members?"

4. "Our specialty spend is growing faster than total spend. What is driving this, and what utilization management opportunities exist?"

5. "Our PMPM trend exceeds national benchmarks by 3 points. What explains the difference?"

Good PBMs will have answers. Great PBMs will have already flagged these issues proactively. The best brokers are already asking these questions on your behalf. And evasive responses? Those are informative in their own way.

---

**The Position We Take**

Proactive review beats reactive negotiation.

The employers who get better PBM contracts are the ones who show up with data. I see it every year. They ask specific questions. They identify gaps before the PBM does. They build negotiating positions on evidence rather than assumptions.

Q1 review is not administrative burden. It is leverage creation. The work you do now determines the conversation you have in six months.

If your Q1 data sits in a folder until renewal forces you to look at it, you are ceding positioning to your PBM. They have seen the data. They know where the opportunities and vulnerabilities lie. The question is whether you know too.

Start with the metrics that matter. Follow where they lead. Build your case now.

## The GER Calculation Your PBM Won't Explain

**Publication:** Benefit Blind Spots
**Publish:** Thursday, March 26, 2026 at 7:30 AM EST
**Length:** ~550 words (~3 min read)
**Tone:** Practical, tactical
**Pillar:** PBM Contracts
**Type:** Field Note

---

### Field Note Body

We reviewed a client's Q1 data last week. Their PBM reported a Generic Effective Rate of 89%. The guarantee was 87%. On paper, everything looked fine.

It was not fine.

When we dug into the methodology, the calculation excluded specialty generics. It excluded 90-day fills. It excluded compounds with generic ingredients. We recalculated using the full claims universe, and the actual GER was 83%. Four points below guarantee.

This is not an unusual finding. We see this pattern regularly.

**What GER Actually Measures**

Generic Effective Rate captures the actual cost savings your plan achieves from generic utilization. Unlike Generic Dispensing Rate (which simply measures what percentage of scripts are generic), GER measures the financial impact.

A high GDR with a low GER means you are dispensing generics but not capturing the expected savings. The gap between those two numbers is often where pricing problems hide.

**Where PBMs Adjust the Calculation**

The GER guarantee in your contract likely contains exclusions. These are the ones we encounter most often:

*Specialty Generics:* Many contracts exclude specialty generics from GER calculations. This removes the highest-cost generic category from the math, making the remaining number look better.

*DAW (Dispense As Written) Claims:* When a prescriber or member requests brand when generic is available, the PBM may exclude these from the GER calculation. The logic is reasonable, but the effect is favorable to the PBM.

*90-Day Supply Fills:* Some contracts calculate GER on 30-day equivalents or exclude 90-day fills entirely. Mail-order generics at 90-day supply represent substantial volume.

*MAC Pricing Methodology:* Maximum Allowable Cost lists determine what the plan pays for generics. PBMs set and update MAC lists. The frequency of updates and the benchmarks used directly affect your GER.

**What to Verify in Your Q1 Data**

Three steps you can take this week. None of them require hiring an auditor:

1. *Request the full calculation methodology.* Ask your PBM to provide the exact formula used for GER, including all exclusions. Compare this to your contract language. If the contract says "all generic claims" and the calculation excludes categories, that is a discrepancy worth raising.

2. *Run your own GER.* Pull total generic ingredient cost and total brand ingredient cost from your claims data. Calculate the percentage. Compare to the PBM's reported number. If there is a meaningful gap, the exclusions are the likely explanation.

3. *Check MAC update frequency.* Ask when the MAC list was last updated. MAC lists that are updated infrequently allow pricing to drift from market, meaning your plan pays more than current generic market pricing would require.

**Why This Matters for Renewal**

GER underperformance is one of the strongest negotiation levers available to employers. Your broker or consultant can help you evaluate whether your PBM is truly meeting the guarantee, or whether the guarantee is structured to be easily met through exclusions. Either finding creates leverage for contract improvement.

Document the gap now. Bring it to renewal with specifics.

Something in the PBM market that does not get enough attention: the gap between reported generic rates and actual generic savings.

Generic Dispensing Rate and Generic Effective Rate are different metrics. GDR tells you how many scripts are generic. GER tells you how much you actually saved.

When you review Q1 data, look at both numbers.

**Morning (7-8 AM) — Expertise Insight:**
A contract pattern we keep seeing in Q1 reviews: rebate guarantee structures that are technically being met while delivering less value than expected.

The guarantee says "minimum $X per brand claim." The PBM meets it. But the guarantee was set conservatively. Market rebates for the employer's specific formulary mix are meaningfully higher than the guarantee level.

The guarantee creates a floor. The PBM retains the difference between the floor and actual manufacturer payments.

If your rebate guarantee has not been renegotiated in three or more years, it is almost certainly below market. Your broker or consultant can help you benchmark this.

One angle that did not make today's Field Note on GER calculations:

MAC list update frequency matters more than most employers realize. Maximum Allowable Cost lists determine what the plan pays for generics. When MAC lists are updated infrequently, generic pricing drifts above market.

Some PBMs update MAC lists weekly. Others update monthly or less frequently. The less frequent the updates, the more favorable the pricing is for the PBM.

Your contract probably specifies update frequency. Check whether it is being followed. And check whether the frequency is competitive.

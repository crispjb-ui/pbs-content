# PART 1: SUBSTACK ARTICLE

## H1 Review: What the First Half Data Shows

**Subtitle:** The five metrics that determine your PBM renewal leverage and how to calculate them

**Publication:** Benefit Blind Spots
**Publish:** Monday, June 15, 2026 at 7:30 AM EST
**Length:** ~1,600 words (~6 min read)
**Tone:** Analytical authority
**Pillar:** Transparency

---

### Article Body

Every renewal season, I watch the same pattern. Your PBM has already run your H1 numbers. They know your trend. They know your rebate position. They know where your guarantees fell short. They are using that information right now to prepare their renewal strategy.

Your PBM has had your H1 numbers for weeks. I would bet money you have not looked at them yet.

I bring this up every year because the pattern never changes. We sit down with clients in June or July for H1 review, and the first question is always the same: "What do our numbers look like?" They do not know. Their PBM does. Their stop-loss carrier does. And while their broker or consultant may have pieces of the picture, the employer has not yet done their own independent analysis. The employer, the one writing the checks, should be the most informed person at the table.

That information asymmetry is how employers end up accepting renewal terms instead of negotiating them.

The window between H1 close and renewal season is where leverage gets built or lost. What you learn in June shapes what you negotiate in September. The employers who benchmark now, working with their advisory team but using their own analysis rather than the PBM's summary, negotiate fundamentally different outcomes.

**What your PBM already knows about you**

They know your per-member-per-month trend and how it compares to their book of business. They know which guarantees they are meeting and which ones they are missing. They know your specialty concentration and where your high-cost claimants are trending. They know your rebate position relative to their contracts with manufacturers. They have modeled multiple renewal scenarios and know which terms they can offer while protecting their margins.

You should know all of this too.

Most employers do not. They rely on PBM-generated reports that present the data in the PBM's preferred framing. Running your own analysis from the raw claims data changes the dynamic. It does not require sophisticated tools. It requires pulling five specific metrics, benchmarking them, and walking into renewal with your own numbers rather than theirs.

**The five metrics that matter**

We start every H1 review with five numbers. These are what determine whether you negotiate from strength or react to terms.

**Metric 1: Generic Effective Rate (GER).** This measures the actual discount you receive on generic drugs as a percentage off the benchmark price. Pull total generic drug spend and total generic prescriptions from your claims report. Calculate average cost per generic prescription. Compare to AWP. GER equals AWP minus actual cost, divided by AWP.

For retail 30-day supply, a GER above 85% is competitive. Below 80% warrants investigation. For mail order 90-day supply, above 88% is competitive. If your GER has declined year over year without a change in your generic mix, your pricing may be eroding. The gap between your actual GER and your contract guarantee is one of the most concrete data points you can bring to a renewal table. I have watched that single number shift an entire negotiation.

**Metric 2: PMPM Trend.** This measures how fast your total pharmacy cost is growing, normalized for population size. Divide total pharmacy spend for H1 by total member months, then compare to the same calculation from prior year H1.

Single-digit PMPM growth (under 10%) is within normal range for most plans. Double-digit growth warrants breaking it down into three components: price trend (same drugs costing more), utilization trend (members using more drugs), and mix shift (members moving from lower-cost to higher-cost drugs, including specialty growth and GLP-1 adoption).

Knowing which component drives your trend changes your response entirely. Price trend requires PBM negotiation. Utilization trend requires clinical programs. Mix shift requires formulary strategy. If your trend is above benchmark, something needs to change. If your trend is below benchmark, that is leverage. Use it.

**Metric 3: Specialty as Percentage of Total Spend.** Divide total specialty drug spend by total pharmacy spend. Most self-funded plans see specialty representing 40% to 55% of total spend. Above 55% signals high concentration risk.

Track the trajectory. If specialty percentage is growing more than two to three points per year, cost concentration is accelerating. Key sub-metrics: your top 10 specialty drugs by spend, biosimilar adoption rate within specialty categories, and specialty PMPM trend versus traditional PMPM trend.

**Metric 4: Rebate Per Brand Claim.** Divide total rebates received in H1 by total brand claims in H1. Compare to your PBM's contractual guarantee. Compare year over year.

Rebate per brand claim should be stable or growing. If it is declining while brand drug prices are increasing, your PBM may be retaining a larger share of manufacturer rebates. If brand utilization is declining (because generics are replacing brands, which is good) but total rebates are also declining, look at whether your formulary is optimized for net cost rather than gross cost. We see this disconnect frequently. It is not always obvious from summary reports.

**Metric 5: High-Cost Claimant Count.** Count members with total pharmacy claims exceeding $50,000 in H1 and separately those exceeding $100,000. Compare to prior year counts at the same thresholds. The count itself varies by plan size, but the trend matters: is the number growing?

Cross-reference with your specific stop-loss attachment point. Each member above $100,000 in pharmacy claims is a potential lasering candidate at renewal. Knowing this number before your stop-loss carrier presents renewal terms gives you and your broker time to prepare and evaluate options. Going in without it means you are reacting to terms rather than anticipating them.

**How to use this before your PBM does**

Compile all five metrics with 12 to 24 months of history. Benchmark against available market data. Figure out where your plan is performing well and where the gaps are.

For any metric trending in the wrong direction, determine whether the cause is PBM performance, plan design, population changes, or market dynamics. That distinction matters. It determines who owns the solution and what leverage you carry into the conversation.

Consider what the H1 market looks like right now. GLP-1 oral formulations are available, shifting pricing dynamics and complicating coverage strategy. Specialty continues to grow as a percentage of total spend, with the pipeline bringing additional high-cost therapies. Regulatory and competitive pressure for transparency is increasing, giving employers more leverage for data access than they have had in years. Gene therapy coverage frameworks are developing, as we discussed last week. All of these factors should inform how you read your five metrics and what priorities you set for H2.

Over the past 12 weeks, we have walked through the Visibility Gap across specialty pharmacy, stop-loss coverage, 340B pricing, prior authorization, contract structure, drug pipelines, fiduciary duty, manufacturer programs, compounding, gene therapy, and parity compliance. Every topic points back to the same thing: the gap between what you see and what is actually happening with plan dollars is where cost, risk, and missed leverage live.

H1 review is the moment to close that gap with data. Not your PBM's data presentation. Your data.

**The window is closing**

June is not early. June is late. Every week you wait, the PBM's preparation advantage grows. They are already modeling your renewal position. Already building the narrative for why your terms should look a certain way.

The employers who walk into renewal with their own five-number dashboard negotiate different outcomes than those who walk in with nothing but their PBM's summary report. I see it every year. Same data, completely different conversations depending on who ran the numbers first.

## Five Numbers That Determine Your Renewal Leverage

**Publication:** Benefit Blind Spots

**Length:** ~550 words (~3 min read)
**Tone:** Practical, tactical
**Pillar:** PBM Contracts

### Field Note Body

When we sit down with clients to prepare for PBM renewal negotiations, we start with five numbers. These are what determine whether you negotiate from strength or react to terms.

**1. Generic Effective Rate vs. Guarantee**

Your generic effective rate (GER) is the actual discount you receive on generic medications, expressed as a percentage of AWP. Compare it to your contract guarantee.

If your GER is meeting or exceeding the guarantee, you have confirmation your pricing is performing. If it is falling short, you have a documented shortfall that strengthens your position. That shortfall works as remedy under the current contract or as leverage in renewal discussions.

The gap between actual GER and guarantee is one of the most concrete data points you can bring to a renewal table.

**2. PMPM Trend vs. Benchmark**

Your per-member-per-month cost trend tells you how fast your pharmacy spend is growing. The number alone is not enough. You need to compare it to market benchmarks.

If your trend is above benchmark, something in your plan design, utilization patterns, or PBM management is driving higher cost growth. That is a problem to solve. If your trend is below benchmark, that is leverage. Your plan is performing well and your PBM should be motivated to retain the business.

**3. Specialty PMPM Trajectory**

Specialty pharmacy drives a large share of total drug spend for most plans. Track your specialty PMPM over the past 12 to 24 months and project forward.

Is specialty spend accelerating? Slowing down? Stable? The trajectory matters more than the current number because it tells you where cost pressure is heading. An accelerating specialty trajectory means your renewal should address specialty management programs, network strategies, and clinical criteria.

**4. Rebate Per Brand Claim**

Total rebates divided by total brand claims gives you rebate productivity per claim. Track this over time and compare to contract terms.

A declining rebate per brand claim may point to formulary changes, manufacturer rebate reductions, or mix shifts working against you. If this number is going down while your PBM says rebate performance is strong, that disconnect is worth exploring before renewal.

**5. High-Cost Claimant Count**

The number of members with pharmacy claims exceeding a defined threshold (often $50,000 or $100,000 annually). Track both the count and the total spend associated with these claimants.

High-cost claimant activity drives stop-loss discussions, specialty management strategy, and overall plan volatility. Knowing this number and its trend gives you context for every other renewal conversation.

**How to use these in renewal**

Compile all five metrics with 12 to 24 months of history. Benchmark against available market data. Figure out where your plan is performing well and where the gaps are.

The employers who walk into renewal conversations with these five numbers documented and benchmarked negotiate different terms than those who rely on their PBM's summary reports. Your PBM controls the narrative when you do not have your own data. These five numbers change that.

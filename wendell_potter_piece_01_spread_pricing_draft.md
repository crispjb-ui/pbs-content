## Draft

### The Markup You Were Never Shown: The Case for Mandatory PBM Spread Disclosure

In the Opening piece I laid out the five revenue streams that run through every PBM contract, and promised to argue each reform in turn. This is the first stream, and the cleanest reform to argue: spread pricing.

A wholesaler that charges retail to its customer, pays wholesale to its supplier, and keeps the difference is running an honest business, as long as the customer knows the markup is there. Spread pricing is the same trade with one difference: the customer never sees the markup, and the contract is written to keep it that way.

Here is how it works inside a PBM contract. The plan agrees to reimburse the PBM for each prescription on one pricing schedule. For generic drugs that schedule is usually a MAC list, short for maximum allowable cost. It sounds like an objective benchmark. It is the PBM's own list of ceiling prices, which the PBM sets and revises. The PBM reimburses the dispensing pharmacy on a different schedule. The plan sees its number. The pharmacy sees its number. The PBM sees both and keeps the gap between them. CVS Caremark, Express Scripts, and OptumRx, the three companies (owned by CVS/Aetna, Cigna, and UnitedHealth) that process the large majority of prescriptions in this country, all carry this structure in their commercial contracts. In the agreements I review, those two pricing schedules are almost never printed in the same exhibit, and the contract rarely requires the PBM to report the difference at all. Spread is not hidden because anyone broke the law. It is hidden because the documents you were given do not let you reconstruct it.

The Federal Trade Commission has already documented this. In its January 2025 staff report, the FTC found that the three largest PBMs generated an estimated $1.4 billion from spread pricing, billing plan-sponsor clients more than they reimbursed pharmacies, on a single category of specialty generic drugs over a six-year window. That is spread alone, on one slice of the drug spend, documented by the regulator on the record.

The industry's trade association, PCMA, calls spread pricing "risk transfer." The claim is that a PBM quoting a fixed price to a small or mid-size plan absorbs the risk of drug-cost swings, and the spread is the premium for carrying it. There is a legitimate version of risk transfer. This is not it. When one company sets the price the plan pays, sets the price the pharmacy receives, keeps the difference, and has no contractual obligation to disclose how large it is, it is not carrying risk. It is sitting on both sides of the same trade, with nothing in the agreement requiring it to show the gap.

None of the current transparency rules reach this cleanly. State disclosure laws exist, but they vary widely and stop at state lines, so a plan with members in several states gets a patchwork and a false sense of coverage. For self-funded employer plans, which cover 63 percent of workers with employer health coverage, a federal law known as ERISA preempts much of that state action, and the federal floor that would replace it has not been built. Contract opacity finishes the job. A plan sponsor cannot audit a number the agreement never required the PBM to produce.

It is worth being precise about the kind of problem this is. Spread is not a rogue actor overcharging in the dark. It is a designed feature of a standard contract, signed by sophisticated parties, operating exactly as written. That is why naming a villain does not fix it, and why disclosure does. The fix has to be structural because the problem is built into the contract, not bolted on by someone breaking the rules.

I see spread-pricing language in nearly every contract I audit. Most plan sponsors do not know it is there until someone reads the definitions section out loud.

The fix is narrow and within reach: the PBM should be required to disclose the spread itself, drug by drug, every quarter. Every medication carries a National Drug Code, a unique identifier for that exact product, so the rule can require the spread to be reported for each drug rather than as a blended average that buries the worst cases. For each one, the report would show two numbers, the price the plan paid and the price the pharmacy received. Those reports would be filed with state insurance departments, and plan sponsors would have access to their own data by right rather than by negotiation. The groundwork is already laid. The Federal Trade Commission built the factual record through its multi-year study of the industry, and in 2024 it opened an enforcement case against these same three PBMs, so a disclosure requirement is the logical next step on a track the agency is already traveling. If the agency will not carry it there, the bipartisan Pharmacy Benefit Manager Transparency Act, reintroduced in February 2025 by Senators Grassley and Cantwell, would put the same requirement into law: it bans spread pricing outright and requires PBMs to report what they take through it. The bill is now before the Senate Commerce Committee, and a nearly identical bill cleared that committee last Congress and went no further. The evidence is in hand. What is missing is the order, or the vote.

In my view this is the cleanest reform on the table, and the first one the FTC should move. The agency already did the hard part: it documented how spread works. What remains is to require that it be shown. A markup a customer can see is just a price. The FTC now has both the evidence and the authority to make this one visible, and it should.

Spread hides one number. The next stream hides something larger: the pharmacy that filled the prescription, and the fact that the PBM owns it. That is vertical integration, and it is where the margin moves when spread disclosure finally arrives. The streams are connected by design. The reforms have to be too.

## Fact-check — researched June 2026 (sources below)

1. **FTC spread figure — VERIFIED, now sourced.** Body cites the **$1.4 billion from spread pricing** the Big 3 generated on specialty generic drugs (2017–2022), from the FTC's **Second Interim Staff Report, January 2025**. This is the spread-specific figure (billing plan sponsors more than they reimbursed pharmacies), which keeps the piece precisely on stream #1 and does NOT conflate it with the separate **$7.3 billion** the affiliated pharmacies earned *over acquisition cost* (that markup is owned-pharmacy margin, stream #5, reserved for Piece 9). The first report (July 9, 2024) documented ~$1.6B in excess dispensing revenue on two cancer drugs and the ~80% top-3 prescription share, also available if a second data point is wanted.
2. **Mechanism — REVISED for accuracy + bill named.** The original "FTC final order under Section 5, no new statute required" overstated it: the PBM reports came from the FTC's **6(b) market study** (not enforcement), and a *blanket industry-wide* disclosure mandate via a single Section 5 order is not clean. Revised to the accurate two-track framing: the FTC built the record via the 6(b) study, **opened a Section 5 enforcement case against the three PBMs in 2024** (the GPO/insulin action), and a disclosure requirement is the next step on that track — with the statutory path being the **Pharmacy Benefit Manager Transparency Act of 2025, S.526** (Sen. Grassley R-IA + Sen. Cantwell D-WA, reintroduced Feb 11, 2025). The bill **bans spread pricing and requires PBMs to report spread, fees, clawbacks, and the affiliated-vs-non-affiliated reimbursement difference** — i.e., exactly the disclosure the piece prescribes. Body names the Act + sponsors (cleaner for the genre); "S.526" is here for the editor. **Status (researched June 2026):** S.526 was introduced Feb 11, 2025, read twice and referred to the Senate Committee on Commerce, Science, and Transportation, where it awaits action (no 119th-Congress markup yet). The predecessor (PBM Transparency Act of 2023, S.127, 118th Cong.) was reported out of that same committee and never received a floor vote — hence the body line "a nearly identical bill cleared that committee last Congress and went no further." Re-verify immediately before publication, since committee calendars move.
3. **Self-funded share — VERIFIED.** "63 percent of workers with employer health coverage are in self-funded plans" — **KFF 2024 Employer Health Benefits Survey** (63% of covered workers; 79% at large firms, 20% at small firms).

**Sources:**

# Ginny's Comment & Reply Voice — Net New Value or Silence

**Created:** July 7, 2026 (Brett-requested). This is the rule-of-record for Ginny's comments and replies on LinkedIn (primary), and it unifies the existing Substack Notes reply bar (`substack_engagement_guide.md`) and X patrol bar (`x_account_strategy.md`) under one principle. Any session drafting suggested replies, comment copy, or engagement-patrol language for Ginny reads this file first.

## The diagnosis

The recurring tic: **replies and comments that restate or reframe what was just said.** A commenter makes a point, and the reply opens by agreeing with it and then says the same thing back in different words. Or a reply to a question repeats the question's premise as if that were the answer. Or a comment on someone else's post summarizes their post back to them.

Why it happens: it is the path of least resistance (validation feels polite, echoing feels responsive), and it is the default output shape of AI-assisted drafting (validate, mirror, elaborate). Why it hurts:

1. **It reads awkward and hollow.** The commenter already knows what they said. The audience already read the post. An echo tells everyone the reply exists to exist.
2. **It burns the credibility the posts earn.** Ginny's posts win because every one carries an unfakeable detail. A reply thread full of restatements is the opposite signal: it reads like engagement-bait or a ghostwriter, and sophisticated readers (the CFOs, brokers, and journalists PBS actually wants) notice the gap between the post voice and the reply voice.
3. **It wastes the thread.** Comment threads are where the post's reach converts to relationship. Every reply slot filled with an echo is a slot that could have carried a second unfakeable detail.

## The core rule

**Every comment and every reply adds at least one thing that was not already in the post or the comment being answered. If there is nothing to add, do not comment. A like is a complete response.**

"Something to add" means one of the six moves below. Politeness is not a move. Agreement is not a move. Rephrasing is not a move.

## The six reply moves (pick one per reply)

1. **Go one level deeper.** The mechanism behind the mechanism. The post said what happens; the reply says why, or what happens next.
   > *"And the reason the definition matters so much: the guarantee is reconciled annually in aggregate, so a narrow definition doesn't just trim each claim, it changes which claims count at all."*

2. **Add the field example.** A specific thing PBS has seen that the post did not use. This is the highest-value move and the most Ginny-sounding one.
   > *"We reviewed a contract last quarter where this exact clause showed up under 'Program Optimization.' Same mechanism, friendlier name."*

3. **Draw the distinction.** Where the point holds and where it flips. Nuance beats agreement.
   > *"True for traditional pricing. Under a cost-plus arrangement this particular gap closes, but a new one opens in the specialty list, which is why we read that exhibit first."*

4. **Ask the genuinely curious question.** Only when actually curious, and specific enough that the answer would teach Ginny something. Never a rhetorical question, never a question that restates their point with a question mark.
   > *"When your team sees that language, does it show up in the base agreement or get introduced at renewal? We almost always find it in amendments."*

5. **Concede and refine.** For pushback. Give the commenter their real point in a few words, then add the part they are missing. The concession is one clause, not a paragraph of validation.
   > *"The negotiating leverage is real. The question I look at is who keeps it: when rebates are defined narrowly, the leverage got exercised and the result stayed with the intermediary."*

6. **The human beat.** A one-line personal or warm response where warmth is the honest reaction. Allowed precisely because it does not pretend to add analysis.
   > *"This comment made my week. Thank you."*

If none of the six fits: **like the comment and move on.** Like-only is the default response to praise.

## Banned reply patterns (the mirror list)

These opener shapes are retired from Ginny's replies. Each one signals an echo is coming:

- "Exactly right — [restatement of their point]"
- "This is such an important point about [their point]"
- "You're spot on that [their point]"
- "Great question! [reframe of the question as the answer]"
- "Thank you! [restatement of her own post]"
- "As I mentioned in the post..." (self-echo; if the post already said it, the reply is done before it starts)
- "Couldn't agree more. [their point, reworded]"
- Any reply whose first sentence could be deleted with no loss of meaning
- Ending every reply with a question to force engagement (questions are move 4, used when genuinely curious, not a closing tic)

**The delete test (run on every drafted reply):** cover the first sentence. If the reply still works, cut the first sentence. Then ask: does what remains contain something that was not already on the screen? If not, don't post it.

## Decision tree by comment type

| The comment is... | The response |
|---|---|
| **A real question** | Answer it with specifics (moves 1, 2, or 3). Never restate the question. If the full answer is long, give the sharpest piece and offer the rest: "The short version is X. Happy to go deeper if useful." |
| **An added data point / expert build** | Build on it one level (move 1 or 3), or ask the curious question (move 4). These commenters are peers and potential relationships; this is where reply effort concentrates. |
| **Praise, nothing to build on** | Like it. Occasionally move 6 (one warm line, no echo). Never thanks-plus-restatement. |
| **Pushback, good faith** | Move 5: concede the real part in one clause, add the missing part. One reply, clinical, no thread war. If they respond well, continue; if it degrades, stop replying (the audience saw the one good answer, which is the point). |
| **Pushback, bad faith / troll** | No reply. Do not feed it. The confrontation lives in the posts, not the comments. |
| **A lead signal** (a CFO, HR leader, or broker asking about their own situation) | Answer generously in-thread first (move 2 or 3, real value in public), then: "Happy to talk through your specific setup if useful, feel free to DM me." Never route to a link in a reply; the generosity IS the pitch. |
| **A wrong claim that could mislead the audience** | Correct it politely with the contract-level fact (move 3), no adjectives about the commenter. Ginny corrects the record, not the person. |

## The same rule when Ginny comments on OTHERS' posts

The engagement patrol (LinkedIn amplifiers, adjacent experts, target-account people) follows the identical bar, with one addition: **never summarize their post back to them.** The author knows what they wrote. Open inside the addition:

- Bad: *"Great breakdown of how rebate aggregators work. It really is true that the GPO layer takes a cut before pass-through begins."*
- Good: *"The piece of this we see in contracts: the pass-through guarantee is defined at the PBM level, so the aggregator's retention sits outside the promise entirely. The words are doing exactly what they were hired to do."*

2-4 sentences, one pharmacy-benefits-specific insight nobody else in the thread would have, proprietary anchor on at least half (per the existing X patrol bar, now applied to LinkedIn too). If the post is outside Ginny's lane and no genuine addition exists, like it or skip it. Presence without value is worse than absence.

## The broader tic watchlist (the "I'm sure there are others")

Same discipline, adjacent tics to catch in ALL reply/comment drafting (several are already banned in post copy by the Humanize Check; this extends them to threads):

1. **Validate-then-echo** — the core tic, covered above.
2. **Over-thanking and exclamation stacking** — one thank-you maximum, exclamation marks rare. Enthusiasm in threads reads as substance, not punctuation.
3. **Hedging doublets** — "really, truly," "just," "I think maybe." Ginny's post voice is declarative; her reply voice should be too.
4. **The closing CTA tic** — replies never carry links or promos. The designated first comment is the only link slot on Ginny's own posts (existing rule); replies to commenters are relationship space, not funnel space.
5. **Uniform reply length** — if every reply is the same two-sentence shape, the thread reads templated. Some replies should be one line, some five. Arrhythmic reads as human (same principle as the post-level rule).
6. **Answering the easy comment and skipping the hard one** — the substantive question sitting unanswered while praise gets thanked is a visible pattern. Reply priority is exactly inverted: hard/substantive first, praise last or like-only.
7. **AI-tell phrases in replies** — the existing blacklist ("Here's the thing," "The truth is," "Most people don't realize," "It's not X. It's Y.") applies in threads, where it is even more conspicuous because replies are presumed spontaneous.

## Mechanics and cadence

- **Priority window:** replies to substantive comments land within ~2 hours of the post going live where feasible (the window where the algorithm is still deciding distribution and commenters are still online). After that, reply on Ginny's schedule; a good reply at hour 20 beats an echo at minute 10.
- **Not every comment gets a reply.** Target: every real question answered, every expert build engaged, praise liked. A thread where Ginny replied to 6 of 15 comments with substance looks better than 15-of-15 with filler.
- **One reply per commenter per thread by default.** Extended back-and-forths move to DMs when they are leads, and simply end when they are debates.
- **X and Substack:** this file's six moves and banned list apply on top of the existing channel bars (`x_account_strategy.md` reply-vs-quote-post rule; `substack_engagement_guide.md` reply checklist). Where those files' example reply patterns open with agreement-echoes, the delete test now governs.

## Worked before/after examples

**Praise:** *"This is why every CFO needs to read their PBM contract. Great breakdown!"*
- Before (tic): "Thank you! It really is amazing how few CFOs read the contract before signing. The definitions section is where everything gets decided." *(restates her own post)*
- After: "Thank you. One practical add for any CFO starting this week: request the contract in Word, not PDF. The tracked-changes history often shows you what other plans negotiated out." *(move 2: net-new tactical detail)*
- Also correct: just like it.

**Question:** *"How often do audit clauses restrict which auditor you can use?"*
- Before (tic): "Great question! Auditor-selection restrictions are really common and they can seriously undermine your audit rights." *(reframes the question as the answer)*
- After: "Most contracts we review restrict it somehow: 'mutually agreeable auditor,' a named-firm list, or a no-contingency-fee rule that quietly excludes most PBM specialists. The strongest language says 'auditor of the plan's choosing' with a confidentiality agreement as the only condition." *(moves 2+3: frequency, the specific forms, the protective language)*

**Pushback:** *"PBMs negotiate discounts employers could never get alone. This framing ignores the value they create."*
- Before (tic): "You're absolutely right that PBMs create real value through their negotiating power. But the issue is transparency around where that value goes." *(paragraph-length validation, then vague)*
- After: "The negotiating leverage is real. What I look at is who keeps the result: when the contract defines rebates narrowly, the leverage got exercised and the proceeds stayed with the intermediary. Both things are true at once, and only one of them is disclosed." *(move 5: one-clause concession, then the missing piece)*

**Expert build:** *"We see the same thing in stop-loss: the pharmacy carve-out language decides more than the premium does."*
- Before (tic): "Yes! The carve-out language really is more important than the premium in so many cases." *(pure echo)*
- After: "Agreed, and the two interact: a gene-therapy exclusion in the stop-loss policy plus mandatory PBM specialty routing means the plan holds the exact tail risk it thought it insured. When you review those policies, do you see the exclusions in the base form or the endorsements?" *(move 1 + move 4: deeper mechanism, genuine question that opens a relationship)*

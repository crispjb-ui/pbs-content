# PART 1: SUBSTACK ARTICLE

## The Questions Your Pharmacy Data Should Answer

**Publication:** Benefit Blind Spots
**Publish:** Monday, March 2, 2026 at 7:30 AM EST
**Length:** ~1,500 words (~6 min read)
**Tone:** Analytical authority
**Pillar:** Self-Funded Guidance

---

### Article Body

I sat down with a plan sponsor last month to review their pharmacy data. They had three years of claims information from their PBM. They had never opened most of it.

That is not unusual. Most employers barely scratch the surface of their pharmacy claims data.

The data tells stories: where money is going, which members need intervention, what is working, what is not. But data without questions is just numbers. And the questions your PBM asks on your behalf are not necessarily the questions that serve your interests.

For self-funded employers, the gap between data available and data utilized represents one of the largest missed opportunities in pharmacy benefit management.

This issue covers the questions worth asking, and how to turn answers into action.

---

**The Problem With Standard PBM Reports**

Your PBM sends quarterly reports. You file them.

Standard reports typically include: total spend, PMPM trends, generic dispensing rates, top drugs by utilization. This information is not wrong. It is incomplete.

Standard reports answer questions the PBM chooses. They rarely answer:

- Why did this drug spike? (Root cause)
- Who is driving this trend? (Member-level patterns)
- Are we getting what we negotiated? (Contract performance verification)
- What should we change? (Actionable recommendations)

The employers who extract value from pharmacy data do not wait for reports. They bring questions.

---

**Five Questions Worth Asking**

*Question 1: Where is the money actually going?*

Not just top drugs by utilization; top drugs by SPEND. These lists often differ significantly.

A drug prescribed to 50 members at $20,000 each drives more plan cost than a drug prescribed to 5,000 members at $50 each. Yet standard reports emphasize utilization counts. (Illustrative example for educational purposes. Actual amounts vary by plan.)

Drill down:
- Top 20 drugs by total spend
- Specialty vs. traditional pharmacy split
- Trend vs. prior period (same drugs, or new entrants?)
- Manufacturer concentration (how much flows to top 3-5 drug makers?)

*Question 2: Who needs help?*

I want to be clear on this one: high-cost claimant identification is not about penalizing members. It is about ensuring the most expensive members receive the most coordinated care.

Identify:
- Members exceeding $50,000 annual pharmacy spend
- Rising risk members (spend increasing quarter over quarter)
- Adherence gaps (fills dropping off for chronic conditions)
- Polypharmacy cases (10+ concurrent medications)

Early intervention in specialty categories (oncology, autoimmune, HIV) can improve outcomes and reduce downstream medical costs. But you cannot intervene on members you have not identified.

*Question 3: Are we getting what we paid for?*

Your PBM contract includes guarantees: discount levels, rebate amounts, generic rates. The only way to know if these are being met is to verify.

Compare:
- Actual discounts vs. guaranteed discounts (by drug type)
- Rebate payments vs. rebate projections
- Generic dispensing rate vs. target
- Generic effective rate (actual generic savings captured)

Variance in any direction warrants investigation. Outperformance may indicate conservative projections. Underperformance may indicate contract non-compliance.

*Question 4: What patterns suggest opportunity?*

Data reveals patterns that suggest intervention points:

- Brand medications with available generics (therapeutic substitution opportunities)
- Specialty medications filled at retail vs. specialty pharmacy (site of care optimization)
- Mail order eligible drugs filled at retail (channel optimization)
- High-cost drugs with lower-cost therapeutic alternatives (formulary optimization)

Each pattern represents potential savings. But patterns invisible in aggregate reports become visible in claims-level analysis.

*Question 5: What should we change?*

Analysis without action is academic exercise. Every data review should conclude with:

- Formulary changes to consider
- Clinical program adjustments
- Contract leverage points for renewal
- Member communication opportunities
- Benefit design modifications

If your data review does not produce a to-do list, you are not asking enough questions.

---

**Building an Analytic Cadence**

How often should you review pharmacy data? The answer depends on plan size and complexity, but in our work with plan sponsors, we have found that minimum standards exist.

*Monthly:*
- High-cost claimant monitoring
- Trend vs. budget tracking
- Any unusual spikes investigation

*Quarterly:*
- Full claims analysis
- Contract guarantee verification
- Clinical program performance
- Formulary effectiveness review

*Annually:*
- Comprehensive benchmarking
- Renewal preparation analysis
- Multi-year trend assessment
- Strategic planning input

Many employers conduct annual reviews only. This means 11 months of operating blind between insights. Problems that could have been addressed in Q1 compound through Q4.

---

**The Data Access Question**

You cannot analyze data you cannot access.

Review your PBM contract for data provisions:

*Claims data:* Do you receive member-level claims data, or only aggregate summaries? Member-level data enables the analyses described above. Aggregate data does not.

*Frequency:* How often is data delivered? Monthly is standard. Quarterly is insufficient for proactive management.

*Format:* Is data delivered in formats that enable analysis (flat files, database exports) or only in PDF reports?

*Lag:* What is the delay between claim processing and data availability? 30 days is reasonable. 90 days undermines timely intervention.

*Rebate detail:* Can you see rebate amounts by drug, or only totals? Drug-level detail enables verification. Totals require trust.

If your contract limits data access, this should be a negotiation priority at renewal. Your broker or consultant can help you evaluate what data provisions to push for.

---

**Turning Data Into Negotiating Power**

Pharmacy data analysis is not just operational management. It is renewal preparation.

The employers who negotiate better PBM contracts are the ones who show up with data. The best brokers and consultants are already helping their clients do this.

Here is what that sounds like in a renewal meeting:

- "Our generic effective rate has averaged 2% below guarantee for six quarters. Here is the analysis."
- "These five specialty drugs represent 30% of spend. What are your rebate terms for each?"
- "Our high-cost claimant count increased 40% while total membership increased 5%. What clinical programs address this?"

Data transforms negotiation from opinion exchange to evidence-based discussion. PBMs respond differently to employers who demonstrate analytical sophistication.

---

**The Position We Take**

Pharmacy claims data is an asset most employers underutilize. I see it in nearly every engagement we take on.

The gap between data available and data utilized exists because PBMs have limited incentive to highlight unfavorable findings, employers lack bandwidth for independent analysis, and standard reports answer comfortable questions rather than hard ones.

We advise employers to treat pharmacy data with the same rigor applied to financial audits. The dollars at stake justify the investment. And the insights available can strengthen both operational management and strategic positioning.

Data without questions is just numbers. Start with better questions.

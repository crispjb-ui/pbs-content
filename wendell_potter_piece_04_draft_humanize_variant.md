## Draft (Humanize Variant)

### The Conflict of Interest at the Heart of Copay Accumulator Programs

Picture a charitable foundation that gives scholarships to college students. Easy enough. Now picture the same foundation hiring an administrator to process the scholarships. Also easy. Now picture the administrator also owning the college, deciding which courses qualify for the scholarship money, and deciding whether that scholarship counts toward the student's tuition bill or just toward the college's revenue. Same dollars. Different beneficiary depending on how the administrator counts them. One entity controlling every decision in the chain.

That's roughly the structural problem at the heart of copay accumulator and maximizer programs in employer health plans. Most of the reform conversation has focused on whether the programs should exist. I think the harder question is who gets to run them.

Quick refresher, in case the terminology's confusing. A copay accumulator program redirects the manufacturer's copay-assistance dollars away from the patient's annual deductible and toward the plan's expense column. The patient still hands over the coupon at the pharmacy; the coupon still covers the claim; but the dollars never credit toward the deductible the patient owes for the year. A copay maximizer is the same mechanic with calibrated math, designed to extract the maximum coupon value across twelve months of refills.

Patients on specialty drugs feel these programs hardest. Members who'd been managing their out-of-pocket exposure with coupons can find themselves looking at four-figure balances they didn't expect. That's the affordability cliff.

Plan sponsors get something different out of these programs. When the design is right and the disclosure is real, accumulator programs are a defensible cost-containment lever. Manufacturer dollars that would otherwise reduce a specialty patient's exposure instead reduce the plan's specialty spend. Done responsibly, that's just program design.

For the PBM administering the program, though, the economics are a third matter entirely. And that's where I want to spend the rest of this piece.

Here's what I see in nearly every PBM contract I audit. The accumulator program isn't run by some neutral third party. It's run by the same PBM that owns the specialty pharmacy filling the drug. That same PBM also writes the formulary that decides whether the drug is accumulator-eligible in the first place. SaveOnSP is owned by Express Scripts. PrudentRx is owned by CVS Caremark. Variable Copay is owned by OptumRx. **Three roles. One vendor.**

That's the structural problem. Not the existence of these programs. The way the PBM gets to wear three hats at once.

Think about what that means in practice. The PBM decides which drugs qualify for accumulator treatment, and the formulary placement decisions don't have to follow clinical criteria. They can follow whichever drug has the biggest manufacturer assistance dollars on the table. The PBM also decides which pharmacy fills those drugs, and the routing tends to favor the PBM-owned specialty pharmacy. And the PBM runs the math on whether the manufacturer dollars credit the member's deductible, the plan's expense, or (increasingly) neither. The same vendor calling every shot. No independent voice in the room to push back.

Twenty-six states plus DC and Puerto Rico have actually moved on this. Most of those laws require manufacturer copay assistance to count toward member cost-sharing in fully insured plans. The state laws get the patient-protection half of this story right. The structural conflict-of-interest half? They miss it almost entirely.

And then there's ERISA. Most state insurance law gets preempted for self-insured employer plans, which happens to be the majority of employer-sponsored coverage in this country. So those state protections reach a minority of members, and the structural conflict at the PBM-administrator level keeps operating in the bigger market without any meaningful regulatory pressure.

Most of the reform proposals I read reach for prohibition. Ban the program. Force every manufacturer assistance dollar to credit the member's deductible. End of conversation. I get the impulse, but in my view it misses the legitimate use case.

Plan sponsors should keep the right to choose these programs as a cost-containment lever, with two conditions. First, the program gets disclosed to members at enrollment, in plain language, with the affordability consequences named clearly. Second, the PBM administering the program can't also own the specialty pharmacy or write the formulary that defines program eligibility. Pick a lane. Run the program or own the pharmacy. Not both.

That's the reform that matches the actual problem. The issue isn't that plans use these programs. It's that one entity gets to profit from the program while also controlling every other variable in the transaction.

Three named actions, ordered from politically easiest to politically hardest.

The first is a federal disclosure rule. Any plan that uses an accumulator or maximizer program has to tell its members about it at enrollment, in plain language, with the affordability consequences spelled out. If HHS can issue this as sub-regulatory guidance under existing authority, that's the fastest path. If it needs an ERISA amendment, that's a heavier lift but still achievable.

The second is the structural firewall. A PBM that administers an accumulator or maximizer program shouldn't also be allowed to own the specialty pharmacy dispensing the eligible drugs. This is the conflict-of-interest reform. It needs federal statute. It's politically harder. It also moves the most dollars.

The third is a formulary-design firewall. A PBM can't define a drug as accumulator-eligible just because the manufacturer is offering big assistance dollars. Eligibility has to follow clinical criteria, documented and auditable by the plan sponsor. This one is the cleanest legally and the toughest to enforce in practice.

Honestly, I think this is the most under-named reform in the entire PBM economy right now. The reform conversation has gotten stuck. Patient advocates want accumulator programs banned outright. Plan sponsors want to keep them as a cost lever. Both sides are arguing past the structural problem sitting between them.

Manufacturer copay cards are a legitimate patient affordability tool. They were designed to help patients afford specialty drugs, and they often do exactly that. Plan-sponsor accumulator programs can be a legitimate cost-containment lever. When the disclosure is honest and the design is fair, plans capture some of the dollars manufacturers were prepared to spend on access anyway. Neither of those is the villain.

The villain is the structure. One vendor wearing three hats. One entity profiting from the program while controlling formulary placement, pharmacy routing, and the crediting math at the same time. That's the conflict of interest. That's the part the reform has to address.

The firewall. The disclosure. Not the prohibition.

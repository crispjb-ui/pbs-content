## 1. The headline finding — the *shape* of the edit

The edit is not uniform tightening. It is two opposite motions applied to two different parts of the piece:

| Part of the piece | Direction of the edit | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **The body (the five revenue-stream explanations)** | **EXPANDED.** More plain-English explanation, more teacherly connectives, mechanics walked through step by step. | The reader (Congress, staff, employers with light PBM familiarity) has to follow the mechanic cold. Generous explanation is the job of the body. |
| **The five reform previews** | **STRIPPED.** From dense paragraphs loaded with bill names, sponsors, FTC case history, statute glosses → down to 2-3 plain declarative sentences each, stating only the reform. | The map *previews*; it does not *argue*. The legislative detail belongs in Pieces 1-5, not in the map. |
| **The close (meta-argument + client bridge)** | **DELETED outright.** | Too composed, too columnist, and the client pivot belongs in PBS cascade content, not the Wendell piece. |

**One sentence:** *Explain the plumbing generously and plainly; state the reforms briefly; cut the cleverness and the client homework; end flat and forward-pointing.*

## 2. Section-by-section forensic comparison

### Opener — UNCHANGED (this is the model)
The tollbooth metaphor and "That is the modern Pharmacy Benefit Manager (PBM) business model. It is not a metaphor stretched. It is the contract." survived **completely untouched** (only change: PBM spelled out on first use). 
**Lesson:** The metaphor-led opener with short, declarative *complete* sentences is exactly right and is the one place clipped punch is welcome. The "no fragments" rule never applied to short complete sentences; it applied to noun-phrase lists standing in for sentences. Keep writing openers like this.

### Intro paragraph — light grounding added
- "clearly named: the administrative fee, which is the smallest" — colon-reveal softened to a relative clause.
- "the plan sponsor, **usually an employer**, has to know" — the *role* glossed, not just acronyms.
- "The reform conversation **so far, in both Washington and the states**, has been a fight…" — the abstract claim pinned to concrete venues.
**Lesson:** Gloss **roles and venues** in stride (comma-bracketed appositives), the same way you gloss acronyms. Ground abstract claims in a place or an actor.

### Spread pricing — light precision + acronym gloss
- "what the **health** plan pays" (which plan), "the pharmacy **that fills it**."
- "**impossible** to line up" (final intensified "hard").
- "Maximum Allowable Cost, or MAC, schedule… the PBM's own list of ceiling prices that it sets and revises **at will**."
- "Federal Trade Commission (FTC)."
**Lesson:** Parenthetical acronym gloss on first use; small precision adds ("health," "that fills it," "at will") that remove ambiguity for a cold reader.

### Rebate retention — heavily EXPANDED (the clearest signal in the whole piece)
Original (repo): four sentences. Final: a full teaching paragraph that adds —
- the *mechanism of the dodge*: "exclusionary language in the pharmacy contract that states these are not considered 'rebates.' **Simply put, they do not define the hidden revenue sources as a rebate.**"
- a plain-English definition of GPOs: "**(also commonly referred to as aggregators)**… act like a **buying club**. They combine drug claims from many plan sponsors and use this **economy of scale** to get the best rebate deals."
- the harm, plainly: "the rebate **passes through a lot of hands** including the GPOs and PBMs before it reaches the plan sponsor… the amount being withheld is not disclosed."
- "Premier" deleted from the aggregator list (factual precision — it is a hospital GPO, not a rebate aggregator); parents/names: "the big three PBMs, (Express Scripts, CVS Caremark and OptumRx) owns or is affiliated with a rebate aggregator."
**Lesson:** When a stream is unfamiliar, **spend words explaining it in everyday terms** ("buying club," "economy of scale," "passes through a lot of hands"). Plain teacherly connectives — "Simply put," "The problem is that" — are *welcome* in this register. They are not on the AI-tell blacklist; that blacklist targets fake-profundity openers ("Here's the thing," "The truth is"), not plain explanation.

### Administrative fees — expanded to complete sentences (as the redline predicted)
Fragments ("Per-claim, per-member-per-month… Five definitions the PBM wrote.") became full prose: "They show up as per-claim, per employee per month, or per member per month charges, and fees for formulary management, clinical programs, and network access. The definitions behind them are all written by the PBM."
**Lesson:** Confirmed — noun-phrase lists become sentences. (Note the final says "the **pharmacy contract**" not "the agreement" — Ginny's plainer house term.)

### Manufacturer-direct payments — expanded list, plainer
"can include formulary placement and education, data licensing, **price protection payments**, and manufacturer service contracts… the plan sponsor sees the **downstream impact** in things like preferred formulary placement."
**Lesson:** Plain outcome language ("downstream impact") over compressed phrasing.

### Owned-pharmacy margin — parents named (as redline showed)
"CVS Caremark owns CVS Specialty. **UnitedHealth's** OptumRx owns Optum Specialty **and Cigna's** Express Scripts owns Accredo."
**Lesson:** Confirmed — name parents at first mention; never "Big 3."

### "That is the structural picture" — kept, plain.

### Fragmented-reform paragraph — REWRITTEN from parallel-staccato to teacherly
Repo draft: "Ban spread, and the margin moves into rebate retention. Guarantee rebate pass-through, and it moves into manufacturer-direct payments. Cap admin fees, and it moves into owned-pharmacy routing."
Final: "**For example, if** spread pricing is banned, the margin will just move into rebate retention. **Or if** a requirement is made to pass-through all rebates, the revenue is rerouted to manufacturer-direct payments that are not classified as rebates. **Each well intended reform risks moving the revenue margin around rather than reducing or eliminating it altogether.**"
**Lesson:** Even a *good* parallel-structure rhetorical run reads as "designed." The final replaces it with "For example… Or if…" and a plain summarizing sentence. **Engineered parallelism is a tell; plain example-driven explanation is the house voice.**

### The five reform previews — STRIPPED to plain previews (the biggest structural change)
The repo draft's reforms each ran a dense paragraph with FTC enforcement history, the PBM Transparency Act + Grassley/Cantwell, ERISA gloss, GLP-1 test case, threshold-trigger mechanics. **All cut.** Final versions, each 2-3 plain sentences under a plain header:
- **Spread Pricing** — "disclose the markup itself, at the drug level, in a report filed each quarter with state insurance departments… made available to a plan sponsor without negotiation."
- **Vertical integration** — "operate behind a firewall that separates pricing decisions from the ownership of the pharmacies they pay. The plan sponsor should have the right to audit how prescriptions are routed."
- **Drug Pricing** (was "high-cost drug categories") — "the Secretary of Health and Human Services should have the power to require PBMs to fully disclose… the actual cost of a drug after rebates."
- **Copay Accumulator Programs** — disclose how they operate; bar a PBM from running an accumulator inside a pharmacy it owns.
- **Medical versus Pharmacy Billing** (was "channel steering and white-bagging") — define the practice, then "provide a side-by-side comparison of what a drug costs through each channel."
**Lesson:** In the **map**, a reform is *previewed in plain words*, not argued. No bill numbers, no sponsor names, no statute history, no enforcement-track narrative. Headers use the **reader-facing label** ("Drug Pricing," "Medical versus Pharmacy Billing"), not the insider term. Save every piece of legislative detail for the dedicated argument piece.

### The close — DELETED and replaced with a flat forward-point
Cut entirely: the "What I would argue… PCMA has spent a decade… Naming the structural picture is the precondition for reform that holds" paragraph **and** the "For self-funded plan sponsors reading this… Pull your contract… the audit argument I make for clients" paragraph.
Replaced with: "These are the five reforms that are needed in order to push toward transparency for plan sponsors. The pieces that follow will describe these in greater detail."
**Lesson:** Two hard ones below (Rules 7 and 8).

## 3. The four rules the old 6-rule digest missed

The original 6 (light reader / gloss acronyms / explain instruments / name parents / complete sentences / plain words) stand. Add these four, which are the ones the final piece actually turned on:

**Rule 7 — The map previews; the argument pieces argue. Keep reform statements to 2-3 plain sentences in any "map" or overview piece.** No bill names, no sponsors, no statute history, no enforcement-track narrative in a preview. (This directly corrects the repo draft, which front-loaded all of it.) In a single-issue argument piece, the detail belongs — but it goes in *that* piece, once.

**Rule 8 — Cut the cleverness, including your best engineered line.** The final deleted a *designated pull-quote* ("Naming the structural picture is the precondition for reform that holds") and a tidy parallel run ("Ban spread, and the margin moves…"). Engineered quotability and parallel structure read as composed/AI in this register. Plain, example-driven explanation is the voice. **The pull-quote-engineering habit from PBS social content does not travel here.** (Reservoir note: pull-quotes for the cascade can still be *lifted* from plain sentences the piece already contains; do not *write toward* a quotable line.)

**Rule 9 — Stay at the policy register the whole way. Do not pivot to client homework.** The "pull your contract / map the five streams / the audit argument I make for clients" bridge was cut. The Wendell piece argues reform for policymakers; the PBS cascade week does the employer-action operationalization. Keeping both in the same piece both dilutes the register *and* burns the PBS reservoir (strategy guardrail #5). One audience per piece.

**Rule 10 — End flat and forward-pointing; explain generously in the body.** Two motions: (a) the body *expands* with plain teaching language ("buying club," "economy of scale," "Simply put," "For example") — generous explanation is correct, not padding; (b) the ending is a plain signpost, not a mic-drop. Even word choice cools down ("argue" → "describe").

## 4. Voice calibration (important, because it cuts against PBS-channel instinct)

| PBS social / LinkedIn instinct | Wendell-channel final voice |
|---|---|
| Engineer a quotable, declarative payoff line | Cut it; let a plain sentence be quotable if it happens to be |
| Parallel triads for rhythm ("Ban X… Guarantee Y… Cap Z…") | "For example, if X… Or if Y…" + a plain summary sentence |
| Compress for punch | Expand for comprehension; punch lives only in the opener |
| One stat as hook | Mechanism explained in everyday terms; stats live in the argument pieces |
| Avoid all explainer connectives | "Simply put," "The problem is that," "For example," "Though" are fine |
| End on a confrontation / CTA | End on a flat forward-point ("the pieces that follow will describe these") |

**The AI-tell blacklist still holds** ("Here's the thing," "The truth is," "Most people don't realize," "paradigm," "game-changer," "at the end of the day," "It's not X. It's Y." with em-dashes) — but note the distinction the final draws: plain *explanatory* connectives are not tells. The tell is *fake profundity*, not *teaching*.

## 6. Process implications

1. **Pick the piece type first and draft to it.** A *map/overview* piece previews reforms in 2-3 plain sentences each (Rule 7). A *single-issue argument* piece carries the full legislative detail — once, in that piece. Do not write a map like an argument.
2. **Draft the body long and plain, the previews short and plain, the close flat.** Budget words *toward* explaining mechanics and *away* from reform scaffolding and rhetorical closes.
3. **Run the "cold staffer" gate** (already in the strategy doc): read as a Congressional staffer who has read one prior PBM story. Add the new gate: **read as Wendell** — if a term would make *him* write "What is this? I don't know" (his actual margin note on "Section 5 authority"), it is dead.
4. **Run a "kill your darlings" pass.** Find the most quotable, most parallel, most composed sentence in the draft and ask whether it is teaching or performing. If performing, cut or flatten it. The final piece cut its own best engineered line.
5. **Keep the client bridge out.** If a "here's what you should do, employers" instinct shows up, move it to the cascade-week PBS doc, not the piece.

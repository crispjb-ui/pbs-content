# Sales Conversation Playbook — discovery calls and objections, in Ginny's voice

**Created:** July 5, 2026. The gap this fills: every content surface has codified voice; the call has none, and the call is where the primary KPI (qualified conversations) converts or dies. These are floors, not scripts: Ginny should sound like Ginny. The 45-minute walk-through with her (gameplan item 24) makes each answer hers before September call volume arrives.

**The posture (never deviates):** clinical, generous, unhurried. We are not convincing anyone; we are helping them see their own contract clearly. The prospect should leave the call smarter whether or not they buy. That IS the sales strategy, the same one the content runs on.

## 1. The discovery call (30 minutes, five moves)

**Move 1 — their situation, fully, first (10-12 min).** Open with: *"Tell me where things stand with your pharmacy benefit, and what made this worth a call."* Then listen. Follow-ups that open people up: "When did you last see the actual contract, not the summary?" · "What does your broker say about the pharmacy line?" (always respectful of the broker) · "What happens on your renewal date?" · "Who besides you cares about this number?"

**Move 2 — one insight, free, specific to them (5 min).** Give real value from what they said: the definition to check, the report to request, the window that is closer than it feels. One insight, fully given. This is the Humanize Check in verbal form: something only a person who reviews hundreds of contracts a year would say.

**Move 3 — the honest fit read (3 min).** Say plainly whether a review makes sense: *"From what you have described, here is what I would do in your seat."* If the answer is "you do not need us yet," say so; those calls generate the best referrals we get.

**Move 4 — the shape of an engagement (5 min).** Only if fit is real: what the review covers, the written findings with dollar magnitude per gap, the Fiduciary File, the timeline (4-6 weeks; Renewal Second Opinion in days), and how the recommendation lands (optimize through amendments, or go to market). Price posture per the pricing memo: state the range without apology, anchored to the average findings, and move on.

**Move 5 — one concrete next step (2 min).** Never "think it over." Either: the data request goes out today (we supply the paste-ready version), a date for the committee conversation, or a named check-in tied to their renewal calendar. The admin is cc'd on the recap the same day.

## 2. Objection handling (the seven that matter)

**"We already have a broker / consultant we trust."**
> Good. Keep them; the best engagements we do are the ones a strong broker is part of. We are not a broker and we do not place anything, so there is nothing for us to compete over. We do one narrow thing: the clinical and contract-language layer of pharmacy, behind your broker, not instead of them. Most brokers we work with bring us in themselves, because it makes their renewal recommendation stronger. Would it be useful if the three of us talked?
*(Never a syllable that implies the broker missed something. The gap is structural: pharmacy contract language is a specialty, and everyone in the room knows it.)*

**"Our PBM says we're getting a great deal / we're compliant."**
> They may be right. But notice who is grading the homework. The guarantee they are pointing to is measured by definitions they wrote, in reporting they produce. An independent read either confirms what they told you, which is worth having in your fiduciary file, or it finds the gap between the summary and the contract. Either outcome is worth knowing before you renew. In our experience the summary and the contract rarely say quite the same thing.

**"We just did an RFP / market check [recently]."**
> That is genuinely good discipline, and I would not redo it. Two things an RFP does not do, though: it prices the contract you signed, not the contract as it behaves, and the drift between those two starts the day the ink dries. And an RFP is a point in time; the definitions, exclusion lists, and program terms amend along the way. A review two years after an RFP is usually where we find the difference between what was won at the table and what is happening in the claims.

**"What does it cost?" (early, as a screen)**
> Fair question, and I will answer it directly. [Range per the pricing memo.] For context, across one hundred thirty-two reviews our average documented savings ran several hundred thousand dollars per client, so the fee conversation usually ends up being about the size of a rounding error in the findings. If the numbers turn out to be a real constraint for your plan, I will tell you before we start, not after.
*(If the guarantee is approved, one added sentence: "And if we find nothing worth acting on, you will not owe us anything.")*

**"Our pharmacy numbers look fine."**
> They might be, and that would make you unusual in a good way. Here is the honest problem: pharmacy numbers almost always look fine, because the reporting that says so is built from the same definitions that set the prices. Looking fine and being fine are separated by about five contract definitions. The review is how a committee gets to say "we checked" instead of "it looked fine," and after the last two years of fiduciary litigation, that difference is the whole game.

**"We don't have time for this right now."**
> Then let us respect that and be precise: the review needs almost none of your time. One data request, a kickoff call, and the findings meeting; we carry everything between. What actually costs time is the version where the renewal signs unread and next year's committee asks who checked. If now is truly wrong, tell me your renewal date and I will tell you the last responsible month to start.

**"Can you just send me something?"**
> Happily, and something useful rather than a brochure: the worksheet that matches what you are working on, and our published standards for what an independent review has to include, so you can hold anyone, including us, to that bar. When you have looked, tell me what your contract does with [the specific thing discussed], and we will take it from there.
*(Send the matched toolkit + the Standards page. The follow-up question makes the send a thread, not an ending.)*

## 3. The PARTNER variant (brokers on the phone)

The buyer script inverts: never sell the audit, offer the bench. The three beats: (1) *"you stay the relationship lead, findings go through you";* (2) one concrete way in: the co-brandable toolkit, the joint three-way call, the renewal-season second-opinion capacity behind their book; (3) the comparing-notes close: *"even if nothing is live today, I would enjoy trading what we are each seeing in contracts this quarter."* Everything else lives in `broker_partner_track.md`.

## 4. After every call (the two-minute discipline)

Same-day recap email (admin cc'd) with the one insight given, the agreed next step, and its date. Sheet row updated (status, last_action, notes). Any question we could not answer perfectly becomes content: send it to the buyer-anxiety map. The call notes are the raw material for the case-study engine later; write them like you will want them in a year.

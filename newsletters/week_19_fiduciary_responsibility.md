## Fiduciary Duty and Pharmacy Benefits: What ERISA Requires

**Subtitle:** What ERISA requires for pharmacy benefit oversight and how to document a prudent process

**Publication:** Benefit Blind Spots
**Publish:** Monday, May 11, 2026 at 7:30 AM EST
**Length:** ~1,500 words (~6 min read)
**Tone:** Analytical authority
**Pillar:** Broker/Consultant Resources

---

### Article Body

If you manage a self-funded health plan, you are an ERISA fiduciary. That is not a technicality. You have a legal obligation to act in your members' best interest when managing pharmacy benefits. Not your PBM's interest. Your members'.

I have sat across the table from employers who genuinely believed they were doing this right. They had a PBM contract. They processed claims. They renewed on schedule. Then a fiduciary complaint showed up, and the question was not "did you have a PBM?" It was "can you show us your process for selecting, monitoring, and evaluating that PBM?"

They could not.

That gap between "we did what we thought was right" and "we can demonstrate a prudent process" is where fiduciary exposure lives. "We trusted our PBM" is not a defense. I have seen it tried. It does not work.

The question I get most often from employers and their advisory teams: what documentation do I actually need? I will walk through that. But first, some context on why pharmacy benefits specifically are drawing litigation right now.

**What ERISA actually requires**

The standard is not perfection. It is a reasonable, documented process. ERISA fiduciaries must act solely in the interest of plan participants, exercise the care of a prudent person, follow plan documents, and manage conflicts of interest.

For pharmacy benefits, that breaks down to four areas. PBM selection should follow a competitive process with documented evaluation criteria. Contract negotiation should show that terms were reviewed for member impact, not just accepted as presented. Ongoing monitoring has to continue after the ink dries, because fiduciary duty does not stop at contract signing. And fee evaluation matters: understanding what you pay and whether it is reasonable.

That last one deserves emphasis. PBM fee structures are designed to make it difficult to figure out what you are actually paying. Which is exactly why you need to do the work. The best brokers and consultants we work with are already pushing clients on this, but the complexity is real.

**Why pharmacy benefits are drawing fire**

Litigation is picking up. In cases like the Johnson & Johnson matter, plaintiffs alleged the employer failed to prudently select and monitor its PBM, and the plan paid excessive costs as a result. Other cases have challenged PBM arrangements on conflicts of interest and competitive pricing. Spread pricing has been a specific target.

The pattern is consistent: employers who cannot show a deliberate, documented process face the most exposure. Even if their actual PBM deal was competitive. The courts are not asking "did you get a good price?" They are asking "can you show us how you decided?"

I am watching these cases closely because they are defining what prudent practice looks like in real time. The bar is going up.

**The documentation framework**

This is where it gets practical. When we sit down with a plan sponsor, we walk through five categories. I want to be specific enough here that you can look at your own files after reading this and see where the gaps are.

*PBM Selection and Procurement.* Your file should include the RFP document with evaluation criteria defined before proposals came in, proposals from at least three PBM candidates, an evaluation matrix showing how each scored, meeting minutes from the selection committee, a rationale memo explaining why you chose the PBM you chose, conflict of interest disclosures, and evidence the committee had clinical, financial, and legal expertise represented.

What "good" looks like: a memo dated before contract signing that explains the decision, references the criteria, and documents which alternatives were considered and why they were passed over. I have seen exactly one employer do this perfectly. Most have pieces of it scattered across email threads.

*Contract Negotiation.* Redlined contracts showing what you changed from the PBM's initial terms. Legal review from benefits counsel (not just general counsel). Benchmark analysis comparing your terms to market. Documentation of specific negotiated terms: pricing guarantees, rebate passthrough, audit rights, termination provisions. Any side letters or amendments with execution dates.

What "good" looks like: evidence that you did not sign the PBM's standard contract unchanged. Your broker or benefits counsel should be involved here. If I open your file and see a clean signature page with no redlines, that is a gap worth closing.

*Ongoing Performance Monitoring.* Quarterly performance reports from your PBM, plus your own analysis comparing performance to contract guarantees. Documentation of guarantee shortfalls and how they were resolved. Annual benchmarking against market comparators. Clinical program outcomes. Committee meeting minutes reviewing this data.

What "good" looks like: quarterly records showing someone reviewed the numbers, compared them to guarantees, and acted on shortfalls. This is the category where I see the most gaps. Plans sign a good contract and then nobody checks whether the PBM is actually delivering on it. That absence is itself a fiduciary risk.

*Renewal and Amendment.* A market check or RFP at renewal (even if you stay with the current PBM). Analysis comparing renewal terms to what else is available. Documentation of what you negotiated. A rationale memo for the renewal decision. An amendment tracking log.

What "good" looks like: evidence that you treated renewal as a decision, not an automatic continuation. We see auto-renewals constantly. They are the easiest gap for a plaintiff to find.

*Committee Governance.* A named fiduciary or committee charter for pharmacy benefit oversight. A regular meeting schedule, quarterly at minimum. Agendas and minutes with decisions recorded. Attendee lists showing consistent participation. Action items with completion tracking.

What "good" looks like: a regular cadence of documented meetings where pharmacy decisions are discussed, data is reviewed, and actions are recorded. Ad hoc, undocumented decision-making is the pattern that creates the most exposure in litigation.

**The one-page test**

I use this with clients as a quick gut check. If a plaintiff's attorney asked you to demonstrate prudent process for your pharmacy benefit management, could you produce:

A selection rationale memo. Evidence of contract negotiation (not just acceptance). Quarterly performance monitoring documentation. A renewal evaluation showing alternatives were considered. Committee meeting minutes with pharmacy-specific decisions recorded.

If any of those five are missing, that is your exposure. Start building the documentation now. Not next quarter. Now.

No checklist replaces qualified legal counsel. But this framework shows you where the gaps are so you can start closing them.

**What employers get wrong**

The most common fiduciary gaps we see have nothing to do with bad decisions. They are about failing to build the process that demonstrates good ones.

Going years without competitive bidding. Not understanding what you pay or how your PBM profits from the relationship. Auto-renewing without evaluation. Failing to track performance against guarantees. These are industry-wide patterns, not a reflection of any single advisory team. PBM contracts are structured to make this oversight difficult by design.

The one that frustrates me most: making smart decisions but never writing them down. I have worked with employers who genuinely managed their pharmacy benefits well, many with strong brokers and consultants guiding the process. Good outcomes. Thoughtful approach. But nothing on paper. If you cannot show the work, it does not count. A plaintiff's attorney will not take your word for it.

Fiduciary compliance comes down to this: can you show that you took reasonable steps to understand what was happening with plan dollars? Benchmarking. Auditing. Monitoring guarantees. Documenting decisions. Not defensive paperwork for its own sake, but genuinely prudent management with a trail that proves it.

Have you audited your fiduciary documentation trail for pharmacy benefits? If you found gaps, leave a comment below. If this framework is useful, share it with your benefits team or legal counsel.

## The Documentation That Matters in PBM Litigation

**Publication:** Benefit Blind Spots
**Publish:** Thursday, May 14, 2026 at 7:30 AM EST

**Tone:** Practical, tactical
**Pillar:** Broker/Consultant Resources

### Field Note Body

ERISA fiduciary litigation involving pharmacy benefits is increasing. If you read this week's article on fiduciary duty, one thing should be clear: courts do not just evaluate whether you made the right decision. They evaluate whether you followed a prudent process. The evidence of that process lives in your documentation.

We have been tracking these cases closely. Here is what courts are focusing on when they evaluate whether plan fiduciaries acted prudently with pharmacy benefits.

**RFP process records**

Did you conduct a competitive bidding process? Courts look for evidence that fiduciaries evaluated multiple options before selecting a PBM. This does not mean you must switch vendors every cycle. It means you must demonstrate that you assessed the market and made a deliberate choice. Retain your RFP specifications, vendor responses, evaluation scorecards, and the rationale for your final selection.

**Benchmarking reports**

Independent pricing benchmarks are strong evidence of fiduciary diligence. They show that you measured your PBM's performance against market standards, not just against the PBM's own guarantees. If your benchmarking reveals gaps, document how you addressed them. If pricing was competitive, document that finding too.

**Meeting minutes showing deliberation**

Fiduciary committee meeting minutes matter more than most employers realize. Courts look for evidence that pharmacy benefit decisions were discussed, that information was considered, and that questions were asked. Brief minutes noting "PBM contract approved" provide far less protection than minutes documenting the discussion, the data reviewed, and the factors that informed the decision.

**Independent analysis**

Engaging independent consultants or auditors to review your PBM arrangement demonstrates that you sought expert guidance. Many brokers and consultants already facilitate these reviews as part of their standard service. Retaining the analysis, and documenting how you acted on the findings, strengthens your position considerably.

**What to do now**

If you are concerned about fiduciary documentation gaps, here are three steps to take this quarter:

> **1. Audit your documentation trail.**
>
> Pull your records from the last PBM selection or renewal. Can you reconstruct the decision process from the documents alone? If not, you have a gap.

> **2. Establish a documentation protocol going forward.**
>
> Create templates for meeting minutes, decision memos, and benchmarking reviews. The structure does not need to be elaborate; it needs to be consistent.

> **3. Schedule an independent review.**
>
> If you have not had an independent party evaluate your PBM arrangement in the last two years, put it on the calendar. The cost of the review is minimal compared to the fiduciary exposure it addresses.

The trend in litigation is clear. Courts are not asking whether you got the absolute best deal. They are asking whether you took reasonable steps to evaluate, monitor, and document your pharmacy benefit decisions.

The documentation you build today is the evidence you will need if your process is ever questioned. Start now.

## "What I'd Ask" #2: Your First PBM Quarterly Review

**Publish:** Tuesday, May 13, 2026 at 7:30 AM EST
**Format:** Standalone Substack post
**Access:** Free (subscriber-only when paid tier launches)
**Length:** ~350 words

### Ready-to-Publish Content

If I were sitting in your first quarterly PBM review meeting, here are the questions I would ask.

**About guarantees:**
1. How are we performing against each pricing guarantee (GER, brand discount, specialty discount)? Show me the calculation.
2. Are we on track for the annual rebate guarantee? What is the current run rate vs. the guaranteed amount?
3. Have any guarantee shortfalls been identified? If so, what is the remediation plan?

**About utilization:**
4. What are the top 10 drugs by total spend? How does this compare to last quarter?
5. Which new drugs entered our claims data this quarter that were not present last quarter?
6. What is our GLP-1 utilization and spend trend?

**About clinical programs:**
7. What is the current prior authorization approval rate? What are the top denial reasons?
8. How many step therapy overrides were granted this quarter?
9. What medication therapy management (MTM) interventions were completed, and what was the documented cost impact?

**About the relationship:**
10. What recommendations does the PBM have for improving our plan's pharmacy performance next quarter? (This question tests whether your PBM is proactively managing your account or just processing claims.)

The quarterly review is where fiduciary documentation is built. Every question asked, every answer received, and every action taken should be recorded in meeting minutes. This is the evidence of prudent process.

#### Post Copy

Between renewals, most self-funded plans leak money in predictable places.

We see four patterns across claims audits:

1. Formulary drift. The PBM reclassifies tier placement or preferred status without notifying the plan, and the new economics quietly show up on the next invoice.

2. Rebate definition creep. The contract term "eligible claim" shifts scope over the period. Claims that qualified for full rebate in Q1 do not in Q4.

3. Biosimilar lag. A biosimilar to Humira, Stelara, or Enbrel launches. The PBM's default tier placement runs at non-preferred for six to nine months. The plan pays the spread.

4. Network drift. Pharmacies exit the preferred network mid-term. Member scripts route to higher-cost alternatives.

Each pattern is quiet. Each one compounds. None of them show up as a line item on a PBM report.

A mid-cycle claims review is the only way to catch them before renewal.

- **Hook:** "Five questions about your formulary that your PBM will redirect, defer, or answer incompletely. Ask all five anyway."

**Content outline (5 confrontational questions, each with one-line why-it-matters):**
- Slide 1 (Hook): "5 Formulary Questions Your PBM Avoids." Accent Blue swipe indicator.
- Slide 2 (Q1): "What changed on our formulary last quarter, and why?" Why it matters: formulary shifts drive member disruption and rebate flow. The "why" is almost always economic, not clinical.
- Slide 3 (Q2): "Which drugs were added or removed because of rebate contracts?" Why it matters: clinical rationale and rebate rationale are different answers. You are entitled to both.
- Slide 4 (Q3): "What is the net cost of our top 10 drugs after all rebates and fees?" Why it matters: gross cost is not plan cost. Net cost is. The PBM has both numbers.

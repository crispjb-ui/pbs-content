## The Opportunity

Wendell Potter, former CIGNA executive and the most credible insurance-industry adversarial voice in U.S. policy media, has invited Ginny Crisp, PharmD to join HEALTH CARE un-covered as a contributing author. Cadence: twice monthly. Length: 800-1,000 words per piece. Audience: employers, policymakers, media.

This is a positioning expansion, not a new content channel. PBS content stays where it is (tactical, plan-sponsor-actionable). The Wendell pieces sit a layer up: macro reform, named structural villains, prescriptive recommendations to regulators and legislators. The same proprietary anchor — hundreds of PBM contracts a year — sits inside every piece, but the frame elevates from "here is the clause to watch" to "here is the law that should pass."

The asymmetric leverage: Wendell's audience is materially larger than PBS's current 1,726 LinkedIn / 836 newsletter / 65 Substack base, and structurally different. Media tier in particular is new — pieces that land cleanly create reverse pathways into Politico, STAT, Axios PBM coverage as a named source. Policymaker tier creates the inbound for Senate-staffer calls when PBM reform legislation moves.

## Voice for Wendell Pieces

Ginny's PBS voice is **diagnostic** ("here is the clause to watch"). The Wendell voice is **prescriptive** ("here is the law that should pass"). Same proprietary anchor; louder prescription; willingness to name CVS Caremark, Express Scripts, and OptumRx as actors rather than "the Big PBMs."

The brand voice drift is deliberate. Three differences from PBS LinkedIn / Substack voice:

**1. Metaphor-led opener.** Wendell pieces open with a short analogy or metaphor that frames the reform argument. Ron uses bomb-disposal. Wendell uses arsonist-fire-department imagery. Ginny gets her own opening conceits per piece (drafted below). These openers carry the reader into the piece without 200 words of setup; the metaphor IS the setup. PBS-channel content does not lead with metaphor; Wendell-channel content does.

**2. Confidently prescriptive endings.** Each piece names a regulator (FTC, HHS, state insurance commissioner) and a named action ("require X by [date]"). PBS content typically ends with reader-action ("ask your broker for Y"). Wendell content ends with policy-action ("Congress should require Z"). Ginny is comfortable with this; some readers from her current PBS audience will see both pieces, and the shift should read as consistent (same Ginny) but louder (opinion, not just observation).

**3. Named adversaries, by company.** Not "the Big 3 PBMs." CVS Caremark. Express Scripts. OptumRx. Ascent. Emisar. Zinc. The specificity is what differentiates a Ginny piece from a generic "PBM reform" piece on the same publication.

**4. Opinion stated openly as opinion — anchored by expertise, never freestanding.** PBS-channel content tends to lean on the proprietary anchor and let the pattern speak for itself — "I see this in nearly every contract I audit." Wendell-channel content adds an openly-stated opinion layer on top: *"I think this is the wrong conversation." "In my view, the policy fight is missing the middle layer." "My take is that the FTC has the authority to act unilaterally and should." "What I would argue is..."* Ron uses similar phrases throughout his pieces (*"I could write a whole book on why this much vertical integration creates problems," "I am as guilty as anyone of focusing on the problems"*); Wendell himself runs the same register.

**The hard constraint: Ginny must read as the absolute expert at all times.** Opinion is the layer; expertise is the foundation. Every opinion sentence sits next to or inside an anchor — a specific contract clause, a specific drug, a specific dollar figure, a specific named entity, a specific observation drawn from the contract-review reservoir. Opinion + anchor = expert opinion (the register Wendell's audience expects from a clinical pharmacist who reviews hundreds of PBM contracts a year). Opinion alone, untethered to the anchor, reads as a hot take and degrades the expertise positioning. The hot-take register is what Ginny is NOT — it is the failure mode to avoid. Pre-publish check on any opinion sentence: does the immediately preceding or following sentence carry a piece of expertise only PBS could produce? If no, anchor it or remove it.

Everything else stays Ginny: the proprietary anchor (hundreds of contracts a year, sometimes refined to specific provision-family observations), the Humanize Check rule (one unfakeable detail per piece, non-negotiable), the no-em-dash rule, the no-fabricated-stats rule, the broker-sensitivity rule (PBM is the structural adversary; brokers are the allies who flag gaps).

## Cascade Architecture (W → W+1)

**Wendell W:** Wendell piece publishes on Topic X — macro/reform frame, named villains, prescriptive ending.

**PBS W+1:** Topic X re-cast for plan sponsors, sharpened. Monday-anchored cascade with Wednesday text-post amplifier.

| Slot | What runs | Topic |
|---|---|---|
| Mon 7:30 AM | Substack deep dive | **Topic X** (tactical) + Plan Sponsor Toolkit handout |
| Mon 7:45 AM | LinkedIn newsletter | **Topic X** briefing version + Substack tool CTA |
| Tue 8:30 AM | LinkedIn carousel | Cycle-rotation pillar (NOT Topic X) |
| Wed 7:30 AM | Substack roundup | Includes Wendell piece as one of 4-5 curated items |
| Wed 8:30 AM | LinkedIn text post | Confrontational hook on **Topic X** when topic supports it; otherwise cycle pillar |
| Thu 7:30 AM | Substack Field Note | NOT Topic X — Thursday Excellence preserved on different pillar |
| Thu 8:30 AM | LinkedIn carousel | Thursday Slot A/B/C rotation, NOT Topic X |
| Fri 8:30 AM | LinkedIn tease | Next Monday's content |

**Why this architecture works:**

- Wendell W does discovery and policymaker/media awareness; PBS W+1 does conversion and plan-sponsor activation. They don't compete in the same week.
- A Politico-reading policymaker who sees the Wendell piece → searches Ginny → finds PBS cascade content freshly published on the same topic operationalized = compound effect.
- PBS regulars who saw the Wendell piece get reinforcement: "she just said this for policymakers; here is what to do about it inside your contract."
- Triple-distinct rule preserved: Mon Substack, Tue carousel, Thu Field Note are three different topics. Wednesday text post sits outside that rule, so it can run Topic X without breaking anything.
- Maximum cross-pollination footprint: four touches on one theme across two publications and two audiences (Wendell piece W, plus Mon + Wed-text + Wed-roundup-mention in W+1).

**Wednesday text post on cascade topic — when:**

Run cascade-topic on Wednesday text post when the topic produces a strong sub-30-word confrontational hook with a named villain. Most of the first 6 pieces support this naturally. When the topic doesn't hit the hook bar in 30 words (Aggregators is the textbook example), Wednesday falls back to cycle-rotation hook on a different pillar.

## The Quotable Callback Mechanic

Every Wendell piece is drafted with **pull-quote-ability** in mind. Each piece carries 2-3 short, declarative, fully-anchored sentences that can be lifted verbatim into PBS cascade-week content. This creates an authority loop:

**Halo effect (PBS → Wendell):** PBS regulars see Ginny was just on HEALTH CARE un-covered. Authority transfer.

**Reverse flow (Wendell → PBS):** Wendell readers who search Ginny see PBS content quoting back to the Wendell piece. Recognition + freshness.

**Three-channel multiplier on one piece of writing:**

- Monday LinkedIn newsletter opens with: *"Last week on HEALTH CARE un-covered, I argued [pull quote]. Here is what that means inside your contract."*
- Monday Substack deep dive includes a mid-article callout box: *"As I wrote last week for HEALTH CARE un-covered: [pull quote]. The operational angle is below."*
- Wednesday Substack roundup features the Wendell piece as one of 4-5 curated items with Ginny's gloss: *"My piece this week on HEALTH CARE un-covered. The prescription: [thesis sentence]."*
- Wednesday LinkedIn text post may use the same pull quote as its opening hook if the line is strong enough standalone.

**Implication for the writing:** Wendell pieces must be drafted with 2-3 quotable sentences engineered into the structure. Short. Declarative. Anchored. The piece is not just a Wendell piece; it is also a content reservoir for the next week's PBS cascade.

## The First 6 Pieces

Front-loaded for impact. Ordered so Ginny's lane is established by Piece 3 and Wendell's audience knows what to expect from her by Piece 4.

### Piece 1: Spread Pricing

**Working title:** *Three Prices for the Same Drug — Why the FTC's Next Move on PBMs Should Be Mandatory Spread Disclosure*

**Opening metaphor:** *A wholesaler that charges retail to its customer, pays wholesale to its supplier, and pockets the difference is legitimate when the customer knows the markup. PBM spread pricing is the same trade with one critical difference: the customer never sees the markup.*

**Named villains:** CVS Caremark, Express Scripts, OptumRx. PCMA (industry trade association) as the regulatory-defense arm.

**Proprietary anchor sentence (pull-quote candidate):** *I see spread-pricing language in nearly every contract I audit, and most plan sponsors don't know it is there until someone reads the definitions section out loud.*

**Prescriptive ending:** Mandatory spread disclosure at the PBM level, enforced by FTC + state insurance commissioners. Quarterly per-NDC spread reports filed with state DOIs and made available to plan sponsors on request.

**Cascade-week PBS angle:** "Three Audit Passes That Surface Spread in Your Plan" — Plan Sponsor Toolkit handout, extends W18 Channel Pricing material.

**Wednesday text-post hook potential:** Strong. *"CVS Caremark sets the price your plan pays. They also set the price the pharmacy receives. They keep the difference, and the contract does not require them to tell you the size of it."*

### Piece 2: Vertical Integration in PBMs

**Working title:** *Your PBM Owns Your Specialty Pharmacy. Your Insurer Owns Your PBM. Here Is the Bill That Should Break That Up.*

**Opening metaphor:** *Imagine if your auto insurer also owned the repair shop, the parts supplier, and the salvage yard, and got to decide which one your claim went to. The repair bill comes back; you have no way to test whether the parts were aftermarket or OEM, whether the labor was fairly priced, or whether the salvage discount was passed back to you. The auto-insurance regulator would have stopped that vertical integration before it consolidated. The PBM regulator didn't.*

**Named villains:** UnitedHealth/OptumRx/Optum Specialty. CVS Health/Caremark/CVS Specialty/Aetna. Cigna/Express Scripts/Accredo/Evernorth.

**Proprietary anchor sentence:** *In the contracts I review, the specialty routing clause is rarely as exclusive as the routing pattern. The contract preserves member choice on paper; the operational workflow steers most fills to the PBM-owned channel anyway.*

**Prescriptive ending:** Divestiture mandate for PBM-owned specialty pharmacies above a market-concentration threshold, OR contractual firewall requirements with audit rights for plan sponsors. State-level passthrough legislation as the parallel track until federal action moves.

**Cascade-week PBS angle:** "The Five Contract Clauses That Lock Your Specialty Fills Into the PBM-Owned Pharmacy" — Plan Sponsor Toolkit handout. Extends W22 Biosimilar material.

**Wednesday text-post hook potential:** Strong. *"OptumRx owns Optum Specialty Pharmacy. OptumRx also writes the prior-authorization rule that decides which pharmacy your member can use. OptumRx also reports the savings to you. Three jobs. Same vendor. No independent voice."*

---

### Piece 3: GLP-1 Coverage and the PBM Profit Layer

**Working title:** *The GLP-1 Access Debate Is the Wrong Debate. The PBM Profit Layer Is the Conversation Nobody Is Having.*

**Opening metaphor:** *Imagine a tariff war where the tariffs go to a third party that nobody is talking about. The manufacturer says, "our list price is high because rebates are high." The plan says, "we pay high prices." The PBM in the middle collects the difference and goes silent on the question of who that difference actually flows back to.*

**Named villains:** CVS Caremark, OptumRx, Express Scripts. The rebate-aggregator layer (Ascent, Emisar, Zinc) named in passing as foreshadowing for Piece 8.

**Proprietary anchor sentence:** *In every other class of drug, the gap between list price and net cost is widest where the PBM has the most leverage. GLP-1s are the highest-leverage class in modern memory. The reform conversation should be about the gap.*

**Prescriptive ending:** Class-specific rebate-flow transparency rules for GLP-1s (and any future class crossing $X billion in annual U.S. spend). HHS Secretary authority to require per-NDC net-cost disclosure within 60 days of any class crossing the threshold. State Medicaid programs pilot the model first.

**Cascade-week PBS angle:** "GLP-1 Channel Pricing — What to Demand in Your Next PBM RFP" — extends W30 site-of-care content and any existing GLP-1-tagged material.

**Wednesday text-post hook potential:** Strong. *"Your employee's GLP-1 cost: ~$1,200 a month. Your PBM's net cost on the same prescription: roughly a third of that. The other two-thirds is the part everyone is fighting about and nobody is naming."*

### Piece 4: Copay Accumulator Programs — The Structural Conflict of Interest

**Working title:** *The Conflict of Interest at the Heart of Copay Accumulator Programs*

**Opening metaphor:** *Imagine a charitable foundation that gives scholarships to college students to help them afford tuition. Now imagine the foundation hires an administrator to process those scholarships. Now imagine the administrator also owns the college, decides which courses qualify for the scholarship, and decides whether the scholarship counts toward the student's tuition bill or simply toward the college's revenue. Same dollars. Different beneficiary depending on how the administrator counts. One entity controlling every decision in the transaction.*

**Named villains:** SaveOnSP (Express Scripts subsidiary). PrudentRx (CVS Caremark). Variable Copay (OptumRx variant). The vertical-integration structure that lets one PBM control formulary placement, specialty pharmacy routing, and accumulator crediting math simultaneously.

**Proprietary anchor sentence:** *In the contracts I review, the accumulator program is almost always administered by the same PBM that owns the specialty pharmacy dispensing the drug and writes the formulary that defines the drug as accumulator-eligible. Three roles. One vendor.*

**Prescriptive ending:** Three structural reforms in order of political achievability: federal disclosure rule for plans using accumulator or maximizer programs (HHS sub-regulatory guidance or ERISA amendment); federal firewall statute prohibiting PBMs from administering accumulator programs while also owning the specialty pharmacy dispensing eligible drugs; formulary-design firewall requiring clinical-criteria basis for eligibility, auditable by plan sponsor. The reform is the firewall and the disclosure, not the prohibition.

**Cascade-week PBS angle:** "The Firewall Audit: Does Your PBM Administer the Program AND Own the Specialty Pharmacy?" — Plan Sponsor Toolkit handout walking an HR Director or benefits manager through a self-audit of conflict-of-interest exposure in their current PBM setup.

**Wednesday text-post hook potential:** Strong. Reframes from member-harm confrontation to structural-conflict-of-interest confrontation, preserving Ginny's pro-copay-card and pro-plan-sponsor-judgment positions. *"Your PBM decides which drugs are accumulator-eligible. Your PBM owns the specialty pharmacy that fills them. Your PBM administers the crediting math. Three jobs, one vendor, and the difference goes to the PBM."*

**Voice note:** The structural-firewall reframe (vs the more common "ban accumulator programs" reform argument) preserves three Ginny positions simultaneously: pro-manufacturer-copay-card (legitimate patient affordability tool), pro-plan-sponsor-judgment (plan sponsors retain the right to choose accumulator programs as a cost lever), and anti-PBM-self-dealing (the vertical-integration conflict of interest is the structural problem). Full draft in `wendell_potter_piece_04_draft.md`.

### Piece 5: White-Bagging / Channel Steering

**Working title:** *Same Drug. Three Channels. Three Prices. One PBM Setting All of Them.*

**Opening metaphor:** *Imagine being charged three different rates at the gas pump depending on which pump you pulled into, with no posted prices, and only one of the pumps owned by the gas station you signed a contract with. That is specialty drug routing in employer plans.*

**Named villains:** CVS Caremark/CVS Specialty, OptumRx/Optum Specialty, Express Scripts/Accredo. The site-of-care steering rules embedded in PA workflows.

**Proprietary anchor sentence:** *Same drug. Same dose. Same patient. The price the plan pays depends entirely on which pharmacy fills it. The pharmacy is chosen by the PBM. The PBM is paid more when the choice routes to their own pharmacy.*

**Prescriptive ending:** Site-of-care neutrality regulation requiring transparent per-channel net cost disclosure before PBMs may route fills. ERISA amendment establishing plan sponsor audit rights for specialty pharmacy pass-through. State insurance commissioners empowered to enforce parity for self-insured plans under state-law-based fiduciary frameworks (where applicable).

**Cascade-week PBS angle:** "Same Drug. Three Channels. Three Prices — Audit Worksheet" — extends the W18 Channel Pricing breakout content directly.

**Wednesday text-post hook potential:** Strong. The W18 audience already responded to this framing. *"Same drug. Same dose. Same patient. Three different prices, set by the same PBM. The contract does not require them to tell you how the spread is allocated."*

### Piece 6: The FTC PBM Report Follow-On

**Working title:** *The FTC Already Told Us the PBM Industry Is Broken. Here Is What Should Happen Next.*

**Opening metaphor:** *When the FTC publishes an interim staff report titled "Pharmacy Benefit Managers: The Powerful Middlemen Inflating Drug Costs and Squeezing Main Street Pharmacies," that is a finding. What follows is supposed to be policy. We are now well past the point where silence is acceptable.*

**Named villains:** The Big 3 PBMs collectively. PCMA. The members of Congress who have blocked PBM reform votes in successive sessions.

**Proprietary anchor sentence:** *Everything the FTC documented in that report is something I have been seeing in contracts I review for a decade. The diagnostic is complete. The treatment plan is what is missing.*

**Prescriptive ending:** 3-5 named regulatory and legislative actions for 2026:
1. FTC final report with enforcement actions, not another interim report
2. HHS Secretary directs CMS to require PBM transparency in Medicare Part D bids matching the FTC's documented disclosure failures
3. ERISA amendment establishing federal audit-rights floor for self-insured plans
4. State pharmacy commissioner authority over PBM business practices, federally backstopped where ERISA preempts state action
5. DOJ antitrust review of pending PBM-Specialty Pharmacy / PBM-Insurer mergers

**Cascade-week PBS angle:** "What the FTC Findings Mean for Your Q4 Renewal" — operationalizes the macro into employer-action.

**Wednesday text-post hook potential:** Moderate-to-strong. Hook depends on a specific Congress-member callout or a specific stalled bill. *"The FTC issued an interim staff report titled 'The Powerful Middlemen Inflating Drug Costs and Squeezing Main Street Pharmacies.' Nearly two years later: no final report, no comprehensive enforcement action, no legislative action. Ask your Senator why."*

## Risks and Guardrails

**1. Stay at policy-proposal altitude, not partisan altitude.** Ron and Wendell both stay above partisan lines. Ginny follows. Name structural villains (PBMs, vertical-integration mergers, accumulator programs); do not name partisan villains. The reform argument works regardless of which party is in the majority.

**2. Anonymize all client examples.** "A self-funded employer in the Southeast" or "a manufacturing client we audited last quarter," never identifying detail. Even composite case studies should be drawn so loosely that reverse-engineering is impossible.

**3. Broker sensitivity rule still applies, doubled.** The PBM is the structural adversary in every piece. Brokers and consultants are the allies who flag the gaps. Never imply that brokers missed something. Frame: "The brokers I respect are the ones who flag this language at renewal, not after the year-end statement." This is load-bearing because the W14 Stop-Loss newsletter deletion showed broker community sensitivity is real and political.

**4. Humanize Check applies double.** Wendell's audience is sophisticated and skeptical. One unfakeable detail per piece is non-negotiable; AI-tell blacklist is enforced; rhythmic AI cadence (short-long-short-long) is the failure mode to avoid. Pre-publish question: can a reader point to one thing in this piece only PBS could have written? If no, rewrite.

**5. Do not burn the PBS reservoir.** Wendell piece and PBS cascade-week deep dive must reinforce, not duplicate. The reader who consumes both should feel: "she said this for policymakers; here is what to do about it inside my contract." If the two pieces overlap in tactical content, the cascade architecture collapses.

**6. Voice drift is intentional and bounded.** Wendell-channel voice = metaphor-led opener + prescriptive frame + named adversaries + Ginny's proprietary anchor. PBS-channel voice = unchanged (confident clinical provocation, diagnostic frame, one stat as hook). Do not drift PBS-channel voice toward Wendell-channel voice; the channels each have their reason for existing.

**7. Lawyer-eyes-on for the first 3 pieces.** Naming CVS Caremark / OptumRx / Express Scripts in the prescriptive frame is not novel — Wendell does it routinely, Ron does it (UnitedHealth named explicitly in his May 13 piece). But it is worth a pre-publish read for the first 3 pieces to make sure no specific factual claim ("UnitedHealth pays its captive physicians 17% more" type assertions) is unsourced or potentially actionable.

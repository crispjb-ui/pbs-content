# PART 1: SUBSTACK ARTICLE

## The Shell Company Between You and Your Rebate

**Subtitle:** Your PBM owns a separate company, often incorporated offshore, that takes a fee on your drug rebates before the pass-through clause ever applies. Here is how the structure works and how to find it in your own contract.

**Publication:** Benefit Blind Spots
**Publish:** Monday, October 12, 2026 at 7:30 AM EST
**Length:** ~1,550 words (~6 min read)
**Tone:** Analytical authority
**Pillar:** Transparency & Industry Education

---

### Article Body

A few years ago I was reading a self-funded employer's PBM contract, and the rebate section was clean. One hundred percent pass-through, defined inclusively, audit rights intact. The kind of language I usually have to fight for. Then I got to a definitions exhibit near the back and found a reference to a "group purchasing organization" the plan had never heard of, sitting between the manufacturer and the PBM. The rebate the plan thought it was getting one hundred percent of had already been touched, and lightened, before the PBM's pass-through obligation even started.

That is the part of the rebate story almost no plan sponsor sees. We spend a lot of time on how rebates are defined and reported, and that matters. But there is a structural layer underneath the definitions: a separately incorporated company, frequently domiciled overseas, that the PBM owns and routes manufacturer money through. By the time the rebate reaches the contract you signed, a fee has been taken in a place your agreement was never written to show you.

This is not a loophole someone found. It is an architecture someone built.

**What a rebate aggregator actually is**

The rebate supply chain you usually hear described has three parties: the manufacturer, the PBM, and the plan. The real chain has four. Between the manufacturer and the PBM sits a rebate aggregator, also called a group purchasing organization or GPO. Its job, on paper, is to negotiate rebates across multiple PBM clients and gain scale. Its job, in practice, is to be a legally distinct entity where a fee can be collected on manufacturer payments before those payments are characterized as "rebates" subject to your pass-through clause.

The flow looks like this: the manufacturer pays the aggregator, the aggregator keeps a fee and passes the rest to the PBM, and the PBM passes some portion to your plan. The aggregator is owned by, or affiliated with, the same parent company as the PBM. So the "negotiating partner" extracting a fee one layer up is, functionally, the PBM wearing a different corporate hat.

The structure is not original. The PBM borrowed it from hospitals. Group purchasing organizations have sat in the hospital supply chain for decades, negotiating on behalf of health systems and taking a fee in the middle. The pharmacy version copied the model down to the name: these entities are literally incorporated as GPOs. That lineage is the tell. A middleman paid as a cut of the transaction earns more when the price it "negotiates" is higher, not lower, whether the product is a hospital's medical supplies or your plan's drug rebates. Obfuscation of the real price is not a malfunction of that arrangement. It is the arrangement working as designed.

**Why the offshore part matters**

Two of the three largest aggregators are incorporated outside the United States. Ascent Health Services, affiliated with Express Scripts and Cigna, is domiciled in Switzerland. Emisar Pharma Services, affiliated with OptumRx and UnitedHealth, is domiciled in Ireland. Zinc Health Services, affiliated with CVS Caremark, is US-based. Three of the largest PBMs, three captive aggregators, two of them overseas.

The location is not an accident of convenience. A separately incorporated, offshore entity is harder for a plan to reach with an audit, sits outside the contract language that governs the PBM directly, and keeps the fee layer off the set of books the plan is contractually allowed to see. When I tell a plan sponsor that the aggregator's fee is invisible to them, I am not being dramatic. The entity that took it is in another country, behind a different corporate veil, outside the four corners of their agreement.

The opacity is not a side effect of the structure. The opacity is the product the structure delivers.

**How this turns "100% pass-through" into something less**

Here is the sentence that does the damage, and it is in most contracts: the PBM agrees to pass through one hundred percent of rebates it *receives*. Read the verb. One hundred percent of what the PBM received, not one hundred percent of what the manufacturer paid. The aggregator sits between those two numbers and takes its fee in the gap. So you get all of a number that was already made smaller, one layer up, by a company your PBM controls.

This is why a contract can be technically honest and still leave money on the table. The pass-through percentage is real. The definition of the thing being passed through is where the value quietly leaves. The percentage is the magician's patter. The definition is the trick.

**This is not small money, and it is growing**

The Federal Trade Commission has been examining this layer directly. According to FTC data, PBM rebate-aggregator fees roughly doubled, from about $3.8 billion in 2018 to $7.6 billion by 2022. Internal industry analysis has shown "fees and GPO" revenue overtaking traditional "rebates and price protection" as a share of PBM gross profit. In plain terms: the fee layer is no longer a side business. For the large PBMs it has become a primary growth engine, and it grows in a part of the chain that plan sponsors cannot see by design.

When a revenue stream that large is concentrated in an offshore, separately incorporated entity, the question for a plan sponsor is not whether it affects your costs. It is whether your contract gives you any way to find out how much.

**The Rebate Routing Audit**

I built this short diagnostic because the aggregator question rarely appears in a standard contract review, and it should be the first thing you ask. It is three questions, and the third one is the whole game. The printable version is below.

> **Question 1: Does an affiliated GPO or rebate aggregator touch our rebates?**
> Ask the PBM directly, in writing. The answer is almost always yes for the three largest PBMs. Get the entity named.

> **Question 2: Where is that entity incorporated, and is it affiliated with our PBM's parent company?**
> Switzerland, Ireland, or a US state. Affiliated or independent. This tells you whether the "negotiating partner" extracting a fee is actually your PBM in a different jacket, and whether it sits inside or outside your audit reach.

> **Question 3: Does our pass-through apply to what the manufacturer paid, or what the PBM received after the aggregator's fee?**
> This is the one that matters. If pass-through is measured at "received," the aggregator fee is invisible and uncapped. The protective language ties pass-through to *manufacturer-paid* value.

The contract language that fixes question three is short, and it is the single most valuable sentence you can add to a rebate exhibit:

> **What you might see:** "PBM shall pass through 100% of Rebates received by PBM to Plan."
>
> **What to ask for:** "PBM shall pass through to Plan 100% of all compensation paid by manufacturers in connection with Plan's utilization, measured at the point of manufacturer payment and inclusive of any amount retained by any affiliated or intermediary entity, including any group purchasing organization or rebate aggregator, prior to remittance to PBM."

That clause moves the measurement point from "received" to "paid," and it names the aggregator so the fee cannot hide behind a separate corporate entity. *Illustrative example for educational purposes. Actual contract language and amounts vary by plan.*

**What to do with this**

You do not need to win a fight about offshore corporate structure to protect your plan. You need to move one measurement point in one clause, and you need to know which entity to name when you do it. The aggregator layer is one of the cleanest examples of how a PBM earns money in a place your contract was written not to show you, and it is also one of the most fixable, because the fix is language, not litigation.

If you are heading into a renewal this fall, this is the question to put on the table before the rebate guarantee gets discussed. The guarantee is a number. The routing is the structure underneath the number. Fix the structure first.

### Field Note Body

We have been reviewing a stack of rebate exhibits this renewal season, and the same quiet line keeps showing up. Not in the rebate section. Next to it. A manufacturer payment called "price protection," and a definition narrow enough that it never has to be passed through.

Here is what price protection is. When a manufacturer raises a drug's list price, it sometimes agrees to pay the PBM the difference on existing utilization, so the PBM is held harmless against the increase. That payment is tied directly to your plan's drugs and your plan's utilization. It is, by any plain reading, money the manufacturer paid because of your members. But if your contract defines "rebate" as "manufacturer rebates" and stops there, price protection is not a rebate. It sits one inch outside the definition, and it stays with the PBM.

This is the same move as the offshore aggregator from Monday, run at the level of a label instead of an entity. Monday's trick moves money through a separate company. This one moves money through a separate word. Both end in the same place: manufacturer dollars tied to your plan that your pass-through clause was written not to reach.

Price protection is not the only one. The family of manufacturer payments that commonly get defined out of "rebate" includes:

> **Price protection payments** (manufacturer covers a list-price increase on your utilization)
> **Administrative fees** (manufacturer pays the PBM a percentage "for services")
> **Formulary access / placement fees** (manufacturer pays for preferred status)
> **Data and reporting fees** (manufacturer buys utilization data the PBM packages)
> **GPO / aggregator service fees** (the offshore layer from Monday, by another name)

Every one of those is manufacturer money connected to your plan's drugs. Every one of them can be defined as something other than a "rebate," and therefore left out of your one hundred percent.

**How to spot it in your own report**

You will not find a line called "price protection withheld." You find it by looking for what is missing. Three moves:

1. **Ask for the manufacturer payment categories, not just the rebate total.** Request a breakout of every category of payment the PBM receives from manufacturers related to your plan. Price protection, admin fees, and access fees should each appear. If they do not, ask why.

2. **Compare your "rebate" definition to the categories that came back.** Any category the PBM receives that is not captured in your definition is a category you are not getting passed through.

3. **Move the definition from "rebates" to "all manufacturer compensation."** The protective language is one phrase: define the passed-through amount as "all compensation received from manufacturers in connection with Plan's utilization, including but not limited to rebates, price protection, administrative fees, and formulary access payments."

That phrase captures the whole family at once. It is the difference between passing through a category and passing through everything the manufacturer paid.

If you fixed the routing on Monday, this is the matching fix on the definition. The aggregator structure and the label structure are two doors to the same room. Close both.

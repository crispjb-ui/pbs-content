# Ad Spend Topic Selection Framework

> **The question this answers:** When should PBS put LinkedIn ad spend behind a topic? Should ad budget amplify topics that are already winning organically, or push topics that aren't getting traction?

> **Honest short answer:** Mostly amplify organic winners, but for a different reason than most people assume. Read the framework.

---

## The wrong mental model

Most people frame ad spend as "boost the post that already worked" — i.e., put paid promotion behind whatever LinkedIn organic post performed best last week. That's the wrong frame for PBS.

**Why it's wrong:**

1. **Organic winners on LinkedIn don't need paid amplification.** A post that hit 17K organic impressions reached the audience LinkedIn's algorithm decided to serve it to. Paying to boost it 10% further serves the same algorithmic audience with diminishing returns.

2. **Boosting a post is a vanity metric.** Reach is not the goal; lead capture is. A boosted post might add 5K impressions and 0 incremental Lead Gen Form submissions if the post isn't built to convert.

3. **PBS has Cuban-amplified organic reach already.** When Mark Cuban comments on or reposts a PBS post, organic reach jumps 60-75%. Paid spend can't replicate that. Paid spend's job is to reach audiences that organic doesn't.

The right mental model is different: **ad spend is for finding lead magnets the audience converts on, then scaling them.** Not for amplifying impressions on already-popular content.

---

## The right mental model

LinkedIn ad spend at PBS does ONE job: drive Lead Gen Form submissions on the lead-magnet Toolkits.

```
Organic LinkedIn post → optional reach amplifier (Cuban, network shares)
                     → drives readers to Substack and rxbs.org
                     → some convert to Substack subs and Toolkit downloads
                     ↓ (parallel)
LinkedIn ad with Lead Gen Form → drives Toolkit downloads directly
                              → captures email in-platform
                              → enrolls in welcome sequence
                              → converts to Contract Review inquiries
```

The two paths run in parallel and serve different functions. Organic builds audience; ads build a captured email list with welcome-sequence conversion.

So the right question is not "which post should I boost?" — it's **"which Toolkit should I run a Lead Gen Form ad for?"** And the framework for answering that is below.

---

## The 4-factor scoring rubric for Toolkit ad spend

Score each Toolkit on four factors. Toolkits scoring high on all four are the ones to spend on. Toolkits scoring low on one or more are not ready (or aren't a good fit for paid promotion).

### Factor 1 — Organic conversion validation (does the Toolkit actually convert when readers see it?)

**What to measure:** Substack subscribe rate when the Toolkit's Substack post ships, OR rxbs.org form submission rate when the Toolkit landing page receives organic traffic.

**Score 1-5:**
- 5: Strong conversion (W18 Channel Pricing pattern: 4-5 free Substack subs from one organic post)
- 4: Above-baseline conversion (3+ subs / form fills per organic post)
- 3: Baseline conversion (1-2 subs / form fills)
- 2: Below-baseline (rare conversions)
- 1: No conversions on organic ship

**Why this factor matters:** Ad spend amplifies the lead-magnet's existing conversion rate. A Toolkit that doesn't convert organic readers won't suddenly start converting paid-traffic readers. Validate organic first.

### Factor 2 — Topic engagement on LinkedIn (does the underlying topic resonate with the audience?)

**What to measure:** Impressions, comments, saves on the Thursday LinkedIn post that previewed the Toolkit. Compare to the W18 Channel Pricing benchmark (17K impressions, 18 saves, 17 comments).

**Score 1-5:**
- 5: Breakout post — 15K+ impressions, 10+ comments, 10+ saves (W18 standard)
- 4: Strong post — 8-15K impressions, 5-10 comments, 5-10 saves
- 3: Solid post — 3-8K impressions, 2-5 comments, 2-5 saves (typical Thursday)
- 2: Below-baseline — 1-3K impressions, 0-2 comments, 0-2 saves
- 1: Underperforming — under 1K impressions

**Why this factor matters:** If the topic doesn't engage on LinkedIn organically, the ad creative will struggle to engage either. Ad spend is an audience-extension tool, not a topic-rescue tool.

### Factor 3 — Buyer alignment (does the Toolkit map to PBS's revenue funnel?)

**What to measure:** Where does this Toolkit sit in the PBS engagement journey? Tier 1 (service-aligned) vs. Tier 2 (mechanic-specific) vs. Tier 3 (workflow / member experience).

**Score 1-5:**
- 5: Tier 1 toolkit (Contract Review Readiness, Optimize-vs-Go-to-Market, PBR Framework) — directly maps to PBS service revenue
- 4: Tier 2, top-priority (Channel Pricing, Rebate Audit, PBM Compensation) — strongly drives Contract Review demand
- 3: Tier 2, secondary (Quarterly Reporting, GER Audit, Specialty Routing) — moderately drives Contract Review demand
- 2: Tier 2, tertiary (Mid-Year Performance Guarantee, Termination Clause, Definition Variance) — weakly drives Contract Review demand
- 1: Tier 3 (workflow / member experience) — does not directly drive Contract Review demand

**Why this factor matters:** Ad spend at PBS is paid for by Contract Review revenue (the consulting business). A Toolkit that doesn't drive Contract Review inquiries doesn't pay back the ad spend, regardless of how many leads it captures.

### Factor 4 — Market timing (is there urgency in the buyer's calendar that a Toolkit-specific ad would catch?)

**What to measure:** Is there a regulatory shift, a renewal-cycle moment, or a market event that makes THIS topic urgent THIS quarter?

**Score 1-5:**
- 5: High urgency right now (e.g., Rebate Report Audit during Q1-Q2 when FTC reports trigger industry attention)
- 4: Seasonal urgency (e.g., RFP Scoring in Q3 when many plans run their RFP cycle)
- 3: Steady-state demand (e.g., Channel Pricing — always relevant, no specific seasonal spike)
- 2: Out-of-season (e.g., Renewal Readiness in Q1 — too far from renewal cycle to feel urgent)
- 1: Counter-seasonal (e.g., RFP Scoring in late Q4 — RFPs are already underway)

**Why this factor matters:** Ad creative is most effective when the audience feels urgency. Same Toolkit, same audience, same ad spend produces 2-3x results during seasonal urgency vs. off-season.

### Total score interpretation

Add the four factor scores. Total = 4-20.

| Total score | Recommendation | Suggested budget |
|---|---|---|
| **17-20** | Highest-priority spend target | $1,500-3,000 over 14 days |
| **13-16** | Strong spend target | $750-1,500 over 14 days |
| **9-12** | Worth testing at small scale | $300-500 over 14 days |
| **5-8** | Don't spend yet — improve organic first | $0; revisit in 60-90 days |
| **4** | Don't spend, period | $0 |

## When organic outperforms — the special case

There's one case where ad spend can actually amplify a Cuban-style organic breakout: **the boost-from-tip-over case.**

If an organic post is performing in the top 20% (8-15K impressions) but hasn't broken out (under 17K), a small targeted ad spend can push it to breakout territory. The mechanism is: the post has the engagement signal LinkedIn's algorithm rewards, but missed the threshold; paid amplification pushes the engagement signal higher, which triggers more organic distribution.

**When this works:**
- Organic post in the top 20% of PBS's Thursday baseline
- Post is converting Substack subs at above-baseline rate (2+ subs from organic alone)
- Post's topic is in the launch-set Toolkit lineup (so paid push has a downstream Lead Gen Form)
- Post is within 7 days of original ship (algorithmic decay sets in after that)

**Budget for this case:** $200-300 over 3-5 days. Targeted to PBS's lookalike audience. Track carefully — if organic continues to grow during the boost window, the boost is working. If organic flattens, paid is just paying for impressions with no compounding benefit.

This is a tactical play, not a strategic frame. Don't build the ad budget around it; use it opportunistically when a strong-but-not-breakout post appears.

## When NOT to put ad spend behind a topic

Specific scenarios where ad spend is wrong:

1. **Topic that bombed organically (under 1K impressions, 0 saves, 0 comments).** Ad spend won't fix bad content. Diagnose why the topic underperformed first.

2. **Topic adjacent to or overlapping a recently-amplified topic.** If you just spent on Channel Pricing, don't spend on Rebate Audit immediately after — let the audience absorb the first ad before the second.

3. **Toolkit without a working welcome sequence.** If the welcome sequence for a Toolkit isn't fully built and tested, ad spend captures leads that never receive the conversion follow-up. Pure waste.

4. **Toolkit on Tier 3 pillar (workflow / member experience).** These don't convert to Contract Review demand; the ad spend doesn't pay back regardless of CPL.

5. **During a launch crisis (e.g., new welcome sequence has deliverability issues).** Don't pour leads into a broken funnel. Fix the funnel first.

6. **When organic CAC is lower than estimated paid CAC.** If organic posts are producing Substack subs at $10 effective cost (zero marginal cost × strong conversion) and paid Substack subs would cost $40-60 each via Lead Gen Form ads, the math says spend more on organic content production, not on ads.

## Decision flow when a new Toolkit ships

When a new Toolkit-paired Thursday LinkedIn post + Substack Field Note ship, run this decision sequence:

```
Step 1 — Wait 14 days for organic data
  ↓
Step 2 — Score the Toolkit on the 4-factor rubric using actual organic data
  ↓
Step 3 — If total score ≥ 13 → schedule a paid Lead Gen Form ad for 14 days
  → If total score 9-12 → run small-budget test ($300) over 14 days
  → If total score < 9 → no ad spend; revisit in 60-90 days after content iteration
  ↓
Step 4 — Monitor ad performance against the kill criteria in tracking_dashboard.md
  → CPL > $75 for 3 consecutive days → pause and diagnose
  → Welcome sequence completion < 30% → investigate sequence quality
  → Substack subscribe rate from ad < 5% over 30 days → audience targeting issue
  ↓
Step 5 — At Day 30 of ad: if metrics are healthy, scale to next Toolkit. If not, refresh creative or pull spend.
```

This framework keeps ad spend allocation disciplined. It prevents the most common failure mode — burning budget on Toolkits that organic data already says won't convert.

## Ad creative refresh cadence

Once a Toolkit's ad is running, creative fatigue sets in around 14-21 days. After that, CPL rises and conversion drops. Mitigation:

- **Day 14:** check creative metrics. If CPL is flat, continue. If rising, queue a refresh.
- **Day 21-28:** ship a creative variant (different image canvas, different headline) running alongside the original. Use the A/B variants documented in each ad creative spec file (`ad_creative/0N_*.md`).
- **Day 35:** retire the lower-performing variant; promote the winner to primary.
- **Day 42-56:** queue the next variant if performance starts dropping again.

Three-variant rotation per Toolkit gives ~60-90 days of effective ad spend before you need a fundamentally new creative. Build a backlog of variants per Toolkit during the slower content weeks.

---

## Quarterly review questions

At the end of each quarter, evaluate the ad spend portfolio with these questions:

1. **Which Toolkits produced the lowest CPL?** Concentrate spend on those.
2. **Which Toolkits produced the best welcome-sequence completion rate?** These are the Toolkits whose audience matches the welcome sequence well — increase spend.
3. **Which Toolkits produced Contract Review consult inquiries?** This is the bottom-line metric. Even high-CPL Toolkits are worth it if they produce signed engagements.
4. **Which Toolkits should be retired from active spend?** If a Toolkit has been running for 3+ months with no Contract Review inquiries, it's not paying back. Retire it.
5. **Should a Toolkit be promoted from Tier 2 to Tier 1?** If a mechanic-specific Toolkit is consistently producing high-quality leads + Contract Review inquiries, it may belong in the rotated Tier 1 ad spend stack.
6. **Are seasonal Toolkits running at the right time?** RFP Scoring should be heaviest Q3; PBR Framework should be heaviest June and November (mid-year and year-end review windows).

## What this framework changes for PBS's first 90 days of ad spend

When ad spend turns on (Phase 5 of `email_gated_toolkit_implementation_guide.md`):

**Days 1-14:** Run only Channel Pricing and Rebate Report Audit ads (the two highest-scoring Toolkits at 17/20). Test budget: $750 each = $1,500 total. Validate the funnel works end-to-end.

**Days 15-30:** If days 1-14 produce positive metrics (CPL under $50, welcome sequence completion above 35%, at least one Contract Review inquiry), expand to include the 3 Tier 1 evergreen toolkits (Contract Review Readiness, Optimize-vs-Go-to-Market, PBR). Test budget: $500 each = $1,500 additional.

**Days 31-60:** Add the next tier of mechanic-specific (PBM Compensation, GER Audit, Quarterly Reporting). Test budget: $500 each.

**Days 60-90:** Concentrate spend on the 3-5 Toolkits with the lowest CPL + highest Contract Review conversion. Pull spend from underperformers.

**Day 90:** Quarterly review. Repeat.

Modify the scoring rubric as PBS accumulates more data — the factor weightings can be tuned based on what actually correlates with Contract Review revenue once 6-12 months of attribution data exists.*

## Audit Rights vs. Audit Reality: What Your PBM Contract Actually Allows

**Publication:** Benefit Blind Spots
**Publish:** Monday, March 9, 2026 at 7:30 AM EST
**Length:** ~1,600 words (~6 min read)
**Tone:** Analytical authority
**Pillar:** PBM Contract Insights

---

### Article Body

Your PBM contract says you have audit rights.

But can you actually use them?

I reviewed a contract last month where the employer was confident they had full audit rights. They did, technically. But when we read the fine print, those rights were so restricted that exercising them was nearly impossible. What you cannot audit, you cannot verify. And PBM contracts are full of performance promises that remain unverifiable without meaningful audit provisions.

This issue breaks down how audit rights get limited, what red flag language to search for, and what strong audit provisions actually look like.

---

**Why Audit Rights Matter**

PBM contracts are performance-based agreements. Your PBM promises discount levels, rebate amounts, generic rates, service standards. These promises determine whether you are getting value.

Without audit rights, every promise is trust-based. You are relying on PBM-generated reports to verify PBM performance. This is not oversight. It is faith.

The 2024 PBMI survey found that only 38% of employers had conducted a PBM audit in the prior three years. Among those who did audit, the average recovery exceeded $1.2 million for large employers.

The math is straightforward. Employers who audit tend to find money. Employers who do not audit may be leaving money on the table, but they will never know.

---

**Common Audit Limitations**

Most PBM contracts include audit provisions. Few provide unrestricted audit access. These are the limitations we see most frequently:

*Time Limitations*

Many contracts restrict when audits can occur. Common provisions:
- Audits must be initiated within 12-18 months of the claim period
- 90-day advance notice required
- Only one audit per contract year

The combined effect is a narrow window that makes meaningful auditing difficult. By the time you complete one audit cycle, earlier periods may have aged out of auditability.

*Scope Limitations*

Contracts often limit what can be audited:
- Claims data only (not rebates)
- Sampling limited to X% of claims
- Certain claim types excluded (specialty, mail order)
- Administrative fees off-limits
- Network arrangements excluded

Each exclusion represents a category where PBM performance cannot be verified.

*Auditor Approval Requirements*

Some contracts require PBM approval of your audit firm. While framed as ensuring "qualified" auditors, this provision gives the PBM influence over who examines their performance.

Stronger language specifies that auditors must meet reasonable professional standards, without requiring PBM approval.

*Recovery Caps*

This may be the most problematic limitation: caps on what you can recover regardless of audit findings.

I have seen this play out. A contract allows audit recoveries up to 2% of annual fees. The audit finds $500,000 in discrepancies, but 2% of fees equals $100,000. You recover $100,000.

*Illustrative example for educational purposes. Actual amounts vary by plan.*

Recovery caps eliminate the financial consequence of underperformance beyond a predetermined threshold.

*Cure Period Provisions*

Some contracts give the PBM a cure period: time to correct identified issues before penalties apply. While reasonable for prospective issues, cure periods should not eliminate accountability for past underperformance.

*Confidentiality Restrictions*

Contracts may restrict sharing audit findings. This prevents employers from using audit results in RFP processes or sharing insights with consultants.

---

**Red Flag Language to Search For**

Pull up your PBM contract and search for these phrases:

*"Reasonable access"* - What defines reasonable? The PBM decides. Stronger: "full and complete access"

*"Material discrepancy"* - Often defined as discrepancies exceeding 2-5%. Anything below the threshold requires no correction. Stronger: "any discrepancy"

*"At PBM's sole discretion"* - Appears throughout many contracts. Each instance represents PBM control over interpretation.

*"Commercially reasonable"* - Undefined flexibility. What is commercially reasonable to a PBM may not serve your interests.

*"May"* vs. *"Shall"* - "May" creates permission without obligation. "Shall" creates obligation. Audit provisions using "may" are weaker than those using "shall."

*"Summary data"* - If your audit rights extend only to summary data, you cannot verify claim-level accuracy.

---

**What Strong Audit Language Includes**

Minimum standards we recommend:

*Access:* Full access to claims data, rebate data, administrative records, and network arrangements. Member-level detail, not just summaries.

*Timing:* Audit window of at least 24 months from claim date. Ability to conduct multiple audits per year if warranted.

*Auditor Selection:* You select the auditor, not the PBM. The auditor should meet reasonable professional standards, but no PBM approval required.

*Scope:* All claim types included. No exclusions for specialty, mail order, or specific drug categories. Rebates auditable.

*Recovery:* No caps on recoveries. All discrepancies subject to correction regardless of magnitude.

*Confidentiality:* Employer owns audit results. Reasonable confidentiality of PBM proprietary information, but findings usable in employer's business decisions.

*No Cure Period for Past Performance:* Cure provisions apply prospectively only. Past underperformance requires remediation.

---

**Negotiation Leverage Points**

When can you push for stronger audit provisions?

*RFP Process:* This is your maximum leverage point. Include audit requirements in your RFP specifications. Evaluate responses partly on audit provision strength.

*Contract Renewal:* Moderate leverage. Use renewal negotiation to strengthen weak provisions. The cost of member transition gives you room to push.

*Mid-Contract:* Minimal leverage. PBMs have little incentive to expand audit rights mid-term. Focus on other improvements and address audit provisions at renewal.

What PBMs will typically concede:
- Extended audit windows (18-24 months)
- Removal of auditor approval requirements
- Inclusion of specialty claims in scope
- Reasonable reduction of recovery caps

What may require walking away:
- Full rebate auditability (some PBMs refuse)
- No recovery caps at all
- Complete scope inclusion (mail order ownership arrangements often protected)

---

**Beyond the Contract: Audit Execution**

Having audit rights and exercising them are different things entirely.

*Audit Firm Selection*

Not all audit firms are equal. In our experience, you want to look for:
- PBM contract expertise (not just pharmacy expertise)
- Independence from PBM relationships
- Transparent fee structures (contingency arrangements create perverse incentives)
- Track record with employers of similar size

*Timing and Frequency*

We recommend:
- First audit within 12 months of contract start
- Annual audits for large employers (5,000+ lives)
- Biennial audits for smaller employers
- Triggered audits when performance reports show concerning trends

*What to Examine*

Prioritize areas where PBM self-reporting is least reliable:
- Discount guarantee compliance
- Rebate passthrough accuracy
- Generic dispensing rate calculations
- Network pharmacy compliance
- Administrative fee accuracy

---

**The Position We Take**

Audit rights are foundational to PBM accountability.

I tell plan sponsors this all the time: a PBM contract without meaningful audit provisions is a trust-based arrangement dressed up in contractual language. Every guarantee becomes a suggestion. Every performance metric becomes an estimate.

We advise employers to treat audit provisions with the same rigor as pricing guarantees. The audit clause determines whether pricing guarantees are verifiable.

If your contract limits audit rights to the point of meaninglessness, you have two options: negotiate better terms at renewal, or acknowledge that you are operating on trust rather than verification.

The employers who audit tend to find money. The question is whether your contract allows you to look.

### Newsletter Body

Your PBM contract says you have audit rights. But can you actually use them?

We review hundreds of PBM contracts annually. The gap between assumed audit rights and actual contract language is one of the most consequential blind spots we see. What you cannot audit, you cannot verify.

**Common Limitations That Gut Your Access**

*Time Restrictions:* Audits must be initiated within 12-18 months. 90-day notice required. One audit per year maximum. The combined effect is a narrow window that makes meaningful auditing difficult.

*Scope Restrictions:* Claims data only, not rebates. Sampling limited to X% of claims. Specialty, mail order, or admin fees excluded. Each exclusion represents a category where performance cannot be verified.

*Recovery Caps:* Maximum recoveries capped at 2% of annual fees regardless of findings. If the audit finds $500K in discrepancies but the cap is $100K, you recover $100K. *Illustrative example for educational purposes. Actual amounts vary by plan.*

*Auditor Approval:* PBM must approve your audit firm. This gives the entity being audited influence over who examines their performance.

**Red Flag Language to Search For**

Pull up your contract and find these phrases:

- "Reasonable access" - What defines reasonable? The PBM decides.
- "Material discrepancy" - Often defined as 2-5%. Anything below requires no correction.
- "At PBM's sole discretion" - Each instance represents PBM control.
- "May" vs. "Shall" - "May" creates permission. "Shall" creates obligation.
- "Summary data" - Cannot verify claim-level accuracy.

**What Strong Audit Language Includes**

- Full access to claims, rebates, and administrative records
- 24+ month audit windows
- Employer selects auditor (no PBM approval)
- All claim types included, no exclusions
- No recovery caps
- Employer owns audit results

**The Numbers That Matter**

Only 38% of employers audited their PBM in the prior three years (2024 PBMI survey). Among those who did, average recovery exceeded $1.2 million for large employers.

Employers who audit tend to find money. The question is whether your contract allows you to look.

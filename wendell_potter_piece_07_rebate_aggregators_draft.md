## ▼ DRAFT BEGINS ▼

**Working title: The Middlemen Your PBM Built Offshore to Stand Between You and Your Rebates**

Imagine your contract says: "We will pass through 100% of the rebates we receive." Then imagine that before your vendor receives anything, a different company takes a cut. The math in your contract is performed honestly, to the penny, on what is left. The company that took the cut has a bland name you have never heard. Your vendor's parent company owns it. That arrangement is not hypothetical. It is the rebate aggregator, and this chapter is the field guide.

Twice already this series has gestured at these entities and moved on: once in the ownership chapter, once inside the GLP-1 money. The last piece noted that even the federal diagnosis barely reached them. On the map, we are standing on the second revenue stream, rebate retention, at the segment of the pipe that was deliberately built out of view.

Here is what happened, in plain sequence. Manufacturer rebates became controversial, and PBMs began promising full pass-through. Around the same time, between roughly 2019 and 2021, each of the big three built or designated a group purchasing organization to negotiate manufacturer payments on its behalf. CVS Health's Caremark works through Ascent Health Services. UnitedHealth's OptumRx works through Emisar Pharma Services. Cigna's Express Scripts works through Zinc Health Services. Some of these entities are organized overseas, which public reporting has noted and which matters for a simple reason: an entity outside the country is harder for American regulators, auditors, and litigants to see into.

Now run the money through it. The manufacturer pays for formulary position. The aggregator negotiates that payment and keeps a portion as its own fee. What remains goes to the PBM. The PBM then passes through 100% of what it received, exactly as the contract promised. Every sentence in that chain is true, and the plan sponsor's rebate check is still lighter than the manufacturer's payment, because the pass-through promise attached to the last segment of the pipe, not the whole pipe. Simply put, the promise is honest and the plumbing is not.

In the contracts I review, you can watch this play out in the definitions. Some contracts name the aggregator and bring its retention inside the pass-through promise; those tend to be the contracts negotiated by sponsors who knew to ask. Many contracts never mention the aggregator at all, and in those, the money that stops at the aggregator simply does not exist as far as the contract's math is concerned. The difference between the two is not luck. It is whether anyone at the table knew the entity was there.

Why have regulators lagged? Three reasons stack. The entities are new, and most policymakers have never heard of them. The structure is deliberately dull; nothing draws less scrutiny than a procurement subsidiary with a generic name. And where the organization is offshore, the ordinary tools, subpoenas, audits, and state filing requirements, run out of reach. Opacity was not a side effect of this design. As best anyone outside the companies can tell, it was the design.

The reform is not complicated, which is worth saying plainly because complexity is this structure's best defense. First, registration and disclosure: any entity negotiating manufacturer payments connected to an American health plan's utilization should register, disclose its ownership, and file its fee schedules with insurance regulators, the way other middlemen in regulated industries already do. Second, and this is the load-bearing plank: pass-through legislation should follow the dollar, not the entity. The rule should read, in effect, that every payment a manufacturer makes in connection with a plan's utilization flows to the plan, whichever hand touches it on the way. Write it that way and the aggregator layer stops being a hiding place, because there is nothing left to hide behind. States can build the same rule for the plans they regulate while Congress works on the rest.

My view is that the aggregator layer is the cleanest example of structural concealment in the entire PBM economy, and I choose the word carefully: not fraud, concealment, achieved through corporate architecture rather than falsehood. The fix is a definition. The obstacle is the lobbying.

One more revenue mechanism belongs in this act. It was banned by regulators in one market, publicly celebrated as a win, and then quietly reassembled under new names. The next chapter is about what happened after the government killed the DIR fee.

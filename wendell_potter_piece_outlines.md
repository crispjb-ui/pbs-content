## Opening Piece: PBM Economics 101 (the Map)

**Working title:** *The Five Revenue Streams That Run Through Every PBM Contract — and the Five Reforms Each One Requires*

**Opening metaphor:**
> *Imagine a tollbooth on a highway where the toll collector also owns the cars passing through, the gas stations along the road, the rest stops, and the road repair contracts. The same entity sets the toll, collects the toll, audits whether the right toll was charged, and reports the result to the highway commission. That is the modern PBM business model — not in metaphor but in mechanics. Five revenue streams run through every PBM contract; one entity collects on all five and discloses, in full, on none of them.*

**Thesis:** The fragmented reform debate over PBMs persists because the business model is fragmented. The structural picture is not. Five revenue streams flow from every plan into the PBM, the disclosure floor on each is uneven, and each requires its own structural fix. This piece is the map. The pieces that follow argue each reform.

**Named villains:** CVS Caremark, Express Scripts, OptumRx as the collective holders of the five-stream model. PCMA as the industry-defense arm. The vertical-integration structure that lets one entity collect on all five mechanisms simultaneously.

**Section beats (~900 words):**

1. **Cold open with the metaphor** (~120 words). Establish the five-stream + single-vendor framing immediately. Name the structural picture.
2. **The five revenue streams** (~400 words; ~80 words each):
   - **Spread Pricing.** The AWP gap; what the plan pays vs. what the pharmacy receives. The MAC pricing schedule, the dispensing fee, and the spread itself. Where the math hides.
   - **Rebate Retention.** What comes back from manufacturers and what doesn't get passed through. Rebate-aggregator carve-outs that sit outside the pass-through guarantee.
   - **Administrative Fees.** The named line items (per-claim, per-member-per-month) and the unnamed line items (formulary management, clinical, network access) in the same family — five definitions the PBM wrote.
   - **Manufacturer-Direct Payments.** Formulary placement fees, manufacturer education funding, rebate aggregator side-channels. The compensation streams the manufacturer-to-PBM contract documents but the plan sponsor never sees.
   - **Owned-Pharmacy Margin.** The per-claim margin earned when the PBM-owned channel fills. Specialty routing is the largest single source.
3. **Why fragmented reform fails** (~120 words). Single-stream regulation pushes margin to the unregulated stream(s). Spread bans push margin into rebate retention; rebate-pass-through bans push margin into admin fees; admin-fee caps push margin into owned-pharmacy routing. The structural picture has to be reformed structurally.
4. **The five reforms previewed** (~200 words; ~40 words each):
   - **Spread:** Mandatory FTC disclosure (Piece 1 will argue the full case).
   - **Vertical integration:** Federal contractual firewall + plan sponsor audit rights, with divestiture mandate as the structural endpoint (Piece 2).
   - **GLP-1 / category-specific:** HHS threshold-trigger disclosure rule for any drug class crossing $X billion in annual U.S. spend (Piece 3).
   - **Copay accumulator structural firewall:** Federal disclosure rule for plans using accumulator or maximizer programs; PBM firewall when the same vendor owns the specialty pharmacy and administers the program (Piece 4).
   - **Channel steering / white-bagging:** Site-of-care neutrality regulation; per-channel net cost disclosure before routing (Piece 5).
5. **Why this map matters for self-funded plan sponsors right now** (~80 words). The audit posture is the same regardless of which reform moves first. Pull the contract; sort the revenue streams; document the disclosure gaps. The reform argument doesn't require the legislation to pass.
6. **Opinion-stated-openly closing** (~80 words). "In my view, the reason PBM reform has stalled at the federal level is that every fight has been framed as a single-stream fight. The reformers have been chasing the margin around the contract. The fix is to put the structural picture on the record and reform all five streams at once. The pieces that follow argue each reform; this piece exists so the map is on the record first."

**Pull-quote candidates:**

- *"Every PBM contract I audit has all five revenue streams baked into it. Most plan sponsors only see one — the admin fee, the smallest of the five — clearly named in the contract."*
- *"The reform conversation is fragmented because the business model is fragmented. The structural picture is not. Five revenue streams. Five reforms. One vendor."*
- *"Spread bans push the margin into rebate retention. Rebate-pass-through bans push the margin into admin fees. Admin-fee caps push the margin into owned-pharmacy routing. Single-stream reform is the reformer chasing the margin around the contract."*

**Prescriptive ending:** Five named regulatory or legislative actions, one per revenue stream, each previewed with the responsible regulator named. Closes with the explicit signpost: each subsequent piece in the column argues one of these reforms in detail; the map exists so the structural picture is on the record before the individual fights are joined.

**Cascade-week PBS angle:** "The Five Revenue Streams Audit — How to Map Where Your Plan's Margin Goes" — extends the existing PBM Compensation Audit Worksheet (Tier 1 toolkit, already shipped). High-leverage: the Map piece cascades into a re-deployment + light refresh of an existing toolkit rather than a new build. Pairs naturally with W24 H1/H2 Review (the Monday topic that cascade week was already planned to be): "as part of your mid-year review, audit the five streams." Monday newsletter opens with the Map piece pull-quote; Wednesday roundup features it; Thursday Field Note operationalizes one stream (likely spread or admin-fee mapping) as the deeper companion.

**Wednesday text-post hook (cascade-week amplifier):**
> *"Your PBM has five revenue streams running through your contract. You probably only see one of them clearly named: the admin fee, the smallest of the five. The other four are where most of the margin lives."*

**Why the Opening Piece comes first (strategic note):** This piece establishes Ginny's PBM-specific lane (distinct from Ron's and Wendell's broader health-system frame) on Day 1 of the contributorship rather than waiting for Piece 3 to do the lane-setting work. It also creates a single anchor every subsequent piece can link back to: each subsequent piece argues one of the five reforms previewed here, so readers always know how the current piece fits the larger argument. The trade-off vs starting with Spread Pricing is one extra cycle before the first single-issue prescriptive piece lands. That cost is worth paying because the map piece is itself prescriptive at the systems level (names five reforms with regulators) and because the map gives every subsequent piece more leverage.

## Piece 1: Spread Pricing

**Working title:** *Three Prices for the Same Drug — Why the FTC's Next Move on PBMs Should Be Mandatory Spread Disclosure*

**Opening metaphor:**
> *A wholesaler that charges retail to its customer, pays wholesale to its supplier, and pockets the difference is legitimate when the customer knows the markup. PBM spread pricing is the same trade with one critical difference: the customer never sees the markup, and the contract was written to keep it that way.*

**Thesis:** PBM spread pricing is a structural deception enabled by contract opacity. The FTC has the authority and the data. The next regulatory move should be mandatory per-NDC spread disclosure filed quarterly with state Departments of Insurance and made available to plan sponsors by right.

**Named villains:** CVS Caremark, Express Scripts, OptumRx. PCMA (the trade association) as the regulatory-defense arm.

**Section beats (~800 words):**

1. **Cold open with the metaphor** (~100 words). Establish the markup-without-disclosure framing immediately.
2. **The mechanic explained** (~150 words). How spread pricing works inside a PBM contract: the MAC pricing schedule, the dispensing fee, the spread itself, where the math hides. Proprietary anchor here.
3. **PCMA's framing vs. the reality** (~150 words). PCMA argues spread pricing is "risk transfer" that lets PBMs offer "predictable, stable drug costs" to small/mid plans. When the middleman keeps 80% of the dollar gap, that is not risk transfer.
4. **Why current transparency rules don't reach this** (~100 words). State-by-state inconsistency, ERISA preemption gaps for self-insured plans, contract opacity layers.
5. **The prescription** (~150 words). Mandatory per-NDC spread disclosure at the PBM level. Quarterly reports to state DOIs. Plan sponsor access by right. FTC enforcement under Section 5 authority.
6. **Opinion-stated-openly closing** (~100 words). "In my view this is the cleanest reform the FTC could move on, and they have the data already. The interim report documented the mechanic. The final order should require disclosure."

**Pull-quote candidates:**

- *"I see spread-pricing language in nearly every contract I audit. Most plan sponsors don't know it is there until someone reads the definitions section out loud."*
- *"PCMA calls this 'risk transfer.' When the middleman keeps 80% of the money, that is not risk transfer. That is just taking the money."*
- *"The FTC already has the data from the 2024 interim report. What is missing is the enforcement instrument."*

**Prescriptive ending:** Mandatory per-NDC spread disclosure required at the PBM level. Quarterly reports filed with state Departments of Insurance. Plan sponsor access by right (not by negotiation). FTC final order under existing Section 5 authority; no new legislation required.

**Cascade-week PBS angle:** "Three Audit Passes That Surface Spread in Your Plan" — Plan Sponsor Toolkit handout extending W18 Channel Pricing material. PBS Monday newsletter opens with the spread-pricing pull quote.

**Wednesday text-post hook (cascade-week amplifier):**
> *"CVS Caremark sets the price your plan pays. They also set the price the pharmacy receives. They keep the difference, and the contract does not require them to tell you the size of it."*

## Piece 2: Vertical Integration in PBMs

**Working title:** *Your PBM Owns Your Specialty Pharmacy. Your Insurer Owns Your PBM. Here Is the Bill That Should Break That Up.*

**Opening metaphor:**
> *Imagine if your auto insurer also owned the repair shop, the parts supplier, and the salvage yard, and got to decide which one your claim went to. The repair bill comes back; you have no way to test whether the parts were aftermarket or OEM, whether the labor was fairly priced, or whether the salvage discount was passed back to you. The auto-insurance regulator would have stopped that vertical integration before it consolidated. The PBM regulator didn't.*

**Thesis:** PBM vertical integration with insurers, specialty pharmacies, and provider groups is the structural problem the FTC's 2024 interim report documented but did not solve. The two paths forward are federal divestiture mandate or contractual firewall requirements with audit rights.

**Named villains:** UnitedHealth / OptumRx / Optum Specialty / Optum Health. CVS Health / Caremark / CVS Specialty / Aetna. Cigna / Express Scripts / Accredo / Evernorth.

**Section beats (~900 words):**

1. **Cold open with the metaphor** (~120 words).
2. **What three-way integration looks like inside a single plan year** (~200 words). The PBM that owns the specialty pharmacy. The PBM that writes the prior-authorization rule. The PBM that reports the savings. Three roles, one vendor, no independent voice. Proprietary anchor: "in the contracts I review, the specialty routing clause is rarely as exclusive as the routing pattern."
3. **The data point and its PBM analog** (~150 words). Ron Howrigon's recent piece named a 17% pay differential for UnitedHealth captive physicians. The PBM-side analog is the routing differential: PBM-owned specialty pharmacy share is materially higher than the network-balance language in the contract suggests.
4. **Why ERISA preemption frustrates state action** (~100 words). State pharmacy commissioners trying to regulate PBM vertical integration hit federal preemption for self-insured plans. The federal regulator who could act is the FTC; the FTC has flagged the problem but has not moved.
5. **The two prescriptive paths** (~200 words):
    - **Path A — Divestiture mandate.** A federal statute requiring vertically integrated PBMs above a market-concentration threshold to divest specialty pharmacy operations. High political cost, long timeline.
    - **Path B — Contractual firewall + audit rights.** Federal floor requiring contractual separation of pricing, routing, and reporting functions when the vendor is vertically integrated. Plan sponsor audit rights for routing patterns. Achievable in current Congress; produces measurable employer benefit even before divestiture moves.
6. **Opinion-stated-openly closing** (~130 words). "My view is that Path B is the more achievable reform in the current political moment, and it is the path that produces measurable employer benefit immediately. Divestiture should remain on the table as the structural endpoint. But contractual firewall plus audit rights is what plan sponsors need this year."

**Pull-quote candidates:**

- *"Your PBM owns the specialty pharmacy that fills your high-cost drugs. Your PBM also writes the prior-authorization rule that decides which pharmacy you can use. Your PBM also reports the savings to you. Three jobs. Same vendor. No independent voice."*
- *"The PBM that owns the specialty pharmacy doesn't have to win the spread argument. It wins it structurally."*
- *"Vertical integration is the reason 'pass-through pricing' contracts still produce wide net-cost variance. The pass-through is honest; the routing is the leverage."*

**Prescriptive ending:** Federal divestiture mandate (Path A, structural endpoint) OR federal contractual firewall + plan sponsor audit rights mandated by ERISA amendment (Path B, near-term achievable). State pass-through legislation as parallel track for cost-disclosure floor.

**Cascade-week PBS angle:** "The Five Contract Clauses That Lock Your Specialty Fills Into the PBM-Owned Pharmacy" — Plan Sponsor Toolkit handout. Extends W22 Biosimilar material.

**Wednesday text-post hook:**
> *"OptumRx owns Optum Specialty Pharmacy. OptumRx also writes the prior-authorization rule that decides which pharmacy your member can use. OptumRx also reports the savings to you. Three jobs. Same vendor. No independent voice."*

## Piece 3: GLP-1 Coverage and the PBM Profit Layer

**Working title:** *The GLP-1 Access Debate Is the Wrong Debate. The PBM Profit Layer Is the Conversation Nobody Is Having.*

**Opening metaphor:**
> *Imagine a tariff war where the tariffs go to a third party that nobody is talking about. The manufacturer says, "our list price is high because rebates are high." The plan says, "we pay high prices." The PBM in the middle collects the difference and goes silent on the question of who that difference actually flows back to. That is the GLP-1 economy.*

**Thesis:** The current GLP-1 access debate is misframed. Medicare coverage timing and manufacturer-pressure framings miss the structural problem. The list-price-vs-net-cost gap on the highest-leverage drug class in modern memory is where reform should focus.

**Named villains:** CVS Caremark, OptumRx, Express Scripts. Rebate aggregators named in passing (Ascent, Emisar, Zinc — foreshadows Piece 7).

**Section beats (~900 words):**

1. **Cold open with the tariff metaphor** (~100 words).
2. **What "GLP-1 access" actually means in employer plans today** (~200 words). Formulary tiering, PA workflow, exclusions for the weight-management indication, the proprietary anchor about how often the obesity indication is explicitly excluded from coverage in the contracts Ginny reviews.
3. **The list price vs. net cost gap on GLP-1s, structurally illustrated** (~200 words). Without fabricating specific numbers: GLP-1 is the highest-leverage class in modern pharmaceutical memory. The gap between list and net is wider than nearly any class on the market. The plan sees list; the PBM sees net; the difference is the profit layer.
4. **Why the Medicare and manufacturer-pressure framings miss the structural problem** (~150 words). The Medicare timing question is real but secondary. The manufacturer-list-price question is real but capped by rebate flow. The middle layer — the PBM-aggregator capture — is where the actual money is, and nobody is fighting about it because it's the part nobody can see.
5. **The prescription** (~200 words). HHS Secretary authority to require per-NDC net-cost disclosure for any drug class crossing a threshold of annual U.S. spend ($X billion). Class definition by CMS to prevent manufacturer or PBM gaming. State Medicaid programs pilot the model first; commercial market follows.
6. **Opinion-stated-openly closing** (~150 words). "My take is that GLP-1 is the test case for what every high-cost class will look like in five years. Get this right and we have a template. Get it wrong and we'll be having this same conversation again about gene therapy, then again about cardiac amyloidosis therapy, then again about the next breakthrough."

**Pull-quote candidates:**

- *"In every other class of drug, the gap between list price and net cost is widest where the PBM has the most leverage. GLP-1s are the highest-leverage class in modern memory. The reform conversation should be about the gap."*
- *"Your employee's GLP-1 cost is what the plan sees. Your PBM's net cost is what the plan pays. The other piece is the part nobody is naming."*
- *"Reform the GLP-1 channel and you have the template for the next class. Don't, and we'll be having this conversation again next year about gene therapy."*

**Prescriptive ending:** HHS Secretary authority to require per-NDC net-cost disclosure for any drug class crossing an annual U.S. spend threshold. CMS-defined class boundaries. State Medicaid programs as the first deployment.

**Cascade-week PBS angle:** "GLP-1 Channel Pricing — What to Demand in Your Next PBM RFP."

**Wednesday text-post hook:**
> *"Your employee's GLP-1 cost: ~$1,200 a month. Your PBM's net cost on the same prescription: roughly a third of that. The other two-thirds is the part everyone is fighting about and nobody is naming."*

## Piece 4: Copay Accumulator Programs — The Structural Conflict of Interest

**Working title:** *The Conflict of Interest at the Heart of Copay Accumulator Programs*

**Opening metaphor:**
> *Imagine a charitable foundation that gives scholarships to college students to help them afford tuition. Now imagine the foundation hires an administrator to process those scholarships. Now imagine the administrator also owns the college, decides which courses qualify for the scholarship, and decides whether the scholarship counts toward the student's tuition bill or simply toward the college's revenue. Same dollars. Different beneficiary depending on how the administrator counts. One entity controlling every decision in the transaction.*

**Thesis:** The reform conversation on copay accumulator and maximizer programs has split between patient advocates (ban the programs) and plan sponsors (preserve them as a cost lever). Both positions miss the structural problem in the middle: the same PBM administers the program, owns the specialty pharmacy dispensing the drug, and writes the formulary defining program eligibility. The reform is the structural firewall and member disclosure, not the prohibition.

**Named villains:** SaveOnSP (Express Scripts subsidiary), PrudentRx (CVS Caremark), Variable Copay (OptumRx variant). The vertical-integration structure that lets one PBM control all three transaction variables simultaneously.

**Section beats (~900 words):**

1. **Cold open with the scholarship-administrator metaphor** (~120 words).
2. **What accumulator and maximizer programs actually are, named honestly across three perspectives** (~200 words). Mechanic: manufacturer coupon redirected from member deductible to plan expense column; maximizer is the same mechanic with the math calibrated to extract maximum value across a year of fills. Patient perspective: affordability cliffs on specialty therapy. Plan-sponsor perspective: legitimate cost-containment lever when designed and disclosed correctly. PBM perspective: revenue stream tied to vertical-integration structure.
3. **The proprietary anchor and the structural problem** (~200 words). "In the contracts I review, the accumulator program is almost always administered by the same PBM that owns the specialty pharmacy dispensing the drug and writes the formulary that defines the drug as accumulator-eligible. Three roles. One vendor." Name SaveOnSP, PrudentRx, Variable Copay. The structural conflict: PBM can write formulary placement to favor drugs with the largest manufacturer assistance available, route fills to its own specialty pharmacy, administer the crediting math to maximize PBM revenue rather than plan savings.
4. **The state law patchwork** (~120 words). Twenty-six states plus DC and Puerto Rico have enacted accumulator-related protections, most focused on patient-protection in fully insured plans. The state laws get the patient-protection question half right. They miss the structural problem entirely. ERISA preempts most state law for self-insured plans (the majority of employer coverage).
5. **The reframe** (~150 words). Most reform proposals reach for prohibition; that misses the legitimate use case. Plan sponsors should retain the right to choose accumulator or maximizer programs as a cost-containment lever, provided two structural conditions are met: (1) disclosure to members at enrollment in plain language with affordability consequences named, (2) the PBM administering the program must not also own the specialty pharmacy dispensing the drug or write the formulary defining program eligibility.
6. **The prescription** (~150 words). Three named actions in order of political achievability: (a) federal disclosure rule via HHS sub-regulatory guidance or ERISA amendment, (b) structural firewall statute prohibiting PBMs from administering programs AND owning specialty pharmacy dispensing eligible drugs, (c) formulary-design firewall requiring clinical-criteria basis for accumulator eligibility, auditable by plan sponsor.
7. **Opinion-stated-openly closing** (~80 words). "What I would argue is that this is the most under-named reform in the PBM economy. The reform conversation has been split between patient advocates who want accumulator programs banned and plan sponsors who want them preserved as a cost lever. Both positions miss the structural problem in the middle. The reform is the firewall. The reform is the disclosure. The reform is not the prohibition."

**Pull-quote candidates:**

- *"In the contracts I review, the accumulator program is almost always administered by the same PBM that owns the specialty pharmacy dispensing the drug and writes the formulary that defines the drug as accumulator-eligible. Three roles. One vendor."*
- *"Manufacturer copay cards are a legitimate patient affordability tool. Plan-sponsor accumulator programs can be a legitimate cost-containment lever. The same vendor should not be allowed to control both ends of the transaction."*
- *"The reform is the firewall. The reform is the disclosure. The reform is not the prohibition."*

**Prescriptive ending:** Three structural actions: (1) federal disclosure rule via HHS guidance or ERISA amendment, (2) federal firewall statute prohibiting PBMs from administering accumulator programs while also owning specialty pharmacy dispensing eligible drugs, (3) formulary-design firewall requiring clinical-criteria basis for eligibility, auditable by plan sponsor.

**Cascade-week PBS angle:** "The Firewall Audit: Does Your PBM Administer the Program AND Own the Specialty Pharmacy?" — Plan Sponsor Toolkit handout walking an HR Director or benefits manager through a self-audit of conflict-of-interest exposure in their current PBM setup.

**Wednesday text-post hook:**
> *"Your PBM decides which drugs are accumulator-eligible. Your PBM owns the specialty pharmacy that fills them. Your PBM administers the crediting math. Three jobs, one vendor, and the difference goes to the PBM."*

## Piece 5: White-Bagging / Channel Steering

**Working title:** *Same Drug. Three Channels. Three Prices. One PBM Setting All of Them.*

**Opening metaphor:**
> *Imagine being charged three different rates at the gas pump depending on which pump you pulled into, with no posted prices, and only one of the pumps owned by the gas station you signed a contract with. That is specialty drug routing in employer plans.*

**Thesis:** Specialty drug channel steering is the most expensive opacity left in employer pharmacy benefits. Site-of-care neutrality regulation + plan sponsor audit rights are the reform paths.

**Named villains:** CVS Caremark / CVS Specialty, OptumRx / Optum Specialty, Express Scripts / Accredo. The site-of-care steering rules embedded in PA workflows.

**Section beats (~850 words):**

1. **Cold open with the gas-pump metaphor** (~100 words).
2. **How specialty channel steering works inside a plan** (~200 words). Proprietary anchor: "the channel-pricing audits we run reveal three different rates for the same drug, same dose, same patient — and the pharmacy is chosen by the PBM, not the patient." The mechanic: PA workflow routing, white-bagging mandates, specialty-drug-list designation.
3. **The economic case — what plans are paying** (~150 words). Characterize the spread structurally without fabricating numbers. The PBM-owned channel typically carries the widest margin; the independent specialty pharmacy and the limited-distribution channel typically carry tighter margins; the contract's MAC clause is silent on the difference.
4. **Why state white-bagging policy is moving** (~100 words). Recent state laws (Louisiana, Texas, others) restricting PBM-mandated white-bagging. The state momentum is real; the federal floor is missing.
5. **The prescription** (~200 words). Site-of-care neutrality regulation requiring transparent per-channel net cost disclosure before PBMs may route fills. ERISA amendment establishing plan sponsor audit rights for specialty pharmacy pass-through. State insurance commissioner authority empowered for non-ERISA plans.
6. **Opinion-stated-openly closing** (~100 words). "My view is white-bagging reform is where state legislatures are leading and federal regulators should follow. The states have done the documentation. The federal floor would not be a leap; it would be the federal regulator catching up."

**Pull-quote candidates:**

- *"Same drug. Same dose. Same patient. Three different prices, set by the same PBM. The contract does not require them to tell you how the spread is allocated."*
- *"The pharmacy is chosen by the PBM. The PBM is paid more when the choice routes to their own pharmacy. The contract does not require disclosure of either."*
- *"White-bagging reform is where state legislatures are leading. Federal regulators have the authority to follow and should."*

**Prescriptive ending:** Site-of-care neutrality regulation requiring per-channel net cost disclosure before PBM routing. ERISA amendment for plan sponsor audit rights for specialty pharmacy pass-through. State insurance commissioner authority strengthened where ERISA permits.

**Cascade-week PBS angle:** "Same Drug. Three Channels. Three Prices — Audit Worksheet" — extends the W18 Channel Pricing breakout content directly.

**Wednesday text-post hook:**
> *"Same drug. Same dose. Same patient. Three different prices, set by the same PBM. The contract does not require them to tell you how the spread is allocated."*

## Piece 6: The FTC PBM Report Follow-On

**Working title:** *The FTC Already Told Us the PBM Industry Is Broken. Here Is What Should Happen Next.*

**Opening metaphor:**
> *When the FTC publishes an interim staff report titled "Pharmacy Benefit Managers: The Powerful Middlemen Inflating Drug Costs and Squeezing Main Street Pharmacies," that is a finding. What follows is supposed to be policy. We are now well past the point where silence is acceptable.*

**Thesis:** The FTC's interim report documented PBM structural failures comprehensively. The diagnostic is complete. The treatment plan is what is missing. Two of the five next steps are within existing FTC authority; the other three require Congress.

**Named villains:** The Big 3 collectively. PCMA. The members of Congress who have blocked PBM reform votes in successive sessions.

**Section beats (~950 words):**

1. **Cold open with the report-as-diagnosis metaphor** (~100 words).
2. **What the FTC actually documented in the interim report** (~200 words). Concise summary of the major findings, anchored by Ginny's contract-review observations that match each: spread pricing patterns, vertical integration effects, formulary-control concentration, pharmacy reimbursement pressure. "Everything the FTC documented in that report is something I have been seeing in contracts I review for a decade."
3. **What has happened in the nearly two years since the report** (~150 words). The silence. The absence of an FTC final report or enforcement action. The failed legislative votes. The continuing PCMA defense posture. The PBM industry's structural moat reinforcement.
4. **The five named actions for 2026** (~300 words):
    - **Action 1 — FTC final report with enforcement actions, not another interim.** Under Section 5 authority. Doesn't require Congress.
    - **Action 2 — HHS Secretary directs CMS to require PBM transparency in Medicare Part D bids matching the FTC's documented disclosure failures.** Doesn't require Congress.
    - **Action 3 — ERISA amendment establishing federal audit-rights floor for self-insured plans.** Requires Congress.
    - **Action 4 — State pharmacy commissioner authority over PBM business practices, federally backstopped where ERISA preempts state action.** Requires Congress.
    - **Action 5 — DOJ antitrust review of pending PBM-Specialty Pharmacy / PBM-Insurer mergers.** Existing authority under Clayton Act; political will is the question.
5. **Opinion-stated-openly closing** (~200 words). "In my view the FTC has the authority to act on actions 1-2 unilaterally. Actions 3-5 require Congress, and Congress has not moved. The single most important step is FTC moving from interim report to enforcement before the political calendar absorbs everything else."

**Pull-quote candidates:**

- *"Everything the FTC documented in that report is something I have been seeing in contracts I review for a decade. The diagnostic is complete. The treatment plan is what is missing."*
- *"The FTC has the authority to act on at least two of the five actions without Congress. The interim report is the basis. Nearly two years in, what is missing is the final order."*
- *"When a federal regulator publishes an interim staff report and nearly two years later there is no final order, that is itself a policy choice. Reverse the choice."*

**Prescriptive ending:** Five named actions, two within existing FTC/HHS authority, three requiring Congress. The unilateral actions are the leverage point; the legislative actions are the structural endgame.

## Piece 7: Rebate Aggregators — The Offshore Middlemen

Reform path: GPO-style transparency rules + federal pass-through legislation that follows the rebate dollar regardless of which entity receives it.

## Piece 8: DIR Fees and Their Successor

**Working title:** *Why the 2024 CMS Rule on DIR Fees Was a Win — and Why the PBM Workarounds Are the Next Reform*

**Opening metaphor:**
> *Imagine a transaction fee that the bank charges retroactively, six months after the transaction cleared, in an amount the bank decides unilaterally based on metrics the bank also calculates. That was DIR fees in Medicare Part D pharmacy. CMS banned the retroactive structure in 2024. PBMs are now reconstructing the same economic effect with new names.*

**Thesis:** The 2024 CMS final rule on DIR (Direct and Indirect Remuneration) fees moved DIR to point-of-sale, ending retroactive clawbacks in Medicare Part D pharmacy. PBMs are reconstructing the same economic effect through new contractual mechanisms. Reform path: CMS guidance clarifying intent + parallel commercial-market disclosure rules.

**Named villains:** CVS Caremark, OptumRx, Express Scripts. PCMA's advocacy for the new workarounds.

**Section beats (~850 words):**

1. **Cold open with the retroactive-bank-fee metaphor** (~120 words).
2. **What DIR fees were and what changed in 2024** (~150 words). The retroactive clawback mechanic; the squeeze on independent pharmacies; the CMS final rule moving DIR to point-of-sale starting 2024. Proprietary anchor: "in the contracts I have reviewed since the rule took effect, the DIR economics did not disappear. They moved."
3. **The new mechanics PBMs are using** (~200 words). Pay-for-performance terms. Network access fees. GER reconciliation timing. Quality-bonus structures that are functionally equivalent to retroactive clawbacks. Each gets a sentence anchored by what Ginny has seen in contracts post-rule.
4. **Why the commercial-market analog is more important than most realize** (~100 words). The CMS rule applies to Medicare Part D. The commercial-market analog mechanics are unregulated. The same PBM-pharmacy economics that the CMS rule was designed to address are now playing out in commercial plans with no federal floor.
5. **The prescription** (~200 words). CMS sub-regulatory guidance clarifying that the DIR rule's intent extends to functional equivalents. FTC investigation into commercial-market analogs under existing Section 5 authority. Commercial-market disclosure parity legislation as the structural endgame.
6. **Opinion-stated-openly closing** (~80 words). "My take is that the 2024 rule was a real win, and the workarounds are real losses. Watching the new mechanics requires understanding them by name. The next regulatory move is naming them."

**Pull-quote candidates:**

- *"The 2024 CMS rule banned retroactive DIR clawbacks in Medicare Part D. The PBMs are now reconstructing the same economic effect with new names. In the contracts I review, the mechanic is visible — it just isn't called DIR anymore."*
- *"Retroactive clawbacks were the most regressive economics in Medicare Part D pharmacy. The CMS rule was a win. The workarounds will be a loss if we don't name them."*
- *"Pay-for-performance, network access fees, GER reconciliation timing — these are the post-DIR mechanics. They achieve the same outcome with new vocabulary."*

**Prescriptive ending:** CMS sub-regulatory guidance extending DIR rule intent to functional equivalents. FTC investigation into commercial-market analogs. Commercial-market disclosure parity legislation.

**Cascade-week PBS angle:** "How Post-DIR-Rule Reconciliation Should Show Up on Your Q-Reports."

The exclusive specialty arrangement clauses. The performance-guarantee clauses that protect retail margin but not specialty margin. Proprietary anchor.

## Piece 10: Manufacturer Patient Assistance Diversion

**Thesis:** Patient assistance programs are being diverted from member benefit to PBM revenue through accumulator/maximizer mechanics (Piece 4 territory) and through specialty pharmacy assignment requirements (Piece 9 territory). Proprietary anchor.

## Piece 12: The Audit Rights Gap

**Working title:** *The Checking Account You Have to Schedule Permission to Look Inside — The Audit Rights Gap in PBM Contracts*

**Opening metaphor:**
> *Imagine a checking account where you have the right to view your balance, but only on the bank's terms, only within a 30-day window each year, only for the prior 90 days of transactions, only if you give 90 days' notice, and only if you pay the bank's audit firm to look. That is the audit-rights regime in most PBM contracts.*

**Thesis:** Plan sponsor audit rights in PBM contracts are technically present but operationally hollow. The contract names the right; the operational restrictions hollow it out. Reform path: federal audit-rights floor via ERISA amendment.

**Named villains:** Generic — every Big 3 PBM contract uses similar audit-rights restrictions. The pattern is industry-wide, not company-specific.

**Section beats (~850 words):**

1. **Cold open with the checking-account metaphor** (~120 words).
2. **What audit rights look like in most PBM contracts** (~200 words). Proprietary anchor: "the audit right is in the contract from day one. It usually allows the plan or its designee to audit claims, rebate calculations, and guarantee performance. The window to exercise is typically annual, sometimes shorter. The PBM never reminds you it exists." The standard language pattern.
3. **The operational hollow** (~200 words). The windows that are too short. The designees that are restricted to PBM-approved firms. The data extracts that come in PBM-defined formats. The 90-day notice requirements. The fee structures that price out small/mid plans. Each restriction is documented in actual contracts; each is a structural barrier.
4. **What strong audit rights look like** (~150 words). The strongest contracts Ginny has seen: 18-month rolling windows, plan-selected designees, standardized data extracts, no fee structures, no notice requirements. These contracts exist; they are the exception.
5. **The prescription** (~150 words). Federal audit-rights floor via ERISA amendment establishing: minimum 18-month rolling audit windows, plan-selected designees, standardized data extract requirements, prohibition on fee structures that price out plans by size. State insurance commissioner authority for non-ERISA plans.
6. **Opinion-stated-openly closing** (~80 words). "In my view the audit-rights floor is the most leveraged structural reform on the table. Strong audit rights produce findings; findings produce remediation; remediation produces measurable employer benefit. The fix is administrative; the political cost is low."

**Pull-quote candidates:**

- *"Plan sponsor audit rights in PBM contracts are technically present but operationally hollow."*
- *"The audit right is in the contract from day one. It usually allows the plan or its designee to audit claims, rebate calculations, and guarantee performance. The window to exercise is typically annual, sometimes shorter. The PBM never reminds you it exists."*
- *"When we audit a plan that never audited, we find findings. Every time. The audit-rights floor is the cleanest structural reform on the table."*

**Prescriptive ending:** Federal audit-rights floor via ERISA amendment. Minimum 18-month rolling windows. Plan-selected designees. Standardized data extracts. State insurance commissioner authority for non-ERISA plans.

**Cascade-week PBS angle:** Pairs to W23 Library 03 Audit Rights content already on calendar. Wendell piece elevates the macro reform; W23 Library 03 carousel operationalizes the contract-language audit.

### Rhode Island AG Pushes to Ban Spread Pricing Before His Term Ends

*Rhode Island Current | May 11, 2026*

Rhode Island Attorney General Peter Neronha is putting his last six months in office behind a quartet of healthcare bills, two of which target pharmacy benefit managers directly. The first, H8360 / S3260, would amend Rhode Island's deceptive trade practices law to define a list of PBM behaviors as unfair, including spread pricing — the practice of charging a plan one price for a drug while paying the pharmacy a different, lower price and keeping the difference. The second, H8382 / S3258, builds on a 2025 law Neronha used earlier this year against unannounced PBM audits of independent pharmacies; the new version requires written justification for any audit that bypasses the PBM's annual quota, bans common-owner audits, requires advance notice to the AG, and authorizes civil penalties of up to $10,000 per violation.

The detail worth flagging for plan sponsors is on the record at the House Committee on Corporations hearing on April 14. The Pharmaceutical Care Management Association — the trade association representing the major PBMs — submitted testimony defending spread pricing on the grounds that "many small and mid-sized employers select spread pricing because it gives them predictable, stable drug costs." The argument is that the PBM is "transferring risk" by holding the spread. When PBS reviews PBM contracts and the spread pricing language is in the definitions section, the actual mechanic is that the PBM charges the plan $80 to $100 for a drug it pays the pharmacy $15 to $25 for, and keeps the difference. That is not risk transfer in any direction a CFO would recognize. Watch the Rhode Island legislative session through June for movement on H8360 specifically; even if it does not pass this cycle, the on-record PCMA defense is the cleanest articulation of the industry's position you will find this year. Read it; bring it up at renewal.

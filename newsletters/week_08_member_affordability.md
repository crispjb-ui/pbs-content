## Copay Accumulators vs. Point-of-Sale Rebates: The Member Affordability Decision

**Publication:** Benefit Blind Spots
**Publish:** Monday, February 23, 2026 at 7:30 AM EST
**Length:** ~1,500 words (~6 min read)
**Tone:** Analytical authority
**Pillar:** Cost Containment

---

### Article Body

There is a difference between cost shifting and cost management. I bring this up in nearly every plan sponsor conversation we have, because the distinction gets blurred constantly in pharmacy benefits.

Cost shifting moves financial burden from one party to another without reducing total spend. Cost management reduces the underlying expense.

Copay accumulator programs and point-of-sale rebates represent opposite approaches to the same problem: how to handle manufacturer rebates and member out-of-pocket costs. Most plan design decisions conflate the two.

For self-funded employers evaluating member affordability strategies, understanding this distinction is not optional.

---

**What Copay Accumulators Actually Do**

Copay accumulators emerged as a response to manufacturer copay assistance cards. When drug makers offer coupons that reduce a member's out-of-pocket cost for brand medications, those payments traditionally counted toward the member's deductible and out-of-pocket maximum.

This created a problem for plan sponsors: manufacturer assistance was accelerating members toward their out-of-pocket limits, after which the plan paid 100% of costs. Members had financial incentive to choose expensive brands over generics since the manufacturer covered their share.

Copay accumulator programs change this. When a manufacturer coupon pays for a prescription, that amount no longer counts toward the member's deductible or out-of-pocket maximum. Only what the member actually pays from their own pocket applies.

The logic is straightforward: prevent manufacturer subsidies from triggering earlier plan liability.

The adoption has been significant. Approximately 60% of large employers now use some form of copay accumulator or maximizer program according to industry surveys. PBM default settings increasingly include accumulator provisions unless employers specifically opt out.

---

**The Member Impact Problem**

From the member perspective, accumulators create a mid-year surprise.

Consider a member taking a specialty medication with a $15,000 annual cost. The manufacturer offers a copay card covering up to $12,000 annually. Under traditional benefit design, the member might meet their $3,000 out-of-pocket maximum within months, after which their medication becomes free.

With an accumulator in place, that same member's $12,000 manufacturer payment counts for nothing toward their limit. When the copay card exhausts around month eight, they suddenly owe thousands in out-of-pocket costs they did not anticipate.

(Illustrative example for educational purposes. Actual amounts vary by plan.)

This is not a rare scenario. Specialty drug utilization continues to grow. Manufacturer copay assistance programs exist precisely because list prices exceed what most members can afford.

The clinical concern is equally significant. Members facing unexpected costs may discontinue therapy. Adherence problems in specialty categories (oncology, autoimmune conditions, HIV) carry real health consequences.

State legislatures have noticed. At least 19 states plus Puerto Rico and the District of Columbia have enacted laws restricting accumulator programs, though these laws apply primarily to state-regulated plans. Self-funded ERISA plans operate under federal jurisdiction and are not bound by most state insurance mandates.

---

**The Point-of-Sale Rebate Alternative**

Point-of-sale rebates take the opposite approach.

Instead of collecting manufacturer rebates centrally and returning them to the employer, POS rebate programs apply rebate value directly at the pharmacy counter when the member fills a prescription. The member pays a lower cost immediately.

The mechanics work through PBM agreements that estimate rebate value for each transaction and reduce member cost-sharing in real time. Actual rebate collection still occurs after the fact, but the member benefit is immediate.

From the member perspective, this is transparency in action. The rebate, which ultimately derives from the high list price of the medication, flows to the person paying at the counter rather than disappearing into plan financials.

Adoption has grown modestly. Approximately 15-20% of employers have implemented POS rebate programs according to industry surveys, up from single digits five years ago. Several states now require or encourage POS rebate passthrough for state employee plans and Medicaid managed care.

---

**The Trade-offs Are Real**

Neither approach is cost-neutral.

Copay accumulators preserve employer rebate revenue and discourage brand preference when generics are available. They shift costs that manufacturers would otherwise subsidize back to members, which may feel appropriate philosophically but creates real financial hardship cases.

The downsides include member experience degradation, potential adherence impacts, communication complexity, and reputational risk as employee awareness grows.

Point-of-sale rebates align employer and member interests around affordability. Members see lower costs. Employers can position themselves as prioritizing member financial wellbeing.

The downsides are financial. When rebates flow to member cost-sharing instead of plan offsets, the employer loses that revenue stream. Depending on rebate magnitude (specialty medications can carry rebates of 30% or more), this represents real money.

There is also a utilization question. Lower member costs may increase brand medication usage versus generics. The evidence on this is mixed, but it remains a concern.

---

**A Framework for Deciding**

The choice between accumulator programs and POS rebates depends on plan philosophy more than financial optimization.

*Question 1: What is your position on member cost exposure?*

Some employers believe members should bear meaningful cost-sharing as a check on utilization. Others prioritize affordability and view high out-of-pocket costs as barriers to appropriate care. Your answer shapes which approach fits.

*Question 2: How significant is manufacturer copay assistance in your population?*

If your member base uses few specialty medications with copay assistance programs, accumulators have limited impact either way. If specialty utilization is significant (and it is growing across most employer populations), the financial stakes increase.

*Question 3: What are your rebate contract terms?*

POS rebate programs require specific PBM contract structures. Not all agreements support real-time rebate application. Implementation may require contract amendments or RFP for new arrangements.

*Question 4: What is your communication capacity?*

Accumulator programs require proactive member education to prevent the mid-year surprise. POS programs are simpler to explain but still require messaging. Assess whether your benefits communication infrastructure supports either approach.

---

**Contract Language Considerations**

For employers evaluating changes, PBM contract language deserves careful review.

*Rebate passthrough provisions:* Understand your current rebate structure. What percentage flows to the plan? What is retained by the PBM? How are rebates reported and audited?

*Accumulator opt-out:* If your PBM has accumulators as a default, confirm you have contractual ability to opt out without penalty. Some agreements make this difficult or tie it to other concessions.

*POS rebate mechanics:* If considering POS implementation, confirm the PBM can operationally support real-time rebate application. What is the reconciliation process? What happens when estimated rebates differ from actual collections?

*Data transparency:* Whatever approach you choose, ensure contract language provides data access to track impact. You need visibility into manufacturer assistance utilization, member cost-sharing patterns, and rebate flows.

---

**The Position We Take**

I advise employers to approach this as a values decision first and a financial decision second. Your broker or consultant can help you model the financial impact of either approach for your specific population.

If your benefit philosophy prioritizes member affordability and you are willing to accept reduced rebate capture as the cost of that priority, point-of-sale rebates align with your position.

If your benefit philosophy emphasizes member cost accountability and you view manufacturer copay assistance as distorting utilization incentives, accumulator programs have a defensible rationale, though member communication becomes essential.

What I discourage is the default position: implementing accumulators because the PBM made it automatic, without conscious decision about member impact. That produces the worst outcome: members harmed by a policy the employer did not deliberately choose.

The difference between cost shifting and cost management matters. Whichever direction you go, go there intentionally.

### Newsletter Body

Two mechanisms. Opposite philosophies. The same underlying question: who benefits when rebates flow through pharmacy claims?

Self-funded employers face a fundamental choice in pharmacy benefit design. Copay accumulator programs and point-of-sale rebates represent competing answers to member affordability, and most employers have one in place by default without deliberate decision.

**Copay Accumulators - The 30-Second Version**

When manufacturers offer copay assistance cards, accumulator programs prevent those payments from counting toward member deductibles or out-of-pocket maximums.

Why employers adopted them: manufacturer assistance was accelerating members to their limits, after which the plan paid 100%. Accumulators preserve rebate revenue and discourage brand preference.

The problem: members face mid-year surprises when copay cards exhaust and they suddenly owe thousands. At least 19 states have restricted these programs, though those laws do not apply to self-funded ERISA plans.

Adoption: approximately 60% of large employers per industry surveys.

**Point-of-Sale Rebates - The 30-Second Version**

POS programs apply rebate value directly at the pharmacy counter. Members pay lower costs immediately rather than rebates flowing to plan financials.

Why it's gaining attention: transparency and member affordability alignment. The rebate, deriving from high list prices, flows to the person paying.

The trade-off: employers lose rebate revenue that would otherwise offset plan costs. Depending on specialty utilization, this can be significant.

Adoption: approximately 15-20% of employers per industry surveys.

3. What do your current PBM rebate terms allow?
4. Can your benefits communication support member education on either approach?

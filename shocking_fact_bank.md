# Shocking Fact / Figure Bank — the "genuinely-unknown structural fact" lever

**Created:** June 2026. **Companion to** `x_recipe_post_bank.md` (the named-program · hidden-extraction recipe) and the CLAUDE.md "Best-kept secret" pattern.

**What this is:** the highest-reach LinkedIn/X hook shape PBS has, codified. Not "here's how spread pricing works" (a decoder), but a **specific, verifiable, jaw-drop fact the plan-sponsor audience genuinely did not know**, delivered as a shock. The proof point: the offshore rebate-aggregator reveal hit **17,816 impressions / 40 comments / 33 saves / 18 followers, fully organic, no Cuban**, in the summer-2026 slowdown, roughly 10x the same week's informational posts. A Cuban repost (+60-75%) would project it toward 28-31K.

**How to use it:** weld one of these facts onto a Thursday LinkedIn breakout attempt (or an AM X amplifier slot, or a Substack opener). It carries the reach; the PBS **proprietary anchor** ("we audit hundreds of these contracts a year; this is in most of them") carries the credibility and the Humanize Check. One shocking fact + one proprietary anchor = the formula.

## Three hard rules (read before posting)

1. **Every figure is cited. No fabrication (CLAUDE.md).** The source is listed on every entry. The figures here are point-in-time (mostly FTC interim reports 2024-2025 + Congressional letters). **Re-verify the number at post time** and cite the body, not the dollar figure alone ("The FTC found…", "Congress's investigation showed…"). The citation IS part of why it lands: a named federal source is unfakeable.
2. **A fact only shocks until it's common knowledge.** Spread pricing and DIR fees were once breakout facts; this audience now knows them, so they no longer shock (they're decoders now, not reveals). Rotate to the next genuinely-unknown one. When an entry below stops outperforming, retire it to the recipe bank as a standard decoder and promote a pipeline candidate.
3. **Pair the fact with a PBS anchor, never naked.** The fact is the hook; "every contract we review has the clause that authorizes this" is the reason a plan sponsor calls. Without the anchor it's a news repost; with it, it's lead-gen.

## The bank

### 1 · Offshore rebate GPOs _(PROVEN BREAKOUT — the model)_
**Fact:** The big-3 PBMs' rebate-negotiating GPOs are registered offshore. Ascent Health Services (Express Scripts / Cigna, Switzerland, 2019), Emisar Pharma Services (OptumRx / UnitedHealth, Ireland, 2021), Zinc Health Services (CVS Caremark, 2020). Manufacturers paid an estimated **$7.6 billion in fees to these aggregators in 2022 alone**. Two of the three sit in Europe, beyond US regulatory reach.
**Source:** FTC PBM investigation; House Oversight Committee letters; Drug Channels; Frier Levitt; healthcareuncovered (Potter).
**Unfakeable specifics:** the entity names (Ascent, Emisar, Zinc) and the countries (Switzerland, Ireland).
**LinkedIn add-on:** "Your PBM passes through 100% of manufacturer rebates. The rebate aggregator it owns, registered in Switzerland, does not have to."
**X add-on:** "The company that actually collects your PBM's drug rebates is registered in Switzerland. Another's in Ireland. Both owned by your PBM. 'Pass-through' stops at the water's edge."
**Fits:** Rebate Economics (W29), Transparency, PBM Contract Insights. **Freshness:** still the strongest; rotate jurisdictions/entities to keep it fresh.

### 2 · Specialty generic markups over 1,000% _(STRONGEST new figure)_
**Fact:** At their own affiliated pharmacies, the big-3 PBMs marked up **nearly two-thirds of specialty generic drugs by more than 100%, and more than one in five by over 1,000%**. Those markups generated **$7.3 billion above the drugs' acquisition cost (2017-2022)**, plus an additional **$1.4 billion from spread pricing**.
**Source:** FTC Second Interim Staff Report, Jan 2025.
**Unfakeable specifics:** "1 in 5 over 1,000%," "$7.3B," and that it's **generics**, not brand drugs.
**LinkedIn add-on:** "The FTC studied the generic drugs your PBM marks up most. More than one in five specialty generics: marked up over 1,000 percent. Not brand drugs. Generics. At the pharmacy your PBM owns."
**X add-on:** "FTC, on the record: big-3 PBMs marked up 1 in 5 specialty generics by 1,000%+ at the pharmacies they own. $7.3B above what the drugs cost them. Generics."
**Fits:** Specialty Management (W34), Cost Containment, Clinical Pharmacy. **Freshness:** high, underused.

### 3 · PBMs pay their own pharmacies up to 2x more
**Fact:** The big-3 reimbursed their **own** pharmacies as much as **100% more than independent pharmacies for the identical specialty generic**, and the drugs marked up more than **$1,000/script** were disproportionately routed to PBM-owned pharmacies.
**Source:** FTC Second Interim Staff Report, Jan 2025.
**Unfakeable specifics:** "up to 100% more for the same drug," "the >$1,000 markups went to their own pharmacies."
**LinkedIn add-on:** "Same drug. Your PBM pays its own pharmacy up to twice what it pays the independent down the street. Then its prior-auth rules make sure the script lands at the one it owns."
**X add-on:** "FTC: PBMs pay their own pharmacies up to 100% more than independents for the identical generic, then steer your members there. Not a network. A funnel with a price tag."
**Fits:** Network Design (W28), Site of Care (W30), Specialty. **Freshness:** high.

### 4 · Three companies, 80% of prescriptions, both sides of the counter
**Fact:** The big-3 PBMs process **~80% of the ~6.6 billion US prescriptions** (2023), up from **52% in 2004**; the top six process over 90%. PBM-affiliated pharmacies now account for **~70% of all specialty drug revenue**.
**Source:** FTC July 2024 interim report.
**Unfakeable specifics:** "80% up from 52% in 2004," "70% of specialty revenue."
**LinkedIn add-on:** "Three companies decide what 80 percent of American prescriptions cost. The same three own the pharmacies that collect 70 percent of every specialty dollar. Both sides of the counter."
**X add-on:** "3 PBMs = 80% of US prescriptions, up from 52% in 2004. Same 3 own the pharmacies filling 70% of specialty spend. Picture 3 banks that also owned 80% of the paychecks."
**Fits:** Transparency, PBM Evaluation (W27), PBM RFP (W31). **Freshness:** medium (concentration is becoming known; the "52% in 2004" growth framing keeps it fresh).

### 5 · The vendor that reclassifies your drug as "non-essential"
**Fact:** Copay maximizer vendors reclassify a member's specialty drug as a **"non-essential health benefit"** to bypass the ACA's out-of-pocket cap, then drain the manufacturer's full annual copay assistance. In J&J's suit against SaveOnSP, Stelara copay assistance ran **~$1,200/script for non-enrolled patients vs. ~$4,300/script for enrolled** ones; J&J alleged **$100M+ in overpayment**. This is not fringe: **nearly 40% of commercially insured members were in plans with copay maximizers in 2025.**
**Source:** Johnson & Johnson v. SaveOnSP; KFF; Frier Levitt; AIMED Alliance. Prevalence figure: Drug Channels news roundup, mid-April 2026 _(added Jul 1, 2026 quarterly research)_.
**Unfakeable specifics:** the "non-essential health benefit" reclassification trick; "$1,200 → $4,300 on Stelara."
**LinkedIn add-on:** "There is a program that legally reclassifies your employee's drug as 'non-essential,' so the copay cap disappears and a vendor drains the full year of manufacturer assistance. It may be bolted onto your plan right now."
**X add-on:** "A vendor reclassifies your member's drug as a 'non-essential health benefit' to dodge the ACA out-of-pocket cap, then drains the manufacturer's copay card. Stelara: $1,200/script became $4,300. It has a name."
**Fits:** Clinical Program ROI (W32), Member Communication (W33), Copay programs. **Freshness:** high, very underused.

### 6 · The "preferred" formulary that excludes 600+ drugs
**Fact:** Each big-3 PBM now **excludes more than 600 drugs** from its main formulary (still true for the 2026 formularies, an all-time high), increasingly replacing them with the PBM's **own private-label** products (all three dropped AbbVie's Humira in favor of private-label biosimilars).
**Source:** Drug Channels (Adam Fein), "The Big Three PBMs' 2026 Formulary Exclusions" (Jan 2026); Becker's; PHSL exclusion reviews. _(Re-verified Jul 1, 2026: 600+ per big-3 holds for 2026; Express Scripts expanded its list.)_
**Unfakeable specifics:** "600+ exclusions, all-time high," "private-label biosimilar swap."
**LinkedIn add-on:** "Your PBM's 'preferred' formulary now excludes more than 600 drugs. The replacements increasingly carry the PBM's own private label. That list isn't clinical. It's a storefront."
**X add-on:** "Each big-3 PBM now excludes 600+ drugs from its formulary, an all-time high. They drop Humira and push their own private-label biosimilar. The formulary is a shelf they rent to themselves."
**Fits:** Formulary Management (W26), Biosimilars, Transparency. **Freshness:** high; refresh the count each plan year.

### 7 · The gap above NADAC growing 42% a year
**Fact:** There is a public benchmark for what pharmacies actually pay for a generic (NADAC). PBM-affiliated pharmacy dispensing revenue **in excess of NADAC grew at a 42% compound annual rate (2017-2021)**.
**Source:** FTC Second Interim Staff Report, Jan 2025.
**Unfakeable specifics:** "NADAC is public," "42% CAGR above it."
**LinkedIn add-on:** "There is a public price for what a generic costs a pharmacy: NADAC. The gap between it and what your PBM bills through its own pharmacy grew 42 percent a year. Compounding."
**X add-on:** "NADAC = the public price generics cost pharmacies. The amount PBMs bill above it, through pharmacies they own, grew 42%/yr. Ask to see both numbers on one claim."
**Fits:** Mid-Year Claims (W23), MAC/pricing, Cost Containment. **Freshness:** high, technical but powerful with the "public benchmark you've never been shown" framing.

### 8 · The markups hit cancer, HIV, and transplant patients
**Fact:** The specialty generics the big-3 marked up most include generic versions of **Gleevec (leukemia), Ampyra (MS), Myfortic (transplant), Sensipar (renal)**, and HIV drugs. The 51 drugs studied spanned 882 NDCs. The biggest markups landed on the sickest patients.
**Source:** FTC Second Interim Staff Report, Jan 2025; Pharmacy Times.
**Unfakeable specifics:** the named conditions (leukemia, MS, transplant, HIV) and drug names.
**LinkedIn add-on:** "The generic drugs your PBM marks up the most are not lifestyle pills. They are the generics for leukemia, MS, transplant, and HIV. The markup scales with how sick your member is."
**X add-on:** "The FTC found the biggest PBM generic markups were on leukemia, MS, transplant, and HIV generics. The sicker the member, the bigger the spread."
**Fits:** Clinical Pharmacy, Specialty Management (W34), Member Communication (W33). **Freshness:** high; the human angle broadens reach beyond the finance audience.

### 9 · The profit that disappears into a sibling company _(NEW — Jul 1, 2026 quarterly research)_
**Fact:** Insurers are required to spend 80-85% of premium on care (the Medical Loss Ratio). Vertically integrated insurer-PBMs get around it with **intercompany eliminations**: the regulated insurance arm **overpays its affiliated providers/pharmacies and keeps rebates inside the parent**, so profit shifts *out of* the MLR-regulated entity and *into* the unregulated sibling companies the rule can't see. The money doesn't get spent on care and it doesn't get refunded, it just moves to another pocket of the same corporation.
**Source:** Drug Channels news roundup, mid-April 2026 (citing a Milliman analysis commissioned by PhRMA). Re-verify at post time.
**Unfakeable specifics:** the term "intercompany eliminations," the 80-85% MLR rule, "overpay the affiliate + retain rebates in the parent."
**LinkedIn add-on:** "There's a rule that says your insurer has to spend 80 cents of every premium dollar on care. Vertically integrated PBM-insurers get around it by overpaying the pharmacy they own and keeping the rebates upstairs. The money doesn't get spent on care. It moves to a sibling company the rule can't see."
**X add-on:** "The MLR rule forces insurers to spend 80% of premiums on care. The workaround: overpay the pharmacy you own, keep the rebates in the parent, book the profit in a sibling the regulator can't touch. It has a name. Intercompany eliminations."
**Fits:** Transparency, PBM Contract Insights, PBM Evaluation, Rebate Economics. **Freshness:** high, genuinely under-known — this is the "hidden structure" reveal shape. Do not run in the same ~6-week window as the offshore-aggregator (1) or specialty-markup (2) reveals.

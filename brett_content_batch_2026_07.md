```
For eight years I helped run pharmacies inside a health system. Four outpatient pharmacies, a specialty pharmacy, a $94 million retail operation.

Here is the strange part. Our own employee health plan was one of our customers. My colleagues filled the prescriptions. And I still could not have told you what the plan actually paid for them once the claims left our counter.

The dispensing side and the paying side of the same institution, one floor apart, working from two different sets of numbers. Neither set complete.

If you run pharmacy at a health system, you know exactly what I mean. And here is the part worth sitting with: your institution is also a self-funded employer. You are living this system from both sides at once, which means you understand it better than almost anyone advising your CFO.

I write from that intersection now. More soon.
```

```
Nobody subscribed for content. They subscribed because a specific Monday briefing answered a question they already had, usually about a contract sitting on their desk.

Marketing people call this product-market fit. Pharmacists call it counseling: nobody remembers the leaflet, everybody remembers the answer to the question they actually asked.

The Pharmacy Benefits Briefing runs every Monday. Built by people who read PBM contracts for a living at Prescription Benefit Solutions. I just help it find the people with the questions.
```

## Post 4 · Sat Jul 25, 9:00 AM · B1 (the medication assistance loop)
_Interlock: W29 Thu maximizer reveal published Jul 23, two days before this; the adjacency is post-publish and this stays on the hospital MAP angle, not the maximizer mechanism. $17M figure verified from CV._

```
At UNC I oversaw a medication assistance program that recovered more than $17 million a year. Manufacturer assistance, foundation grants, charity programs, matched to patients who could not afford their medications.

Health systems run these programs at industrial scale. There are whole teams whose job is knowing which manufacturer will cover which drug for which patient.

Now the part I did not appreciate until I moved to the benefits side. The employers paying for coverage rarely see any of this machinery. Assistance dollars move through the system in ways that never show up on a plan's claims report, even when the same dollars are shaping what the plan pays.

Two sophisticated parties, the health system and the employer, each seeing half the money.

If you work on either side of that split, I would genuinely like to hear how it looks from your seat.
```

_Interlock: weekend, no collision. Toolkit count ~29 verified._

```
The consulting firm I work for gives away 29 printable worksheets. Contract review checklists, audit frameworks, renewal scorecards. Free, at rxbs.org. No payment gate.

People keep asking why. Here is the honest answer.

A plan sponsor who works through a channel-pricing worksheet does not conclude they can skip the experts. They conclude the opposite: they see exactly how deep the water is, two pages in. The worksheet builds the vocabulary; the vocabulary makes the eventual conversation useful.

And a broker who hands a client one of our checklists looks sharper for it. That matters to us. Brokers are the allies in this work, the ones who flag the gaps in the first place.

I spent my pharmacy career watching trust get built one honest answer at a time, at the counter. Turns out firm-building works the same way.
```

## Post 7 · Mon Aug 3, 10:30 AM · B1 (ask your pharmacy director)
_Interlock: W31 Mon = PBM RFP, Thu = market concentration. Neither. This one CAN mention RFP season obliquely but stays off the question-bank subject._

```
An underused resource for any CFO trying to understand pharmacy benefits: your own health system's pharmacy director. And if you are not in healthcare, the pharmacy leaders in your network.

These are people who live with drug pricing every day. They know what a medication actually costs to acquire, what the aggregators do, what happens between the list price and the counter. They have sat across from the same vendors your plan negotiates with, wearing a different hat.

For years I was one of them, and here is the thing: almost nobody from the benefits side ever asked us anything.

The finance office and the pharmacy department of the same institution can go years without comparing notes on the drug supply chain they are both paying into.

If you own a benefits budget, find your pharmacist. Buy them coffee. Ask what surprised them most about drug pricing. I promise the hour pays for itself.
```

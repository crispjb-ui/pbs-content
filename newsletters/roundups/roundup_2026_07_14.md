### Employers Keep Tightening GLP-1 Coverage, and CVS Pulled Zepbound

*GoodRx / CNBC | July 2026*

CVS Caremark removed Zepbound from its main national formulary effective July 1, and the broader picture is that more large employers are narrowing GLP-1 access this year, some raising the qualifying BMI threshold, some dropping coverage altogether. The number of people with no commercial coverage for Zepbound climbed sharply into the summer.

A member on that formulary lost the specific drug their prescriber chose, and the plan sponsor was not the one who made that call. The PBM was. If GLP-1 spend is one of your top pressure points, the question for your broker is not "should we cover it," it is "who controls the formulary that decides which one we cover, and do we have any say when it changes mid-year."

---

### CMS Opens the Door on How PBMs Get Paid

*Federal Register / CMS | Comments due July 18, 2026*

CMS published a request for information on pharmacy benefit manager compensation and data collection, asking the industry how to define terms like "PBM affiliate" and "bona fide service fee" under the Consolidated Appropriations Act of 2026. The comment window closes this week. The rules themselves are aimed at Medicare Part D and take effect in 2028.

I am watching this one even though it is a Medicare rule, because the definitions the government settles on tend to ripple outward. "Bona fide service fee" is the phrase to track. It asks whether a PBM's compensation reflects an actual service performed at fair market value, or a spread the PBM named for itself. That is the exact question every self-funded employer should be asking about its own contract, Medicare or not.

---

### A Quiet Warning on 340B for Employers

*Drug Channels (Adam J. Fein, PhD) | July 8, 2026*

Adam Fein published a short piece arguing that employers and health plans have quietly become major participants in the 340B program's economics, often without realizing it, through the retrospective identification of 340B-eligible claims. The 340B debate usually centers on hospitals and manufacturers, not plan sponsors.

We see the footprint of this in claims data more often than employers expect. The action step is simple and worth taking before renewal: ask your PBM and your broker to walk you through how 340B contract-pharmacy arrangements touch your own claims, and where the resulting margin lands. If nobody can answer cleanly, that is the finding.

---

### First Interchangeable Rituximab Biosimilar

*Celltrion / FDA | July 1, 2026*

The FDA granted interchangeability status to Truxima, a biosimilar to Rituxan, making it the first rituximab biosimilar to earn that designation. Interchangeability lets a pharmacist substitute the biosimilar without going back to the prescriber in many states.

Rituximab is a real specialty line item for a lot of plans. Interchangeability tends to speed biosimilar uptake, which is good for your specialty spend, but only if your formulary and your specialty channel are actually steering toward it. Worth confirming that they are.

---

### One More Worth Watching

Only about 35% of commercial plan sponsors run a site-of-care program for specialty drugs, according to Pharmaceutical Strategies Group data flagged in Drug Channels this week, even though Medicare adopted site-neutral payment more than a decade ago. Provider-administered drugs are one of the fastest-growing and least-managed costs employers carry. Monday's deep dive on network design went deeper on exactly this.

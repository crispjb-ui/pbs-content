# PART 1: SUBSTACK ARTICLE

## Where PBM Profit Hides in Your Plan

**Publication:** Benefit Blind Spots
**Publish:** Monday, March 16, 2026 at 7:30 AM EST
**Length:** ~1,600 words (~6 min read)
**Tone:** Analytical authority
**Pillar:** Transparency & Industry Education

---

### Article Body

Your PBM contract defines what you pay. It does not always define what your PBM earns.

I sat down with a plan sponsor last quarter who thought their PBM relationship was straightforward: admin fees, dispensing fees, stated margins. That covered the disclosed category. The undisclosed category is where the real money often lives. And for self-funded employers, understanding that gap is the difference between managing pharmacy costs and subsidizing PBM profit margins you never agreed to.

This issue maps the revenue streams that exist outside your contract's pricing guarantees, and the questions that expose them.

---

**The Revenue Streams Nobody Negotiates**

*Spread Pricing*

This is the most discussed, and in my experience one of the least understood, hidden revenue stream.

Here is how it works: your PBM charges your plan one price for a prescription and pays the pharmacy a different, lower price. The PBM keeps the difference.

Your contract may guarantee you a discount off AWP or a MAC price. The PBM honors that guarantee. But the amount they pay the pharmacy is substantially lower than what they charge you. The guarantee is met. The spread still exists.

Spread pricing is not illegal. It is not always disclosed. And when it is disclosed, the magnitude is rarely transparent.

Here is the challenge for plan sponsors: your claims data shows what you paid. It does not show what the pharmacy received. Without that comparison, spread pricing is invisible.

*Rebate Retention*

Your contract specifies a rebate passthrough, often framed as a percentage or per-script guarantee. But rebates flow through a complex chain: manufacturer to PBM to plan sponsor.

We tell employers to ask these questions:
- Is your passthrough based on total rebates received or a subset?
- Are admin fees deducted before passthrough calculation?
- Which drug categories are excluded from the rebate guarantee?
- Does the PBM aggregate rebates across clients before allocating?

The structure of the rebate guarantee matters more than the percentage. A 100% passthrough with exclusions and deductions can deliver less than a 90% passthrough with clean definitions.

*Manufacturer Revenue*

PBMs receive revenue from pharmaceutical manufacturers beyond traditional rebates:
- Formulary placement fees (pay-to-play for preferred tier)
- Data licensing fees (selling aggregated claims data)
- Clinical program sponsorship (manufacturer-funded adherence programs)
- Administrative fees tied to volume commitments

These revenue streams influence which drugs appear on your formulary and at what tier. As a clinical pharmacist, this one frustrates me. Your plan's formulary may reflect PBM revenue optimization as much as clinical value.

*Pharmacy Network Revenue*

PBMs that own mail-order or specialty pharmacies create internal revenue through vertical integration:
- Your plan pays the PBM price for mail-order prescriptions
- The PBM fills them through its own pharmacy at internal cost
- The margin between those numbers flows to the PBM's pharmacy division

When your PBM recommends mail order, the recommendation serves their revenue model. It may also serve your plan. But the incentive alignment is worth examining.

*Retroactive Pricing Adjustments*

PBMs apply post-sale reconciliations and pricing adjustments to claims after they are processed, sometimes weeks or months later. These adjustments can change pharmacy reimbursement, alter effective discount rates, and shift the true net cost of claims.

For plan sponsors, retroactive adjustments create a disconnect. The price at point-of-sale is not the final price. Whether those adjustments are visible in your reporting, and whether any recoveries flow back to your plan, depends on your contract language.

---

**Why Transparency Is Hard to Get**

PBMs resist transparency for a straightforward reason. Disclosure reduces revenue.

If plan sponsors could see the spread on every claim, they would demand narrower spreads. If rebate flows were fully transparent, retention strategies would be challenged. If formulary placement fees were disclosed, clinical justification would face scrutiny.

The contractual tools used to limit transparency:

*Proprietary Pricing:* PBMs classify pharmacy reimbursement rates as proprietary. You can see what you paid. You cannot see what the pharmacy received.

*Aggregated Reporting:* Rebates reported in aggregate rather than by drug, obscuring which products generate the most PBM revenue.

*Broad Confidentiality Clauses:* Contract language that restricts sharing pricing information with consultants or during RFP processes.

*Defined Terms:* "Rebates" defined narrowly to exclude manufacturer payments that function like rebates but carry different labels: fees, incentives, administrative payments.

---

**How to Find It in Your Plan**

You do not need a full audit to start identifying potential hidden revenue. We walk employers through these approaches:

*Claims-Level Analysis*

Pull your claims data and compare:
- AWP discount achieved vs. contract guarantee (how much cushion exists?)
- MAC pricing vs. publicly available pharmacy acquisition cost benchmarks
- Brand vs. generic mix by therapeutic class. Does it align with clinical best practice, or does it look more like PBM rebate optimization?

*Rebate Reconciliation*

Request from your PBM:
- Total rebates received from manufacturers for your plan's claims
- Total rebates passed through to your plan
- The difference, itemized by drug category
- Any deductions applied before passthrough calculation

If your PBM will not provide this breakdown, that tells you something.

*Network Comparison*

For mail-order prescriptions:
- What would the same prescriptions cost at retail?
- What is the actual acquisition cost at the PBM-owned pharmacy?
- Is the "savings" calculation based on retail AWP (inflated) or actual retail network price?

*Formulary Analysis*

Compare your formulary against:
- Clinical practice guidelines
- Independent formulary recommendations
- Whether preferred products correlate with highest-rebate products

A formulary optimized for your plan looks different from a formulary optimized for PBM revenue.

---

**Contract Provisions That Create Transparency**

For your next renewal or RFP, these provisions force disclosure:

*Passthrough Pricing:* The PBM charges you exactly what they pay the pharmacy, plus a disclosed admin fee. This eliminates spread pricing entirely.

*Fiduciary Standard:* PBM acts in the best interest of the plan. Creates legal accountability beyond contract guarantees.

*Full Rebate Disclosure:* All manufacturer payments, regardless of label, passed through or disclosed. No exclusions, no deductions before calculation.

*Audit Access to Pharmacy Reimbursement:* Right to compare what you paid vs. what the pharmacy received. Claim-by-claim.

*Formulary Transparency:* Disclosure of manufacturer payments that influence formulary placement.

These provisions are available in the market today. Not every PBM will agree to all of them. And the ones that refuse are telling you something about their revenue model.

---

**The Position We Take**

I want to be clear: hidden revenue is not inherently wrong. PBMs provide real services that warrant compensation.

The problem is compensation without disclosure. When a PBM earns revenue from your plan's claims volume in ways your contract does not address, you are paying more than you agreed to. You just cannot see it.

We advise every employer we work with to assume hidden revenue exists unless proven otherwise. Not as cynicism, but as basic risk management.

The question is not whether your PBM earns undisclosed revenue. The question is how much, and whether you are comfortable with that number.

If you have never asked, now is the time. Q1 data is available. The answers are in the numbers, if you know where to look.

### "Traditional vs. Transparent" (Strong POV Text)

**Format:** Text Post (Strong POV)
**Pillar:** Transparency
**Word Count:** 108 words

#### Post Copy

"Traditional" PBM pricing is a polite way of saying "we keep the spread."

The industry divided itself into two camps: traditional and transparent. The names alone tell you which model benefits the PBM.

Traditional: PBM sets your price. PBM sets pharmacy price. PBM keeps the difference. Guarantees are met. Revenue is hidden.

Transparent: PBM charges what they pay, plus a disclosed fee. No spread. No guessing.

The transparent model is available. It is not new. It is not experimental.

So why do most employers still use traditional pricing?

Because nobody showed them the math.

Ask your PBM for both models side by side. Watch what happens.

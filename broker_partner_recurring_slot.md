# Broker-Partner Recurring Slot
## Converting Broker Sensitivity Into Broker Amplification

**Purpose:** Week 14 Stop-Loss newsletter was deleted after the primary broker felt the content implied inadequate contract review. Broker sensitivity is a durable risk. The fix is not softer content. The fix is content that positions brokers as the heroes who catch these gaps, then lets them re-share it into their own networks.

**Why this works:** Brokers control distribution to self-funded employers. A broker who feels flattered by a piece of content will share it with their book. A broker who feels criticized will object to it being published. The same underlying facts can produce either reaction depending on framing.

**Placement:** Recurring Monday newsletter slot, rotating every 4-6 weeks. Could start as early as Week 18 or Week 21, with cadence stabilizing once the format proves out.

---

## The recurring slot format

**Title pattern:** "What the Best Brokers Are Doing Right Now" / "What Good Brokers Ask Their PBMs" / "The Question Every Broker Should Raise at Renewal"

**Structural promise to the reader:**
- One or two specific tactics the strongest brokers we work with are deploying
- Exact language they use (in meetings, in RFPs, in contract redlines)
- The adversary remains the PBM, never the broker
- An implicit invitation for brokers to recognize themselves in the piece and share it

**Tone:** Positive, peer-to-peer, professional respect. No condescension about "most brokers miss this." Replace with "the best brokers are already on this, and here is what they do."

**Substack exclusive tool:** Each broker-partner installment ends with a tool specifically designed for broker use, not just employer use. Examples:
- A one-page RFP scoring rubric a broker can hand to a client
- A contract redline cheat sheet a broker can use during negotiations
- A client conversation script for raising a specific cost issue
- A renewal-season project plan a broker can adopt for their book

**CTA:** "If you are a broker or consultant, forward this to your team. If you are a plan sponsor, share it with your broker and ask what they think."

## First installment: "What the Best Brokers Are Doing Right Now for Renewal"

**Suggested placement:** Week 25 (Renewal Countdown) or new Week 31 (replacing PBM RFP per the cross-week audit).

**Opening (200 words):**

Most of what I write here is aimed at the plan sponsor. This one is different. This is for the brokers and consultants who do the harder work of taking our kind of analysis and translating it into renewal outcomes for their clients.

We review dozens of PBM contracts every quarter at PBS. The best brokers we work with have a specific set of moves they make in the 90 days before renewal. I am going to lay out three of them here, with the exact language they use, because these moves are what separates a reactive renewal from a negotiated one.

None of this is proprietary. The brokers doing it already know it works. The ones who have not added it to their workflow yet, this is your prompt. And plan sponsors reading this, if you see your broker doing any of these three things already, your renewal is in better hands than most.

**Three moves to document in the article:**

1. **The Independent Claims Analysis Request.** Strong brokers pull their own claims data before the PBM does their renewal presentation. They come to the renewal meeting with their own numbers. Exact data request they send to the PBM. Exact questions they ask about the numbers.

2. **The Term Sheet Before the Quote.** Strong brokers submit a negotiating term sheet to the PBM before the PBM submits renewal terms. They define what good looks like. The PBM responds to the broker's framework instead of the other way around. Sample term sheet headers.

3. **The Stop-Loss Coordination Meeting.** Strong brokers pull in the stop-loss carrier during renewal prep, not at renewal. They catch the pharmacy exposure gaps before they become a claim. Specific coordination checklist.

**Closing:**

If your broker is already doing all three of these, say thank you. If your broker is doing one or two, the third is a fair conversation to have. If your broker is not doing any of them, the best time to raise the question is right now, before renewal pressure compresses their timeline.

Brokers reading this: if you want a copy of the term sheet template we use with clients, reply to this email. We will send it. We are not trying to replace your workflow. We are trying to upgrade the tools you already have.

## Substack exclusive tool for the first installment

**One-page renewal term sheet template** that a broker can hand to a client and to the PBM. Includes:

- Pricing guarantee improvements (specific redline language)
- Rebate guarantee improvements (definition-tightening language)
- Audit rights expansion (scope, timing, recovery cap language)
- Reporting requirements (frequency, format, line-item detail)
- Biosimilar commitments (substitution, formulary placement)
- Clinical program guarantees (outcomes, ROI reporting)
- Termination provisions (notice periods, data portability)

This is the same template PBS uses in actual client engagements. Releasing it as a Substack exclusive is a calculated giveaway: it costs nothing, makes brokers look good when they use it, and creates reciprocity when the broker eventually needs deeper help (audit, dispute, complex renegotiation) that PBS does for a fee.

## Measurement

Track per installment:
- Newsletter impressions (baseline vs. broker-partner editions)
- Comments from named brokers or consultants
- Reposts (broker reposts are the signal we are after)
- Reply volume from brokers to any email in the series
- Substack subs gained (broker segment identified by title/role if available)
- PBS lead conversations sourced "via a broker who shared a newsletter" (track in CRM)

If after two installments the broker-partner editions underperform the standard editions by more than 30% on reach, adjust. The goal is broker amplification. If brokers are not amplifying, the format is not earning its slot.

---

## What this slot does NOT do

- It does not compete with what brokers do. The content is about what brokers SHOULD do, framed as recognition of what the best ones already do.
- It does not publish client-specific stories that any broker could read as "that was my client." Keep examples either composite or explicitly anonymized with context scrubbed.
- It does not position PBS as a replacement for brokers. PBS is a specialist the broker calls in when the engagement needs depth. That relationship is worth protecting.

---

## Broker sensitivity review checklist

Before any broker-partner installment (or any newsletter touching renewal / stop-loss / audit topics) publishes, run this checklist:

- [ ] Is any sentence readable as "your broker missed this" rather than "the PBM hid this"?
- [ ] Does the piece explicitly credit brokers at least twice?
- [ ] If a specific pattern is called out (e.g., weak audit rights language), is it attributed to the PBM's contract-drafting practice, not to broker oversight?
- [ ] Are any specific client examples scrubbed of details that would let a broker identify their own client?
- [ ] Does the Substack tool help brokers do their jobs better, not replace them?

If any answer is no, rewrite before publishing.

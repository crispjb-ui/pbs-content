Four stories this week and one worth watching. Two of them point the same direction: state reforms keep running into ERISA, and the dollars keep flowing to affiliated pharmacies unless your contract language does the work.

### Sixth Circuit Says ERISA Preempts Tennessee's Anti-Steering PBM Laws

*Mintz / NFP | April 15, 2026*

In McKee Foods Corp. v. BFP Inc., decided April 7 and drawing analysis this week, the Sixth Circuit affirmed that ERISA preempts two Tennessee laws designed to stop PBMs from steering members to affiliated pharmacies. The court distinguished the Tennessee statutes from the reimbursement rules upheld in Rutledge v. PCMA, holding that plan design and network steering sit in ERISA's protected zone.

The plaintiff is a self-funded Tennessee employer, which is the part worth sitting with. If you are counting on your state's PBM reform law to block affiliate steering, this ruling narrows that shield. The protection has to live in your contract: network composition rights, audit rights on specialty routing, and language that forces cost comparison when an affiliated pharmacy is the steered destination. We see this pattern in the contracts we review; the protective language is almost never in the base template.

---

### Lilly's Employer Connect Puts Zepbound on a Direct-to-Employer Rail

*Sequoia / Lilly | April 2026*

Eli Lilly launched Employer Connect, a platform that offers defined-price Zepbound access through participating third-party administrators. Employers can opt in through a TPA and bypass the traditional PBM rebate structure for the GLP-1 category, with pricing disclosed up front.

This is the first credible attempt to route a GLP-1 around the PBM rebate flow. If you are deciding whether to cover GLP-1s for weight management, the question just changed. Instead of "what does the PBM say the net cost is," it is "how does the Lilly direct price compare to our current GLP-1 spend net of rebates, and what share of that rebate do we actually see." Ask your broker to run both numbers before the next formulary review.

---

### HRSA's April 20 Deadline on the 340B Rebate Pilot Passes

*HRSA / HFMA | April 20, 2026*

HRSA's Request for Information on the 340B Rebate Model Pilot closed Monday. Under the pilot, eight approved manufacturers can provide rebates rather than upfront discounts to 340B providers for nine drugs tied to the first wave of Medicare negotiated prices.

The structural question for employer plans is straightforward: if 340B shifts from a discount at dispense to a rebate to the covered entity, commercial charges may rise while the covered entity captures the value on the back end. Plans do not get that rebate. The contract language that matters is how your PBM defines "340B claim" and whether claims review covers hospital-affiliated and contract pharmacy routing. I flagged this because we pull 340B claims in every contract review, and the flagging logic in most contracts is weaker than plan sponsors assume.

---

### FDA Moves to Treat Biosimilars More Like Generics

*FDA / HHS | April 2026*

FDA plans to finalize biosimilar and interchangeability guidance in the first half of 2026, aligning the pathway closer to the generics model. Analysts project annual biosimilar approvals moving from 8-10 to 15-20, with routine switching studies removed from the default approval package.

More approvals, faster. That sounds like a cost story, and it is, but only if your formulary actually moves. The 2026 formulary preferences for Humira and Stelara are already dominated by private-label biosimilars affiliated with the three largest PBMs. Ask your broker what your PBM's biosimilar preference logic looks like, and whether your plan is getting the lower-cost option or the PBM's own private label. Those are not always the same drug at the same net price.

---

### One More Worth Watching

Cigna's Evernorth is piloting a rebate-free pharmacy pricing model with a subset of employer clients. The pitch is administrative simplicity and fewer conflicts. The real test is whether it reduces total plan spend or just redistributes where the margin sits. If your broker has seen the pilot terms, ask what the guarantees look like on net pharmacy cost, not just on rebate flow.

---

Two of these stories, the Sixth Circuit ruling and the 340B rebate pilot, land on the same question: can state or federal rules force transparency the contract itself does not require. So far the answer keeps coming back the same way. If your plan wants protection, it has to be written in.

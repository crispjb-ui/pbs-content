## A · POLL BANK (7 PM interactive)

Format: question + 4 options (the throwaway option is the hook). Put the reveal/answer in the FIRST REPLY. X polls run 1-7 days; default 1 day so it closes same week.

**A1 · Generic spread**
```
Does your plan know its generic spread, the gap between what the PBM pays the pharmacy and what it bills you?

· We've measured it
· We think our broker checks
· Our PBM says there's no spread
· What's a spread?
```
First reply → `rxbs.org/toolkit/pbm-compensation`

**A2 · Rebate destination**
```
When your PBM says it passes through "100% of rebates," 100% of what?

· What the manufacturer paid
· What the PBM "received" after its aggregator's cut
· Not sure, never asked
· We don't get rebates?
```
First reply → `benefitblindspots.substack.com/p/pbm-contract-language-library`

**A3 · Specialty fill location**
```
Pull your specialty claims. What share filled at the PBM's OWN specialty pharmacy?

· We've never pulled that

**A4 · Audit rights**
```
Your PBM contract says you can audit it. In practice, who picks the auditor and the records?

· We've never tried to audit

**A5 · Reporting login**

## B · ONE QUESTION FOR YOUR PBM (8:30 PM close)

Format: one actionable question the plan sponsor can send. Plan-sponsor-actionable, repostable, evergreen.

**B1**
```
One question that changes the room: "Show me the difference between what you paid the pharmacy and what you billed us, by claim." Watch how long the answer takes.
```
**B2**
```
Ask your PBM: "What did you collect in rebates and fees from manufacturers on our plan last year, and how much reached us?" The gap between the two numbers is the conversation.
```
**B3**
```
Ask your PBM to define "pass-through" in writing, then ask which noun it attaches to: what the manufacturer paid, or what you received. The definition is the whole deal.
```
**B4**
```
Ask your PBM: "Which of our specialty claims filled at a pharmacy you own?" If the answer is most of them, that's not a network. It's a funnel.
```
**B5**
```
Ask your PBM for the audit clause and read who picks the auditor, who picks the records, and who decides if the findings count. Three answers tell you if you have an audit right or a tour.
```
**B6**
```
Ask your PBM: "List every fee on our monthly invoice and the contract section that authorizes each one." The line items with no clause behind them are the ones to cut.
```
**B7**
```
Ask your PBM: "When our covered lives drop, do our admin fees drop with them?" If there's no true-up clause, you're paying for members you no longer have.
```
**B8**
```
Ask your PBM: "Which formulary exclusions on our plan are tied to a rebate, not to clinical evidence?" The honest answer reorders your formulary.
```
**B9**
```
Ask your PBM for your generic effective rate against the contract guarantee, by month. "On average over the year" is where the misses hide.
```
**B10**
```
Ask your PBM: "If we wanted to leave, what in this contract makes it expensive or slow?" The termination and transition language is the leash. Read it before you need it.
```

Transparency is the word every PBM uses and the thing every PBM contract is built to prevent.

**C5**

Your PBM doesn't have a network. It has a funnel with a clinical-sounding name.

**C6**
```
"Pass-through" is the most expensive two words in your contract, because of the definition sitting three clauses away.

**C7**

A middleman that owns the pharmacy, sets the price, pays itself, and audits its own books isn't a service you buy. It's a toll road you're required to drive.

**C9**

## D · ORIGIN-ANCHORED FIELD NOTE (8:30 PM close — MAX 1 PER WEEK)

A first-person in-the-room proof atom + a confrontational decode. NOT an autobiography. The atom (a contract reviewed, a clause found, a number seen) is the unfakeable anchor; the decode is the payload. Rotate; never run two in a week.

**D1**
```
Reviewed a contract last week where "pass-through pricing" had its definition three clauses away, and the definition quietly undid the promise. Read your nouns. The PBM defined them, not you.
```
**D2**
```
Pulled a client's specialty claims this month and sorted by filling pharmacy. Most of them filled at one the PBM owns. No member chose that. The prior-auth rules chose it for them.
```
**D3**
```
Found a "free implementation" clause this week that turned out to be amortized into twelve months of admin fees. The plan paid for it in full. It just paid in installments under a friendlier label.
```
**D4**
```
Read an audit clause yesterday that let the plan audit "once per year, using the PBM's auditor, on the records the PBM provides." That's not an audit right. I see that exact language constantly.

**D5**
```
Checked a plan's generic effective rate month by month last week. The annual average hit the guarantee. Eight of twelve months missed it. The averaging clause is where the money hides.
```
**D6**
```
Opened a rebate report this week that proudly said "100% pass-through." A PBM-owned aggregator had already taken its cut upstream. 100% of what was left is still 100%. The math was honest. The noun wasn't.
```
**D7**
```
Saw a termination clause this month that required 180 days' notice, a transition fee, and PBM sign-off on the new vendor's file format. That's not an exit. It's a leash dressed as a process.
```
**D8**

## E · NEWSJACK TEMPLATES (7 PM interactive — fill the bracket when a viral consumer post lands)

Quote-post the PBM mechanism under a viral consumer medical-bill / drug-price post. Sympathize with the patient; name the PBM structure. **Ally-positive only.** These are templates, not pre-scheduled; deploy when a matching viral post appears (evening is when they peak). If none lands, fall back to a poll.

**E1 · "$X for [drug]" viral bill**
```
This isn't the manufacturer's price and it isn't greed at the counter. A middleman set what your plan was billed, kept the spread, and called the markup a service. The patient is the last person who got to see the number.
```
**E2 · surprise pharmacy bill / copay card denied**
```
What happened here has a name: a copay accumulator. The card paid, the PBM kept it, and none of it counted toward the deductible. The patient hits the same wall and pays again. It's written into a contract they never saw.
```
**E3 · "insulin shouldn't cost this much"**
```
It doesn't. A vial costs under $10 to make. The patient pays a copay on a list price inflated to fund rebates that flow to a middleman. The high price isn't an accident. It's the part of the system that pays someone.
```
**E4 · "my pharmacy closed / can't afford to fill"**
```
Independent pharmacies are closing because a PBM reimburses below cost, then claws part of it back weeks later (DIR). The same PBM often owns the mail-order pharmacy it's steering those patients to. The squeeze and the destination are the same company.
```
**E5 · generic costs pennies, billed dollars**
```
The pharmacy bought that generic for a few dollars. Your plan was billed many times that. The pharmacy didn't keep the difference. A middleman did, on a line your plan's report doesn't print.
```

## F · NEWS → CONTRACT BRIDGE TEMPLATES (7 PM interactive)

When a PBM story is in the headlines (the AM slot reacted to it), the 7 PM post decodes the contract clause the story is actually about. Fill the bracket with the week's live story; the decode half is reusable.

**F1 · spread-pricing suit/story**
```
Everyone's posting about the [spread-pricing case]. Here's the part that matters for your plan: the clause that makes it possible is the pricing definition, and the one line that would stop it is a true pass-through with audit access to both numbers. Most contracts have neither.
```
**F2 · PBM-ownership / vertical-integration story**
```
The [PBM-ownership story] is in the news. The contract version: look for whether your specialty and mail-order routing rules point to a pharmacy the PBM owns. If they do, the "network" is doing exactly what the headline describes, on your plan.
```
**F3 · rebate / formulary story**
```
The [rebate story] making the rounds shows up in your contract as the rebate definition and the formulary-exclusion clause. Ask which exclusions are clinical and which are rebate-driven. The headline is abstract. Your formulary is where it's concrete.
```
**F4 · FTC / regulatory action**
```
The [FTC action] names the practice. Your contract is where you find out if you're exposed to it. The regulator can describe the pattern; only your audit rights let you check your own plan for it. Read the audit clause this week, not after the rule lands.
```

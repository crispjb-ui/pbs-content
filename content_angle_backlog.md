## CA-01: Self-Funded Health System as Plan Sponsor

**Surfaced:** May 16, 2026 in Bryce Platt LinkedIn thread on hospital-owned specialty pharmacies. Ginny referenced UNC Health experience in the comment chain.

**Core insight:** The hospital-owned specialty pharmacy debate is usually framed as a system-level question (good or bad for healthcare). The plan sponsor angle almost nobody articulates: **who owns the plan determines whether the 340B savings flow back to the plan or stay with the hospital.**

- **Self-funded health system as plan sponsor** (UNC Health, Cleveland Clinic, Mayo, similar hospital-system-owned plans): the dispensing hospital and the plan share the same balance sheet. 340B savings on specialty fills flow back to the plan as reduced net cost. Integration genuinely benefits the plan.
- **Independent employer plan buying specialty from an unrelated health system:** the 340B acquisition discount stays on the hospital's balance sheet. Plan pays AWP-based or WAC-based reimbursement on a drug the hospital acquired at 340B. Hospital captures the spread.

**Best-fit formats:**
- Substack deep dive: "When the Plan Sponsor Owns the Pharmacy: 340B Savings, Channel Routing, and Who Captures What"
- Thursday Field Note: "Who Owns the Specialty Pharmacy Determines Who Captures the Savings"
- LinkedIn carousel (decoder): "Hospital-Owned Specialty Pharmacy: When It Helps Your Plan and When It Doesn't"

**Open questions to develop before drafting:**

- How 340B Drug Pricing Program eligibility intersects with the hospital's own self-funded plan vs. third-party employer plans (covered entity status, GPO prohibition, child-site rules).
- The PBM contract clause that determines whether channel routing is plan-controlled or PBM-controlled (specialty exclusivity, network design, step therapy bias).

## CA-02: GLP-1 Coverage Alternatives + the Three PBM Contract Clauses Underneath

**Surfaced:** May 16, 2026 in Bryce Platt LinkedIn thread on GLP-1 coverage alternatives. Bryce posted a "4 Alternatives to Traditional GLP-1 Coverage" decision matrix (HRA, EBHRA, Health FSA, Carve-Out) and listed PBM contract considerations as a one-line afterthought. PBS's lane is the layer underneath the matrix — which contract clauses determine whether the carve-out math actually works.

**Core insight:** Most coverage-model matrices in circulation (Bryce's matrix, Mercer / Aon advisory PDFs, broker decks) stop at the financial structure layer: who pays, how it integrates with ACA, what the administrative burden looks like. The clauses in the existing PBM contract are what determine whether a GLP-1 carve-out is cost-saving or cost-amplifying. PBS sees three structural clauses that decide the outcome:

1. **The exclusivity / network-flow clause.** Varies meaningfully across PBM contracts. Some require all prescriptions through the PBM network. Some specify all formulary drugs. Some already carve out specific therapeutic categories. Reading the actual clause determines whether a GLP-1 carve-out needs (a) a contract amendment, (b) a sole-source waiver, or (c) sits cleanly within existing language.

2. **The rebate-guarantee voiding clause.** Most PBM contracts include language that voids rebate guarantees across the entire formulary if the plan changes coverage outside the agreed structure. A GLP-1 carve-out that triggers this clause can cost the plan more in lost rebates on the rest of the formulary than it saves on the GLP-1 spend. This is the trap that turns a "high cost savings" carve-out on paper into a net negative in practice.

3. **The service-category exclusivity clause (the competing-program trap).** Distinct from drug-level exclusivity. Most PBM contracts now include right-of-first-refusal or service-category exclusivity language on programs the PBM also operates (CVS Weight Management Program, ESI EncircleRx, Optum specialty weight management). A carve-out that touches a service category the PBM operates can trigger this language even when the GLP-1 drug itself sits outside drug-level network requirements. The conflict isn't always at the drug level.

**Best-fit formats:**
- Substack deep dive: "Four Ways to Cover GLP-1s. Three Contract Clauses That Decide If Any of Them Work."
- Plan Sponsor Toolkit handout: "GLP-1 Carve-Out Contract Review Checklist" (paste-ready language audit for the three clauses above, decision tree for amendment vs. waiver vs. existing-language path)
- LinkedIn carousel (decoder): "Decoding the GLP-1 Carve-Out Contract" — five slides on the exclusivity clause, rebate-voiding language, service-category trap, amendment timing, and audit-rights interaction
- Thursday Field Note: "The Competing-Program Trap: Why Your GLP-1 Carve-Out May Already Be Blocked"

**Open questions to develop before drafting:**

- Status of compounded GLP-1 navigation post-shortage (FDA removed semaglutide from shortage list Feb 2025; tirzepatide later). Plans built carve-out strategies around compounded products in 2024-2025 are now reassessing. Worth a sidebar or its own section.
- Affordability-threshold sensitivity: the carve-out only delivers Bryce's "high cost savings" position if the plan secures direct manufacturer pricing (Eli Lilly Direct, Lilly Self Pay) or transparent network pricing (Costco, Cost Plus, Capital Rx). Without those, carve-out is administrative complexity without unit-cost savings. Math example worth building.

## CA-03: AFP (Alternative Funding Program) Contract Design

**Surfaced:** May 19, 2026 across two LinkedIn threads. (1) Heath Ingram (Partner, 2nd connection) asked on a Wendell Potter-adjacent post where there is "a place for AFPs" and how to control for safety risks like the HIV ART case study Ginny had referenced. (2) Chris Vanderwolk, Esq. (Benefits Compliance & Innovation Leader, 1st connection) began a multi-day legal series critiquing AFP vendor "FDA permits 90-day personal importation" sales positioning, citing FDA Regulatory Procedures Manual section 9-2-5 and the four-prong test for personal importation.

**Core insight:** Plan sponsors evaluating AFPs face three distinct evaluation layers that almost no public commentary stacks together:

1. **Legal compliance layer** (Chris Vanderwolk's lane): does the AFP vendor design actually satisfy the FDA personal-importation four-prong test, or is the "90-day supply" pitch dispositively failing at prong (a)? Most AFP vendor sales positioning fails the legal test before the plan ever signs.

2. **Clinical safety layer** (PBS's clinical pharmacy lane): even where prong (a) might theoretically clear for an orphan drug genuinely unavailable domestically, AFP vendor design rarely carves safety-sensitive medication classes out of the eligibility list. HIV ART, oncology, transplant immunosuppressants, anticoagulants, and narrow-therapeutic-index drugs all carry resistance and adverse-event risk profiles that don't tolerate supply chain variation regardless of legal positioning.

3. **Contract design layer** (PBS's contract lane): most AFP vendor agreements don't include drug-class carve-outs, member transparency requirements, pharmacist consultation gates, audit rights on supply chain documentation, or fiduciary documentation of the cost-vs-clinical-risk decision by class. The standard vendor contract is structured to maximize vendor flexibility, not plan sponsor oversight.

**Six controls plan sponsors should negotiate before signing an AFP vendor agreement:**

1. Contract-level drug class carve-outs from foreign-sourcing eligibility (HIV ART, oncology, transplant immunosuppressants, anticoagulants, anticonvulsants, narrow-therapeutic-index drugs). Manufacturer PAP coordination only for these classes.
2. Member transparency on supply chain source, with right to opt out and use standard benefit.
3. Pharmacist consultation gate before routing into foreign-sourcing AFP: clinical appropriateness and therapeutic equivalence counseling.
4. Audit rights on supply chain documentation, member consent records, and PAP success rates vs. foreign-sourcing rates.
5. Fiduciary documentation of the coverage decision rationale by drug class: cost savings weighed against clinical and member-impact assessment.
6. Annual carve-out list review at renewal as new drugs come to market in safety-sensitive categories.

## CA-04: PA Criteria Architecture Audit

**Surfaced:** May 19, 2026 across two Bryce Platt LinkedIn threads in the same evening. (1) PSG specialty drug benefits survey post on whether plans support relaxing PA requirements (Bryce: "Where do you think we are on the spectrum of too much vs not enough PAs?"). (2) Follow-up Bryce question after Ginny's reframe: "Who should set the criteria?"

**Core insight:** PA isn't one tool. Its value depends entirely on **who set the criteria and on which categories.** PA delivers genuine ROI on specialty, oncology, and biologics. It often loses money on mature generics and stable chronic refills, where administrative cost exceeds savings. But most plan sponsors don't control the criteria architecture; PBMs do, and the PBM's rebate-revenue incentives can bias criteria toward preferred brands over genuinely cost-effective alternatives.

**Structural principle:** Separation of criteria-setting authority and rebate revenue. The party whose income depends on which manufacturer rebates flow shouldn't have unilateral authority over which drugs get prescribed.

**Practical implementation** (the non-utopian version — revised May 19 after early draft was flagged as impractical):

Most plan sponsors will not, and should not, draft PA criteria from scratch. PBMs operate the P&T committees and have the clinical infrastructure. The realistic plan sponsor leverage points are:

1. Demand actual PA criteria in writing as a contract attachment, not summary descriptions.
2. Independent pharmacist review (one to two days of consultant time, not a full-time hire) of criteria in the highest-spend categories: specialty, oncology, biologics, GLP-1.
3. PBM disclosure when a preferred drug carries higher manufacturer rebates than the non-preferred alternative being denied.
4. Annual audit of actual PA decisions against the published criteria.

The structural principle still holds. The practical implementation is transparency, targeted independent review, and annual audit — not full criteria drafting from scratch.

## CA-05: Biosimilar Contract Architecture

**Surfaced:** May 19, 2026 in Bryce Platt LinkedIn thread on biosimilar lowest-net-cost strategy (PSG specialty drug benefits report data: 18% of employers vs. 56% of health plans using lowest-net-cost biosimilar strategy across both pharmacy and medical benefits).

**Core insight:** The 18% employer figure isn't an awareness gap. It's a contract architecture gap. Five specific structural pieces are missing for employers to move from Bryce's Level 2 (Basic Adoption) to Level 4 (Cross-Benefit Integration) or Level 5 (Lowest Net Cost Mastery):

1. **PBM compensation aligned with biosimilar utilization.** Most contracts are built around branded biologic rebate revenue (Humira reference, Stelara reference). Steering members to biosimilars reduces that revenue. Without performance terms tied to biosimilar adoption, PBMs aren't structurally motivated to drive the switch.

2. **Medical-benefit infrastructure.** Biosimilars on the medical side need white-bagging arrangements, site-of-care language, and network utilization management. Most self-funded plans don't have the contract scaffolding to manage this on the medical benefit, which is where Level 4 (Cross-Benefit Integration) lives.

3. **Coordinated copay strategy.** Member on reference Humira gets $0 via manufacturer copay card. Switch to biosimilar can cost $50+. Member sees the switch as cost-increasing even when the plan saves significantly. Without coordinated copay alignment, member resistance derails switches.

4. **Net cost visibility.** Most plan sponsors get utilization reports, not net cost comparisons after rebates, manufacturer programs, member cost-share differentials, and utilization data.

5. **Resourced switching protocols.** P&T criteria updates, prescriber outreach, member communication. None of this happens unless contractually triggered and budgeted.

**Synthesis (after Bryce challenge):** Bryce pushed back that "employer (or their consultant) education is the fix." Refined position: education is necessary but not sufficient. The education that actually moves the needle is naming the specific contract clauses to renegotiate at renewal so PBM compensation aligns with biosimilar utilization and the medical-benefit scaffolding supports the Level 4 work. Education + contract architecture together, not education alone.

**Best-fit formats:**
- Plan Sponsor Toolkit handout: "Biosimilar Lowest-Net-Cost Readiness: Five Contract Levers Employers Are Missing" (one to two pages, pairs with W22 Monday Biosimilar Readiness Assessment as the contract-side companion).

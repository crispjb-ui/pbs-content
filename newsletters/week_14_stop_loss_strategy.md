# PART 1: SUBSTACK ARTICLE

## Stop-Loss and Pharmacy: The Coverage Gap Nobody Discusses
**Subtitle:** How self-funded employers can evaluate pharmacy exposure in stop-loss coverage

**Publication:** Benefit Blind Spots
**Publish:** Monday, April 6, 2026 at 7:30 AM EST
**Length:** ~1,500 words (~6 min read)
**Tone:** Analytical authority
**Pillar:** Self-Funded Guidance

---

### Article Body

I reviewed a stop-loss policy last month where the employer discovered a gene therapy exclusion after a member was approved for a $1.9 million treatment. The plan covered the therapy. The stop-loss policy did not.

*Illustrative example for educational purposes. Actual amounts vary by plan.*

That gap, between what your plan covers and what your stop-loss actually protects, is more common than it should be. The specialty drug landscape evolved faster than stop-loss policy language, and most employers do not read their stop-loss contracts with pharmacy-specific scenarios in mind.

This is the Visibility Gap in stop-loss coverage. It is the kind of exposure that only surfaces when a claim tests the limits of your policy. And by then, your negotiating position is gone.

**The Pharmacy Exposure Problem**

Self-funded employers buy stop-loss coverage to protect against catastrophic claims. But pharmacy claims have become the catastrophic claims.

A single gene therapy can exceed $2 million. Multiple sclerosis treatments run $80,000 to $100,000 annually. Oncology regimens routinely blow past specific stop-loss attachment points. One or two high-cost pharmacy claimants can reshape your plan's financials for the year. That is exactly the scenario stop-loss coverage is designed to address.

The question is whether your stop-loss policy actually covers these claims as expected. In our contract review work at PBS, the answer is frequently: not as well as the employer assumed.

**Four Coverage Gaps We See Repeatedly**

We audit stop-loss policies alongside PBM contracts. Four gaps show up over and over.

**Gene therapy exclusions.** Some stop-loss policies exclude or limit coverage for gene and cell therapies entirely. Given the cost profile of these treatments (often $1 to $3 million per administration), an exclusion can leave an employer holding the full claim. If your policy is silent on gene therapy, it is not covered. Silence is exclusion.

**New drug limitations.** Policies may limit coverage for drugs approved after the policy effective date. A member diagnosed mid-year with a condition treated by a recently approved drug may generate claims that fall outside stop-loss protection. This creates timing risk that most employers never consider.

**Experimental treatment definitions.** Stop-loss policies typically exclude experimental or investigational treatments. But the carrier's definition of "experimental" may be narrower than the FDA's approval status. If your plan covers a treatment that your stop-loss carrier considers experimental under their criteria, you bear the cost difference. We have seen this disconnect create substantial exposure on a single claim.

**Pharmacy versus medical benefit routing.** The same specialty drug can be billed through the pharmacy benefit or the medical benefit depending on the site of care. Some stop-loss policies treat these differently, applying different reimbursement methodologies or coverage criteria depending on which benefit channel processes the claim.

**The Lasering Problem**

Lasering occurs when a stop-loss carrier identifies a high-risk member and either excludes them from coverage or raises the attachment point specifically for their claims. Pharmacy utilization is now one of the primary drivers of lasering decisions.

Here is what makes this particularly frustrating for employers: the employee who needs the most protection becomes the one who gets the least. A member on a high-cost specialty medication is the most likely to generate a catastrophic claim and the most likely to be lasered at renewal.

Most employers do not find out until the renewal quote arrives. By then, your options have narrowed considerably.

When a member is lasered, you have a short list of options: accept the laser and budget for the exposure, negotiate a higher premium for coverage inclusion, seek alternative carriers, or demonstrate pharmacy management that may reduce the claim trajectory. The best defense against lasering is proactive management. Carriers are more likely to laser members whose costs appear unmanaged. In our experience, demonstrating clinical programs, utilization management, and cost containment strategies can influence carrier decisions at renewal. It does not guarantee anything, but it gives carriers a reason to keep you in the pool.

**The Two-Contract Problem**

Stop-loss and PBM contracts do not always align, and they are almost never negotiated together.

The timing can differ. PBM contracts and stop-loss policies may have different effective dates. A drug covered under your PBM contract may not be covered under your stop-loss policy if the approval dates do not match. I have seen this catch employers mid-year when an FDA approval creates coverage under one contract but not the other.

The definitions can differ. What your PBM considers a covered medication may differ from what your stop-loss policy covers. Specialty drug definitions, experimental treatment criteria, and coverage limitations may not match between the two contracts.

The claims flow can differ. Pharmacy claims typically process faster than medical claims. Stop-loss reimbursement timing may create cash flow challenges for high-cost pharmacy claims that take months to reconcile. For smaller plans especially, that cash flow gap is real.

This is the Visibility Gap that spans two contracts. What your PBM contract covers and what your stop-loss policy covers may not align. The gap between those two documents is where the financial surprise lives.

**How to Review Your Stop-Loss Policy for Pharmacy Gaps**

Pull your current stop-loss policy (not just the summary) and review these five provisions.

*1. Gene Therapy Coverage*

Look for explicit inclusion or exclusion of gene and cell therapies. Red flag language: "Experimental or investigational treatments as determined by [carrier]" without referencing FDA approval status. Protective language: "Covered charges include FDA-approved gene and cell therapies consistent with the plan's benefit design." If your policy is silent on gene therapy, it is not covered.

*2. New Drug Limitations*

Look for how the policy treats drugs approved after the policy effective date. Red flag: "Drugs not included in the formulary as of [date]" or "Medications requiring prior authorization that was not obtained." Protective language: "Coverage extends to FDA-approved medications consistent with the plan's formulary, regardless of approval date."

*3. Experimental Treatment Definition*

Look for whether the carrier's definition aligns with your plan's definition. The carrier may define "experimental" using their own medical policy, which can be narrower than FDA approval. Place your plan document's definition next to the stop-loss policy's definition. Any gap is exposure.

*4. Lasering Provisions*

Look for the renewal terms regarding individual member exclusions. Key questions: What triggers a laser? What is the notification timeline? Can you negotiate a buy-up instead of exclusion? Protective language: "Carrier will provide 90-day advance notice of any individual member exclusions or attachment point modifications."

*5. Pharmacy vs. Medical Benefit Routing*

Look for whether the policy treats pharmacy benefit claims and medical benefit claims the same way. Some policies apply different reimbursement methodologies depending on which benefit channel processes the claim. The same drug can be billed through either channel depending on site of care.

This review takes approximately one hour and can identify coverage gaps before they become claim denials. The employers who do this before renewal negotiate from knowledge. Those who do not discover the gaps when a claim is denied.

**What We Advise**

Stop-loss and pharmacy strategy are connected decisions that most organizations treat separately. The PBM contract determines what drugs are covered and at what cost. The stop-loss policy determines what catastrophic claims are protected. When those two contracts are not coordinated, gaps emerge.

We advise employers to:
- Review stop-loss policy language for pharmacy-specific provisions annually
- Model high-cost pharmacy scenarios against coverage limits
- Coordinate PBM and stop-loss contract terms at renewal
- Demonstrate pharmacy management to reduce lasering risk

The specialty drug landscape has changed faster than stop-loss policy language has evolved. Employers who assume coverage without verification may find gaps when claims arrive.

Have you reviewed your stop-loss policy for pharmacy gaps? If you found something unexpected, leave a comment below. If this analysis is useful, share it with your broker or benefits team.

## Setting Attachment Points in a High-Cost Drug Era

**Publication:** Benefit Blind Spots
**Publish:** Thursday, April 9, 2026 at 7:30 AM EST
**Length:** ~550 words (~3 min read)
**Tone:** Practical, tactical
**Pillar:** Self-Funded Guidance
**Type:** Field Note

---

### Field Note Body

We have been reviewing stop-loss renewals this quarter and one pattern is clear: specific attachment points that were set three or four years ago no longer reflect current pharmacy risk.

The math has shifted. When an employer set a $150,000 specific attachment point in 2022, gene therapies exceeding $2 million were rare pipeline events. Specialty medications running $100,000 or more annually were concentrated in a handful of therapeutic categories. That is no longer the case.

**What has changed**

The number of FDA-approved therapies exceeding $100,000 per year has grown substantially. Gene and cell therapies with single-administration costs above $1 million are no longer theoretical. GLP-1 medications, while individually less expensive, create aggregate exposure that affects plan cost projections. The drug cost environment that informed your current attachment point may no longer exist.

This does not mean every employer needs a lower attachment point. It means the analysis supporting that number needs updating.

**How to assess your current position**

First, model your current high-cost claimants against your attachment point. How many members are within striking distance? What is the trajectory of their utilization? A member on a specialty medication trending upward may cross your attachment threshold mid-year.

*Illustrative example for educational purposes. Actual amounts vary by plan.* An employer with a $175,000 specific attachment point and three members on specialty medications running $120,000-$140,000 annually faces meaningful exposure. If any of those members requires a therapy change, dosage adjustment, or additional specialty medication, they cross the threshold. The question is whether stop-loss coverage responds as expected when they do.

Second, evaluate what your attachment point actually buys. A lower attachment point provides more protection but increases premium. A higher attachment point reduces premium but increases retained risk. The right answer depends on your plan size, risk tolerance, cash reserves, and pharmacy utilization profile.

Third, check for pharmacy-specific exclusions at your attachment level. Some stop-loss policies exclude gene therapies, limit new drug coverage, or apply different terms to pharmacy versus medical claims. Your attachment point only protects you if the claims that hit it are actually covered.

**The practical takeaway**

Attachment point decisions should be revisited annually with current pharmacy data, not rolled forward from prior years. The cost environment changes faster than most renewal conversations acknowledge.

Three questions for your next stop-loss conversation:

1. What is our current high-cost pharmacy claimant profile, and how many members are approaching the attachment point?
2. Are there pharmacy-specific exclusions or limitations that could leave us exposed even when claims exceed the threshold?
3. What is the premium impact of adjusting the attachment point by $25,000 in either direction?

The attachment point is a financial decision that deserves financial analysis, not a number carried forward by default.

### Newsletter Body

We reviewed a stop-loss policy last month where the employer discovered a gene therapy exclusion after a member was approved for treatment. The claim exceeded $2 million. The policy excluded it. The employer absorbed the full cost.

That is not an edge case anymore. It is the core problem: your stop-loss policy and your pharmacy benefit are managed as separate decisions, and the gap between them is where financial shock lives.

Specialty medications now routinely run $80,000 to $100,000 annually. Gene therapies exceed $2 million per treatment. These claims hit attachment points that were set before this generation of therapies existed. The stop-loss policy that protected you five years ago may not protect you today.

Three gaps come up consistently.

Gene therapy exclusions. Some policies exclude or cap gene therapy coverage entirely. Your plan approves the treatment. Your stop-loss does not cover the claim. You find out after the fact.

New drug limitations. Medications approved after your policy's effective date may not be covered until renewal. A mid-year FDA approval can create months of unprotected exposure.

Experimental treatment definitions. If your stop-loss defines "experimental" differently than your plan does, you have a mismatch that creates silent risk.

Then there is lasering. Your most expensive pharmacy claimant is the member your stop-loss carrier watches most closely. At renewal, that member may be excluded entirely or assigned an attachment point so high the coverage becomes meaningless. The employee who needs the most protection becomes the one who gets the least.

Most employers negotiate PBM contracts and stop-loss policies with different teams, at different times, using different data. That disconnect is the problem. When your pharmacy strategy and your stop-loss approach are coordinated, you can demonstrate active management to carriers and reduce lasering risk. When they are not, you discover the gap at the worst possible time.

When a member is lasered, your options are: accept the exposure, negotiate a premium increase for continued coverage, seek alternative carriers, or demonstrate active pharmacy management to reduce the risk profile. That last option only works if your PBM strategy and stop-loss approach are already coordinated.

Before your next renewal, verify five things: gene therapy and new drug coverage provisions, experimental treatment definitions, how your policy handles pharmacy versus medical benefit claims, your high-cost claimant trajectory, and whether your PBM contract terms align with your stop-loss terms.

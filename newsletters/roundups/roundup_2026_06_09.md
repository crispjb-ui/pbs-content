## Article Body

A quieter news week on the surface, but two of these stories will outlast the headlines. The FTC's insulin case against the two largest PBMs is about to go to trial, and the Supreme Court just handed plan sponsors a cost win most people read as a patent story. Here is what matters for your plan.

### The FTC's Insulin Case Against Caremark and OptumRx Heads to Trial June 17

*FTC, Buchanan Ingersoll, Fierce Healthcare | June 4, 2026*

Express Scripts settled with the FTC in February, agreeing to business changes by January 1, 2027 that the agency projects will cut patient out-of-pocket costs for drugs like insulin by billions over a decade. Caremark and OptumRx did not settle. Their case, over rebating practices the FTC says inflated insulin list prices through the PBMs' own group purchasing organizations, goes to an evidentiary hearing on June 17.

I am watching this because the GPO layer is exactly the structure most plan sponsors cannot see in their own reporting. We review hundreds of PBM contracts a year, and the rebate-aggregator entities at the center of this case sit between the manufacturer rebate and what your plan actually nets, usually under contract language that never names them. Whichever way the hearing goes, it will produce the most detailed public record yet of how that money moves. That is a transparency precedent worth tracking, not a Medicaid sideshow.

---

### Supreme Court Hands Generics a Unanimous Win on Skinny Labels

*STAT News, Supreme Court | June 4, 2026*

In Hikma v. Amarin, the Supreme Court ruled unanimously that a generic maker that properly carves a patented use off its label is shielded from induced-infringement claims. Justice Jackson wrote the opinion. The decision protects the "skinny label" pathway that lets generics launch for the unpatented uses of a drug without waiting out every patent on the brand.

Read it as a cost ruling, not a patent ruling. Skinny labels are one of the main reasons generics reach your formulary years before a brand's full patent thicket expires. Had this gone the other way, brand manufacturers would have gained a new tool to delay generic entry, and delayed generics show up in your claims data as continued brand spend. This one quietly protected the cheapest part of your plan.

---

### The Obesity Pipeline Crowded Up at the Diabetes Meeting

*STAT News | June 6-8, 2026*

At the American Diabetes Association meeting, AstraZeneca reported mid-stage promise for an oral GLP-1 pill, Lilly shared safety and tolerability data on its next-generation drug retatrutide, and Pfizer detailed a monthly obesity drug. Oral options and longer dosing intervals are moving from concept to data.

For plans with GLP-1 already in their top three by spend, more competition is not automatically relief. Pills remove the injection barrier, and a lower barrier means more eligible members fill. More entrants can pressure net price over time, but the near-term effect is usually higher utilization before any pricing benefit reaches you. If your plan covers obesity GLP-1s, this is the year to revisit step therapy and eligibility criteria with your broker, before the pill versions land on formulary.

---

### PCMA Releases a Policy Platform That Points at Pharma

*PCMA | June 9, 2026*

The PBM trade group released "Path to Patient Affordability," a six-part policy platform that frames drug prices as a Big Pharma patent problem and urges Congress to focus there. It follows last week's seven-figure ad campaign and a quiet rebrand toward "Pharmacy Benefit Companies."

The pattern is consistent. With February's federal delinking and rebate pass-through rules now law, the trade group is redirecting attention upstream to manufacturers. Patent abuse is real. So is spread, and so are the GPO structures in the case above. For a plan sponsor, none of this changes the only place value is measurable, which is your contract and your quarterly report.

---

### One More Worth Watching

A study presented this week found that staying on a GLP-1 for at least three years could prevent a meaningful number of knee replacements. Expect to hear the medical-offset argument more often in coverage debates. Keep the time horizon honest. The pharmacy spend is now, and the surgical savings, if they materialize, land years later and usually on a different line of your budget.

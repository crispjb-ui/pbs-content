## ▼ DRAFT BEGINS ▼

**Working title: The Auction Nobody Can See: What Specialty Drug Routing Actually Pays, and to Whom**

Imagine that every time a doctor wrote a prescription for an expensive drug, an invisible auction decided which pharmacy would fill it. The winning bidder is almost always the pharmacy owned by the company running the auction. The bids are never posted. The auction house's fee is built into prices nobody itemizes. That is specialty drug routing economics. Earlier chapters showed the auction exists; this one is about what it pays.

Two chapters back, this series looked at fees that arrive after the fact. Before that, it walked the steering practices, the prior authorization rules and white-bagging mandates that direct specialty prescriptions to particular doors. On the map, we are still standing on the fifth revenue stream, but this chapter goes below the routing to the margin itself, because the routing is only worth building if the destination pays.

Here is how the economics assemble, piece by piece, in the contracts I review. Start with the specialty drug list: the PBM writes it, amends it, and everything on it becomes subject to specialty rules, including routing. Then the exclusivity clause: specialty fills are restricted to the PBM's designated pharmacy, which, as this series established, the PBM's parent owns. Then the pricing architecture: the contract's tightest price protections, the ceiling schedules and guarantee mechanics, were built for retail generics and mostly do not reach specialty channels. And finally the reporting: the standard quarterly package summarizes specialty spending by category, not by channel margin, so the number that would reveal the gap is precisely the number the plan never routinely receives. The federal government's own second interim report on this industry documented what that architecture produces: enormous markups on specialty generic drugs dispensed through PBM-owned pharmacies. What I can add from the contract side is that nothing about those findings requires misconduct. The contract, read closely, permits every step.

Why is this the biggest pool left? Because specialty is where the dollars concentrated. A plan with a few thousand members may find that fewer than two percent of its claims drive half its drug spending, and nearly all of those claims flow through the routed channels this chapter describes. A modest per-fill margin on those claims outweighs aggressive pricing games on everything else in the plan combined. The industry understood this years before its customers did, which is why the pharmacies got bought and the routing got built.

The lever, and I want to be precise about this, is not banning integrated specialty pharmacy. Some of these pharmacies perform genuinely well clinically, and dismantling them by statute is neither likely nor obviously wise. The lever is making the routing economics visible and auditable, because a margin that must survive being seen behaves differently from one that lives in the dark. Two planks, consistent with everything this series has argued. First, per-fill disclosure: for any drug the PBM routes to an affiliated pharmacy, the plan sponsor receives the net cost of that fill and the net cost available in the alternative channels, at a cadence that permits comparison. Second, the audit right that reaches it: a federal floor, written into ERISA for the self-funded plans that state law cannot reach, giving the plan's own auditor access to specialty pricing and routing data without the restrictions the next-to-last chapter of this series will dissect.

My view is that specialty routing economics are where the next decade of PBM reform will actually move dollars. The rebate fights of the last decade were loud and produced the concealment architecture this series has walked. The specialty margin is quieter, larger per claim, and still mostly unexamined in the places where it could be examined cheaply, which is to say, in the contract and the claims data every plan sponsor already owns the rights to see, or should.

There is one more group of dollars flowing through those same routed pharmacies, and they were never the plan's or the PBM's to begin with. They belong, at least nominally, to patients: the manufacturer assistance funds this series touched in the accumulator chapter. What happens to that money inside the routing machinery is the next chapter.
